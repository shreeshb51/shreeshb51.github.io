Concepts from Project: Wasserstein Distance as a Loss Function for Image Morphing

  1. Zero Mass: A condition where a specific point or outcome within a distribution has a probability of exactly zero, meaning no “weight” is assigned to that location. It defines the regions where a probability measure is inactive or empty, which is critical for determining the boundaries of a distribution. Identifying these regions helps establish the support of a measure and dictates where transport plans cannot send or receive mass.

  2. Mass: The total amount of probability assigned to a specific point or region, representing the “weight” that must be accounted for during transport. In discrete settings, it refers to the value of the probability mass function at a given coordinate. It serves as the fundamental substance that is shifted or redistributed when comparing two different datasets or distributions.

  3. Support Points: The specific coordinates in a space where a probability measure is non-zero, representing the actual locations of data points. These points define the physical “footprint” of a distribution and serve as the source and destination indices for any transport calculation. Knowing the support is essential because optimal transport only concerns itself with moving mass between these defined locations.

  4. Cost Matrix: A structured array that stores the individual penalties or distances associated with moving mass from every source location to every destination location. Each entry \(C_{ij}\) quantifies the effort required to transport one unit of mass from point \(i\) to point \(j\). It acts as the ground metric that shapes the final transport plan and determines which movements are most efficient.

  5. Euclidean Distance: The straight-line length between two points in a multi-dimensional space, computed as the square root of the sum of squared differences between coordinates. It provides the most common “ground distance” for cost matrices in physical or geometric problems. This metric assumes a flat geometry and serves as the baseline for many higher-level optimal transport calculations.

  6. Wasserstein-1 Distance: A metric that calculates the minimum cost of transforming one distribution into another using the absolute distance between points as the cost function. It scales linearly with distance, meaning it treats moving a single large unit of mass the same as moving many smaller units if the total displacement is identical. Often called the Earth Mover’s Distance, it is robust to outliers and frequently used in image processing.

  7. Wasserstein-2 Distance: A transport metric that utilizes the square of the Euclidean distance as the cost for moving mass between points. By squaring the distance, it disproportionately penalizes long-range movements, which often results in unique, “smoother” transport plans that favor local shifts. It is the standard choice in many geometric deep learning applications because of its deep connections to Riemannian geometry and fluid dynamics.

  8. Earth Mover’s Distance: An alternative name for the Wasserstein-1 distance that emphasizes the physical intuition of moving piles of “dirt” to fill “holes” with minimum work. It measures the total mass moved multiplied by the distance traveled, providing a meaningful divergence even when distributions do not overlap. This property makes it superior to traditional information-theoretic measures like KL-divergence for comparing disjoint datasets.

  9. \(W_2\) Transport Plan: The specific mapping or “coupling” that achieves the minimum cost under a squared Euclidean penalty. Because it heavily discourages long-distance jumps, the resulting plan tends to preserve the local structure of the data more effectively than other metrics. This makes it a powerful tool for interpolating between shapes or distributions in a way that feels physically natural.

  10. Normalization: The process of scaling a vector of non-negative values so that they sum exactly to one, effectively turning a set of raw weights into a valid probability distribution. This ensures that the total “mass” is conserved and consistent across different datasets regardless of their original scale. It is a mandatory preprocessing step in optimal transport to ensure that the total supply at the source matches the total demand at the destination.

  11. Optimal Transport Plan: The specific mapping that defines exactly how much mass should be moved from each source location to each destination to achieve the minimum total cost. It is represented as a joint probability distribution where the entry at row \(i\) and column \(j\) indicates the amount of mass transferred between those two points. Finding this plan is the central objective of the primal transport problem, providing a clear visualization of how one distribution geometrically transforms into another.

  12. Marginal Constraints: The requirement that the sum of all mass exiting a source point must equal its original weight, and the sum of all mass arriving at a destination must match its target weight. In the transport matrix, these constraints ensure that row sums and column sums align perfectly with the source and target distributions, respectively. They act as the conservation laws of the system, guaranteeing that no mass is created or destroyed during the redistribution process.

  13. Regularized Transport Plan: A modified version of the transport map that incorporates an entropy penalty to “smooth” the resulting distribution of mass. This adjustment prevents the plan from becoming too sparse and allows for much faster computation using iterative algorithms like Sinkhorn’s. While it introduces a small amount of diffusion or “blur,” it makes the optimization problem strictly convex and numerically stable.

  14. Kernel: A matrix where each entry \(K_{ij}\) represents the exponential of the negative cost scaled by a smoothing parameter, quantifying the “affinity” between two points. It acts as the core operator in regularized transport, effectively filtering the source distribution to see how easily its mass can reach different destination points. This transformation turns the constrained optimization problem into a series of simple matrix-vector multiplications.

  15. Numerical Underflow: A computational failure that occurs when a value becomes so small that the hardware represents it as zero, losing all meaningful data. In optimal transport, this often happens when calculating the exponential of large negative costs during the kernel construction. Preventing underflow is the primary motivation for shifting calculations into the log-domain to maintain the integrity of the potentials.

  16. Log-Domain: A computational approach where operations are performed on the logarithms of values rather than the values themselves to preserve precision across many orders of magnitude. By turning multiplications into additions and divisions into subtractions, this method avoids the precision loss associated with extremely small or large numbers. It is the standard environment for implementing stable Sinkhorn iterations in production-level code.

  17. Epsilon (\(\epsilon\)): A smoothing parameter, often called “temperature,” that controls the trade-off between the accuracy of the transport distance and the speed of the computation. A high \(\epsilon\) produces a very blurry, entropic plan that is easy to solve, while a low \(\epsilon\) approaches the exact, sharp transport solution but becomes numerically difficult to handle. It serves as the primary dial for balancing physical realism with algorithmic performance.

  18. Floating Point: A standardized system for representing real numbers in digital hardware using a significand and an exponent to cover a vast range of values. Because it has a finite number of bits, it can only approximate most real numbers, leading to tiny errors that can accumulate during complex iterations. Understanding these limitations is vital for implementing the stable mathematical operators required for high-dimensional transport problems.

  19. Log-Sum-Exp (LSE): A specialized mathematical function designed to compute the logarithm of a sum of exponentiated values without ever explicitly calculating the large exponentials. It maintains numerical stability by factoring out the largest value in the set before performing the summation. This operator is the fundamental building block for performing stable Sinkhorn updates within the log-domain.

  20. Dual Potentials: The variables \((f, g)\) used in the dual formulation of optimal transport that represent the relative “price” or “pressure” at each source and destination point. Rather than describing the movement of mass, they describe the geometry of the cost landscape and how it supports the optimal plan. In the Sinkhorn algorithm, these potentials are iteratively updated until the marginal constraints are satisfied.

  21. Strong Duality: A mathematical property ensuring that the minimum cost of moving mass in the primal problem is exactly equal to the maximum “profit” or potential difference in the dual problem. When this condition holds, there is no duality gap, meaning the two seemingly different perspectives provide the same optimal value. It provides a powerful verification tool, as one can check the accuracy of a transport solution by comparing the primal and dual objectives.

  22. Kantorovich Potentials: The pair of functions that maximize the dual objective, effectively assigning a value to each point such that their difference never exceeds the transport cost between them. They represent the “shadow prices” of the marginal constraints, indicating how much the total cost would change if mass was added to a specific location. These potentials are the primary output when solving the dual problem and are used to reconstruct the displacement interpolation.

  23. Dual Problem: A reformulation of the optimal transport task that shifts the focus from finding a transport plan to finding a pair of potential functions that maximize a specific objective. This perspective is often easier to solve because it involves unconstrained optimization over vectors rather than a large matrix. It provides the mathematical foundation for understanding the “pressure” or “gradient” that drives mass from one distribution to another.

  24. Primal Problem: The direct formulation of optimal transport that seeks the specific matrix of mass movements that minimizes the total weighted distance. It deals with the concrete physical constraints of the system, such as ensuring all mass is moved and no capacities are exceeded. While intuitive, it is often more computationally expensive to solve directly than its dual counterpart in high-dimensional settings.

  25. Linear Interpolation: A method of blending two distributions by taking a weighted average of their intensities at every fixed coordinate. It does not account for the underlying geometry or move mass between locations, instead causing a “fading” effect where the source disappears as the target appears. It serves as the simplest baseline for transitions but fails to preserve the structural integrity of the objects being morphed.

  26. Displacement Interpolation: A geometric method of creating intermediate distributions by physically shifting mass along the optimal paths defined by a transport plan. It follows the shortest path, or geodesic, in Wasserstein space, ensuring that the characteristics of the source distribution are preserved as they travel to the target. Often called McCann interpolation, it is the standard technique for creating fluid, motion-based animations and shape transitions.

  27. Push-Forward: A mathematical operation that describes how a probability distribution is reshaped when its underlying points are moved by a specific function or mapping. It calculates the new distribution by tracking where every unit of mass ends up after the transformation is applied. This is the fundamental mechanism used to define how mass actually travels during a transport-based interpolation.

  28. Interpolation: The process of constructing new data points or distributions that represent a smooth transition between two known endpoints. In the context of optimal transport, it involves finding a sequence of intermediate states that represent a “middle ground” between a source and a target. It allows researchers to explore the space between data categories, such as finding the average of two distinct facial expressions or medical images.

  29. Transport Plan (\(\pi\)): A joint distribution matrix that serves as a detailed matching guide, specifying the exact quantity of mass to be transferred from every source point to every destination point. Each entry \(\pi_{ij}\) acts as a coupling that links a location in the first distribution to a location in the second. It is the primary output of the primal optimization problem and provides the routing instructions for displacement interpolation.

  30. Convex Combination: A specific type of weighted average where the coefficients are non-negative and sum exactly to one. It ensures that the resulting intermediate state remains within the bounds of the original inputs and retains the properties of a valid probability distribution. This mathematical structure is used to define the progress of an interpolation at any given time step \(t\) between zero and one.

  31. Mass Conservation: The fundamental physical constraint requiring that the total probability weight remains constant throughout the entire transport process. It ensures that every unit of mass leaving the source is accounted for at the destination, preventing any “leakage” or spontaneous creation of weight. This principle is strictly enforced by the marginal constraints and is essential for maintaining the validity of the distributions during morphing.

  32. Morphing: The visual process of transforming one shape or image into another by combining changes in both position and intensity. Unlike simple blending, high-quality morphing in optimal transport involves “sliding” the mass through space to create a continuous and physically plausible motion. It is the practical application of displacement interpolation, used extensively in computer graphics and medical imaging to visualize deformation.
