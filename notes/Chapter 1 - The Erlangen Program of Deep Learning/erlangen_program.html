    Chapter 1 - The Erlangen Program of Deep Learning: Symmetry as Architecture

    Introduction

    Every major advance in the history of neural network architecture has, in retrospect, been a discovery about symmetry.

    The convolutional neural network — the architecture that made deep learning practical for computer vision — is not merely a clever engineering trick. It is the neural network that respects the translation symmetry of images. The insight of LeCun and colleagues in the late 1980s was not just that sharing weights reduces parameters. It was that the meaning of a visual feature — an edge, a corner, a texture — does not depend on where in the image it appears. A horizontal edge in the top-left corner of an image is the same kind of thing as a horizontal edge in the bottom-right corner. The translation-equivariant convolution operation builds this fact into the architecture at the mathematical level, so the network never needs to learn it from data. Every convolutional network ever trained is implicitly an application of group theory — the group of discrete translations acting on the pixel grid.

    The transformer architecture — the architecture that dominates natural language processing, protein structure prediction, and increasingly computer vision — is the neural network that respects the permutation symmetry of sets. A sentence is a sequence, but the self-attention mechanism treats it as a set of tokens with positional encodings added afterward. The attention operation is equivariant to permutations of the token sequence — if you permute the tokens, the attention outputs permute in the same way. The transformer is a set-equivariant architecture with positional information injected as a feature, not as a structural constraint.

    These are not coincidences. They are instances of a single principle that Felix Klein articulated for geometry in 1872, in a document called the Erlangen Program, and that Bronstein, Bruna, LeCun, Szlam, and Vandergheynst articulated for deep learning in 2021, in a paper called Geometric Deep Learning: Grids, Groups, Graphs, Geodesics, and Gauges. The principle is: the right way to understand a geometric space is through its symmetry group — the group of transformations that leave the relevant structure unchanged. Klein unified Euclidean geometry, affine geometry, projective geometry, and hyperbolic geometry as special cases of a single framework by identifying each geometry with its symmetry group. The Geometric Deep Learning blueprint unifies CNNs, transformers, GNNs, and equivariant networks as special cases of a single framework by identifying each architecture with the symmetry group of its data domain.

    Chapter 1 develops this framework from the ground up, building the precise mathematical language that makes the subsequent chapters rigorous.

    The chapter has two topics corresponding to the two bullet points of the roadmap’s syllabus.

    The first topic is Invariance versus Equivariance — the two fundamental ways a neural network can respect a symmetry. A function is invariant to a group action if applying the group transformation to the input does not change the output: \(f(g\cdot x) = f(x)\) for all group elements \(g\). A function is equivariant to a group action if applying the group transformation to the input transforms the output in a corresponding way: \(f(g\cdot x) = g\cdot f(x)\) for all \(g\). Invariance is appropriate for scalar outputs — the energy of a molecule does not change when you rotate it. Equivariance is appropriate for structured outputs — the force vectors on the atoms of a molecule rotate when you rotate the molecule, in exactly the same way. The distinction between invariance and equivariance is not merely terminological. It determines what kind of information a neural network layer can and cannot preserve, and therefore what tasks it can and cannot perform. An invariant layer destroys information about the orientation of its input. An equivariant layer preserves it. Building a neural network requires deciding, layer by layer, which symmetry-respecting operation is appropriate for each layer’s role in the computation.

    The second topic is Groups — the precise mathematical objects that encode symmetry. A group is a set \(G\) with a composition operation satisfying four axioms: closure, associativity, identity, and inverses. The two groups of central importance in Phase 2 are the permutation group \(S_n\) — the group of all bijections from \(\{1,\ldots,n\}\) to itself, encoding the symmetry of graphs and sets — and the rotation group \(SO(3)\) — the group of all orientation-preserving orthogonal transformations of \(\mathbb{R}^3\), encoding the symmetry of three-dimensional geometric objects. These two groups appear in completely different physical contexts — \(S_n\) is a finite discrete group of combinatorial symmetries, while \(SO(3)\) is an infinite continuous Lie group of geometric symmetries — but both are handled by the same mathematical framework of group actions, representations, and equivariant maps. Understanding these two groups deeply is the prerequisite for every subsequent chapter in Phase 2.

    By the end of this chapter, symmetry will not feel like an optional add-on to neural network design — a regularization trick or an engineering heuristic. It will feel like the primary design principle, the constraint that determines architecture before a single weight is trained. The Erlangen Program of deep learning is the claim that every good neural network architecture is secretly a statement about group theory, and that making the group theory explicit produces better, more data-efficient, more interpretable architectures. The convolutional network was a good architecture because someone implicitly understood translation symmetry. The equivariant graph neural network is a better architecture because we now understand permutation and rotation symmetry explicitly, mathematically, and completely.
