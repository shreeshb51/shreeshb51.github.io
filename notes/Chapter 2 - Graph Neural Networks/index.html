Chapter 2 - Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)

Introduction

Chapter 1 established the theoretical foundation. You now have the precise mathematical language of symmetry: you know what a group is, what it means for a function to be invariant or equivariant with respect to a group action, and why the permutation group \(S_n\) and the rotation group \(SO(3)\) are the two central symmetry groups of structured data in machine learning. You know that Schur’s lemma classifies equivariant linear maps as block-diagonal in the irreducible representation basis, and that this classification — applied to the permutation representation of \(S_n\) — produces the unique two-parameter family \(L = \alpha I + \beta \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top\) as the most general \(S_n\)-equivariant linear layer on scalar node features. The architecture of graph neural networks is not an engineering heuristic. It is the inevitable consequence of demanding \(S_n\)-equivariance.

Chapter 2 makes this consequence explicit and complete. It builds the full Graph Neural Network framework — from the most basic aggregation operation to the theoretical ceiling of what any message-passing network can and cannot compute — across three topics that correspond exactly to the three bullet points of the roadmap’s syllabus.

The problem that Graph Neural Networks solve is the following. You have data whose natural representation is a graph: a set of nodes \(V = \{1, \ldots, n\}\), a set of edges \(E \subseteq V \times V\), node features \(x_i \in \mathbb{R}^d\) for each node \(i\), and optionally edge features \(e_{ij} \in \mathbb{R}^k\) for each edge \((i,j)\). You want to learn a function that maps this graph to a prediction — either a prediction for each node (node classification, node regression), a prediction for each edge (link prediction), or a single prediction for the entire graph (graph classification, graph regression). This function must be \(S_n\)-equivariant at the node level and \(S_n\)-invariant at the graph level: it must not depend on the arbitrary choice of node numbering.

The challenge is that standard neural network operations — matrix multiplications, convolutions — are defined for fixed-size vectors and grids. A graph has variable size: different graphs have different numbers of nodes and edges, and the edges define an irregular connectivity structure that is not a grid. You cannot simply flatten the adjacency matrix into a vector and apply a multilayer perceptron, because that operation is neither \(S_n\)-equivariant nor computationally sensible for large graphs.

Graph Neural Networks resolve this challenge by replacing the fixed-grid convolution with a graph-structured aggregation operation that is provably \(S_n\)-equivariant by construction. The central operation is message passing: each node collects information from its immediate neighbors, transforms that information, and updates its own feature vector. After \(k\) rounds of message passing, each node’s feature vector encodes information about its \(k\)-hop neighborhood — the subgraph reachable within \(k\) steps. Stacking \(k\) message-passing layers is the graph analogue of stacking \(k\) convolutional layers in a CNN: each layer expands the receptive field by one hop, just as each convolutional layer expands the receptive field by one stride.

Chapter 2 develops this program across three topics.

The first topic is Spatial GNNs: the Message Passing Framework. This is the dominant paradigm for graph neural networks in practice — the framework that underlies every major GNN architecture from GraphSAGE to Graph Attention Networks to the molecular networks used in drug discovery. The message passing framework decomposes each GNN layer into three operations: a message function that computes a vector for each edge based on the features of the two endpoint nodes (and optionally the edge feature), an aggregation function that combines all incoming messages at each node into a single vector (typically by summing or averaging), and an update function that computes the new node feature from the aggregated message and the old node feature. The framework is spatial in the sense that the computation is defined directly on the graph topology — edges connect nodes that are geometrically or semantically related, and the message passing propagates information along these connections. The spatial GNN framework is general enough to express virtually every practically successful GNN architecture as a special case, making it the universal language of graph learning.

The second topic is Spectral GNNs: Convolution via the Graph Laplacian. This is the mathematically deeper approach to graph convolution, rooted in spectral graph theory and the analogy between the graph Laplacian and the continuous Laplace-Beltrami operator of differential geometry. The key insight is that the Fourier transform on a grid is defined via the eigenfunctions of the Laplacian \(\Delta = -\nabla^2\), and that the graph Laplacian \(L = D - A\) (degree matrix minus adjacency matrix) is the discrete analogue of the continuous Laplacian. Just as the Fourier transform decomposes a signal into frequency components (eigenfunctions of \(\Delta\)), the graph Fourier transform decomposes a node signal into graph frequency components (eigenvectors of \(L\)). Convolution on the graph is then defined as pointwise multiplication in the graph Fourier domain — exactly as convolution on the grid is pointwise multiplication in the Fourier domain. The ChebNet architecture makes this spectral convolution computationally tractable by approximating the spectral filter with a Chebyshev polynomial of the graph Laplacian, which localizes the computation to \(k\)-hop neighborhoods and avoids the expensive eigendecomposition of the full Laplacian. The spectral approach provides the rigorous mathematical foundation that connects graph convolution to differential geometry and, ultimately, to the manifold learning of Chapter 3.

The third topic is The Weisfeiler-Lehman Isomorphism Test: the Theoretical Limits of GNN Expressive Power. This is the theoretical ceiling that characterizes exactly what any message-passing GNN can and cannot compute. The Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) test is a classical algorithm from combinatorics for testing graph isomorphism — determining whether two graphs are structurally identical up to node relabeling. The test works by iteratively assigning color labels to nodes based on the multisets of colors of their neighbors, and checking whether the resulting color histograms distinguish the two graphs. The fundamental theorem connecting GNNs to the WL test — proved by Xu, Hu, Leskovec, and Jegelka in 2019 — states that any message-passing GNN is at most as powerful as the WL test: two graphs that the WL test cannot distinguish will receive the same embedding from any message-passing GNN, regardless of the choice of message, aggregation, and update functions. This theorem has a constructive companion: a GNN that uses injective aggregation (specifically, summing over neighbor features rather than averaging) achieves exactly the expressive power of the WL test — it is the most powerful message-passing GNN possible within the standard framework. Understanding this theoretical boundary is essential for knowing when message-passing GNNs will work and when you need higher-order architectures — the \(k\)-order equivariant networks with Bell number \(B_{2k}\) parameters from Chapter 1.

The three topics of Chapter 2 form a complete intellectual arc. The spatial framework gives you the practical tool: a flexible, scalable, and easily implemented family of architectures for graph learning. The spectral framework gives you the mathematical foundation: the connection to Fourier analysis, the graph Laplacian, and the differential geometry that bridges Chapter 2 to Chapter 3. The Weisfeiler-Lehman analysis gives you the theoretical boundary: a precise characterization of what message-passing computation can and cannot achieve, grounding the practical architectural choices in rigorous theory.

By the end of Chapter 2, you will be able to derive the message passing framework from first principles as the unique \(S_n\)-equivariant architecture consistent with local graph topology, analyze spectral graph convolution as Fourier-domain filtering on the graph Laplacian eigenspace, and prove the equivalence between GNN expressive power and the Weisfeiler-Lehman isomorphism test. These three capabilities together give you the complete theoretical and practical command of graph neural networks that the checkpoint project — building an \(E(3)\)-equivariant GNN from scratch and proving its equivariance — demands.
