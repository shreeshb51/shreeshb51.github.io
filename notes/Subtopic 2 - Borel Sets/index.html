Subtopic 2 - Borel Sets

Layer 1 (Story)

You are a cartographer hired to produce the definitive map of Kathmandu valley. Your predecessor left you an enormous archive of hand-drawn sketches — thousands of them, each one marking a specific region: this neighborhood, that river bend, this stretch of road between two landmarks. No single sketch covers the whole valley. But together, if you combine them correctly, they ought to produce a complete and consistent picture of the entire terrain.

Your task is not to draw new regions from scratch. Your task is to take the simplest, most natural regions your predecessor drew — the basic building blocks — and construct every other region you will ever need by combining them using a fixed set of operations. You can take the complement of a region. You can take the union of countably many regions. And because you are working within a \(\sigma\)-algebra, those two operations together generate everything else you need.

The question is: what are the simplest, most natural building blocks to start with?

For a cartographer mapping physical terrain, the natural building blocks are the open patches — regions where every point has some breathing room, some small bubble of space entirely within the patch. These are the open sets from Subtopic 1 of Topic 1. They are the regions your intuition reaches for first, because they are the ones that arise naturally from the topology of the space — from the metric, from the notion of nearness.

On the real line, the simplest open sets are the open intervals — regions like \((a, b)\), the set of all real numbers strictly between \(a\) and \(b\). Every open subset of \(\mathbb{R}\) can be built from these. They are the atoms of the topological structure of the real line, the most basic patches your predecessor’s archive contains.

Now your predecessor hands you a specific instruction: start with all the open intervals of \(\mathbb{R}\), and generate the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra that contains all of them. Close the collection under complementation. Close it under countable unions. Close it under countable intersections — which comes for free, as you learned in the previous subtopic. Keep going until the collection cannot grow any further while remaining a \(\sigma\)-algebra.

The result of this process is the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra on \(\mathbb{R}\), and its members are called Borel sets.

Let us trace through what gets generated, step by step, to see how rich this collection becomes.

You begin with all open intervals \((a, b)\). These are your seeds.

Taking complements of open intervals gives you closed intervals \([a, b]\) and half-lines like \((-\infty, a]\) and \([b, \infty)\). These are now in your collection.

Taking countable unions of open intervals gives you arbitrary open sets — any open subset of \(\mathbb{R}\) can be written as a countable union of open intervals, a non-trivial but provable fact. So all open sets are in your collection.

Taking complements of open sets gives you all closed sets. So all closed sets are in your collection.

Taking countable intersections of open sets gives you a new class of sets called \(G_\delta\) sets — countable intersections of open sets. Taking countable unions of closed sets gives you \(F_\sigma\) sets. Taking countable intersections of \(F_\sigma\) sets gives you \(F_{\sigma\delta}\) sets. And so on, through an infinite hierarchy of increasingly complex combinations.

By the time you have exhausted all possible countable combinations of open and closed sets — all complements, all countable unions, all countable intersections, applied repeatedly in every conceivable order — you have the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra. It is a vast and enormously rich collection. It contains every interval, every open set, every closed set, every countable set, every set you will ever encounter in ordinary mathematical practice.

Here is what makes the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra the canonical, natural choice for working on the real line. Every other reasonable \(\sigma\)-algebra on \(\mathbb{R}\) either contains the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra or is contained within it. The Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is the one generated by the topology — by the open sets — and since the topology is the most fundamental structure on the real line, the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is the most natural companion to it. It is the cartographer’s definitive map: built from the simplest natural patches, closed under every operation probability needs, and just large enough to contain everything useful without admitting the pathological sets that would cause contradictions.

Now here is the break moment. The naive assumption is that the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra contains all subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) — that generating from open intervals and closing under countable operations eventually captures everything. After all, the process seems exhaustive. What could possibly be left out?

What is left out is subtle but real. The Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is generated by a countable process — countable unions, countable intersections, complements — starting from a countable collection of generating intervals. Because the operations are always countable, the resulting collection, while enormous, is in a precise sense smaller than the full power set \(2^\mathbb{R}\). There exist subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) that are not Borel sets. The Vitali set from the previous subtopic is one. But there are others — called analytic sets, or projective sets — that arise naturally in descriptive set theory and are not Borel.

More importantly for the roadmap: there exist subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) that are Lebesgue measurable but not Borel measurable. The Lebesgue \(\sigma\)-algebra — which we will meet in the next subtopic — is strictly larger than the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra, because it includes all subsets of Lebesgue null sets, even non-Borel ones. The Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is not the largest possible well-behaved \(\sigma\)-algebra on \(\mathbb{R}\). It is the most natural one, the one generated by the topology. These are related but distinct virtues.

The break destroys the assumption that “generated from simple things” means “contains everything reasonable.” Countable operations, no matter how many times applied, cannot reach every subset of an uncountable space. The Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is vast but not all-encompassing, canonical but not complete in the sense of containing all measurable sets.

Here is the picture to hold. Imagine your cartographic archive arranged in layers. The ground floor contains the open intervals — the simplest sketches, the raw building blocks. The first floor contains everything built from countable combinations of ground-floor sketches. The second floor contains everything built from countable combinations of first-floor sketches. And so on, upward through infinitely many floors. The entire building — every floor, every room — is the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra. It is immensely tall and immensely rich. But the full power set \(2^\mathbb{R}\) is not a building with floors. It is the uncountable wilderness outside, containing rooms that no countable construction process can ever reach.

In ordinary mathematical practice — in integration, in probability, in the study of distributions — you will almost never leave the building. Every function you encounter, every set you work with, every event you assign a probability to, will be a Borel set. The wilderness outside exists, and it matters theoretically, but the building is where the mathematics of this roadmap lives.

Layer 2 (Mathematics)

We build the formalism in four stages: the precise definition of the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra via generation, the hierarchy of Borel sets that the generation process produces, the extension to general topological spaces, and the critical concept of a Borel measurable function, which is the compatibility condition that ties everything together.

The Borel σ-algebra on ℝ

Let \(\mathcal{T}\) denote the collection of all open subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) — the standard topology on the real line, which you can think of as all sets that can be written as countable unions of open intervals. The Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra on \(\mathbb{R}\), written \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\), is the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by \(\mathcal{T}\):

\[\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) = \sigma(\mathcal{T})\]

Unpacking this using the generated \(\sigma\)-algebra definition from the previous subtopic:

\[\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) = \bigcap \{ \mathcal{F} : \mathcal{F} \text{ is a } \sigma\text{-algebra on } \mathbb{R} \text{ and } \mathcal{T} \subseteq \mathcal{F} \}\]

It is the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra containing all open sets of \(\mathbb{R}\). Members of \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) are called Borel sets, and the measurable space \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))\) is the canonical setting for real-valued probability theory.

An equivalent and more concrete generating set is the collection of all open intervals:

\[\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) = \sigma\big( \{(a, b) : a < b, \; a, b \in \mathbb{R}\} \big)\]

This equivalence holds because every open set in \(\mathbb{R}\) is a countable union of open intervals — a standard result from real analysis that you encountered implicitly in MATH 213. Since \(\sigma\)-algebras are closed under countable unions, generating from open intervals and generating from all open sets produces the same result.

There are in fact several equivalent generating collections, all producing the same \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\):

\[\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) = \sigma\big(\{(a,b) : a < b\}\big) = \sigma\big(\{[a,b] : a \leq b\}\big) = \sigma\big(\{(-\infty, b] : b \in \mathbb{R}\}\big)\]

The last generating collection — the half-lines \((-\infty, b]\) — is particularly useful in probability theory because the cumulative distribution function \(F(x) = P(X \leq x)\) is precisely the probability measure applied to sets of this form. The CDF you studied in MATH 208 is secretly a function on the generating collection of \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\).

The Borel Hierarchy

The generation process produces sets in layers, organized by the number and type of operations required to build them from open sets. This hierarchy has classical notation inherited from descriptive set theory, and while you do not need to memorize it in full, understanding its first few levels clarifies exactly how rich \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) is.

The \(G_\delta\) sets are countable intersections of open sets:

\[G_\delta = \left\{ \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} U_n : U_n \text{ open for all } n \right\}\]

Every closed set is a \(G_\delta\) set — the closed interval \([a,b]\) can be written as \(\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty (a - \frac{1}{n}, b + \frac{1}{n})\), a countable intersection of open intervals. The set of irrationals \(\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}\) is also a \(G_\delta\) set, being the complement of the rationals which are an \(F_\sigma\) set.

The \(F_\sigma\) sets are countable unions of closed sets:

\[F_\sigma = \left\{ \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} F_n : F_n \text{ closed for all } n \right\}\]

Every open set is an \(F_\sigma\) set — the open interval \((a,b)\) can be written as \(\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty [a + \frac{1}{n}, b - \frac{1}{n}]\) for large enough \(n\). The rationals \(\mathbb{Q}\) are an \(F_\sigma\) set, being a countable union of singleton closed sets \(\{q\}\).

The hierarchy continues: \(G_{\delta\sigma}\) sets are countable unions of \(G_\delta\) sets, \(F_{\sigma\delta}\) sets are countable intersections of \(F_\sigma\) sets, and so on through infinitely many levels. Every level is strictly larger than the previous one — there exist \(G_\delta\) sets that are not \(F_\sigma\), and vice versa. The full Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is the union of all these levels, an infinite tower built entirely from countable operations applied to open sets.

The Borel σ-algebra on ℝⁿ and General Spaces

The construction generalizes immediately. For \(\mathbb{R}^n\), the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n)\) is generated by the open sets of \(\mathbb{R}^n\), or equivalently by the open rectangular boxes:

\[\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n) = \sigma\big(\{(a_1,b_1) \times \cdots \times (a_n, b_n) : a_i < b_i\}\big)\]

A crucial fact connecting product spaces to individual coordinates is that:

\[\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n) = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) \otimes \cdots \otimes \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\]

where \(\otimes\) denotes the product \(\sigma\)-algebra — the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by all sets of the form \(A_1 \times \cdots \times A_n\) with each \(A_i \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\). This means the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is completely determined by the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebras on each coordinate, which makes it compatible with the coordinate-wise analysis you performed throughout MATH 201.

For a general topological space \((X, \mathcal{T})\) — any space equipped with a collection of open sets satisfying the axioms of a topology — the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{B}(X)\) is defined identically as \(\sigma(\mathcal{T})\). This means the construction works for metric spaces, for manifolds, for function spaces, and for spaces of probability distributions — wherever a topology exists, the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra follows automatically. This is why Borel sets are the universal canonical choice: they are defined purely from the topological structure, which is the most basic structure any space of interest possesses.

Borel Measurable Functions

With the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra in place, the general definition of measurability from the previous subtopic specializes to the most important case. A function \(f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) is Borel measurable if:

\[\forall B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}), \quad f^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\]

That is, the preimage of every Borel set is a Borel set. This condition can be checked on the generating collection alone — it suffices to verify:

\[\forall a \in \mathbb{R}, \quad f^{-1}\big((-\infty, a]\big) = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : f(x) \leq a\} \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\]

This is a dramatic simplification: instead of checking all Borel sets, you only need to check that the sublevel sets of \(f\) are Borel. This is the measurability condition you will verify most often in practice.

The relationship between continuity and Borel measurability is clean and important. Every continuous function \(f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) is Borel measurable, because continuous functions pull open sets back to open sets, and open sets are Borel sets. The converse is false — Borel measurability is strictly weaker than continuity. Every monotone function is Borel measurable. Every pointwise limit of Borel measurable functions is Borel measurable. The class of Borel measurable functions is closed under the operations of analysis in a way that continuous functions are not, which is precisely why measurability rather than continuity is the right condition for integration theory.

The Borel σ-algebra and Probability Distributions

The canonical probability space for a real-valued random variable is \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}), \mu)\), where \(\mu\) is a probability measure — a function we will define precisely in the next subtopic on Lebesgue measure. For now, note that the law or distribution of a random variable \(X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}\) is the probability measure \(\mu_X\) on \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))\) defined by:

\[\mu_X(B) = P(X \in B) = P(X^{-1}(B)), \quad B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\]

This is well-defined precisely because \(X\) is measurable: \(X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}\) for every \(B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\), so \(P(X^{-1}(B))\) is always a legitimate probability. The distribution of a random variable is not defined on all subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) — it is defined on the Borel sets, and only on the Borel sets. This is the point where \(\sigma\)-algebras, Borel sets, and probability distributions converge into a single coherent structure.

The cumulative distribution function you studied in MATH 208 is:

\[F_X(x) = \mu_X\big((-\infty, x]\big), \quad x \in \mathbb{R}\]

which is the measure applied to the generating half-lines \((-\infty, x]\). The CDF completely determines \(\mu_X\) on all of \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) — a consequence of the fact that \((-\infty, x]\) generates \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) and measures are determined by their values on generating collections. Your CDF from MATH 208 was secretly the restriction of a Borel measure to its simplest generating sets.

WHY THIS MATTERS

In MATH 213 (Analysis I), you worked extensively with open and closed sets on the real line, and you proved results about continuous functions pulling open sets back to open sets. Every such result was implicitly a result about Borel measurable functions, since continuous functions are Borel measurable and open sets are Borel sets. In MATH 208, every probability distribution you encountered — uniform, normal, exponential — was implicitly a measure on \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))\), with the CDF encoding its values on the generating collection. What you are building now is the explicit structure that was always operating underneath those results.

Layer 3 (Bridge)

Every element of the cartographer’s story has a precise mathematical address. Let us walk through them one by one, until the archive, the building, and the formalism are a single unified picture in your mind.

The terrain of Kathmandu valley — the full landscape being mapped — is the real line \(\mathbb{R}\), or more generally the topological space \((X, \mathcal{T})\). It is the sample space \(\Omega\) from the previous subtopic, now equipped with an additional structure: a topology, a notion of which subsets count as open. On the real line, this topology is the standard one — the open sets are exactly the countable unions of open intervals. The terrain is not just a set of points. It is a set of points with a built-in notion of nearness, inherited from the metric \(d(x,y) = |x - y|\) you studied in Topic 1.

The hand-drawn sketches in your predecessor’s archive — the simplest, most natural regions — are the open intervals \((a, b)\). These are the generating collection \(\mathcal{G} = \{(a,b) : a < b, \; a,b \in \mathbb{R}\}\). They are the atoms of the topological structure of \(\mathbb{R}\) — the building blocks that every open set can be assembled from via countable unions. Your predecessor did not draw every possible region. They drew only the simplest ones, because the generation process takes care of everything else. The choice of open intervals as the seed is not arbitrary — it is the most natural choice precisely because they generate the topology, and the topology is the most fundamental structure on the space.

The instruction to take the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra containing all the sketches is the definition \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) = \sigma(\mathcal{T})\). This is the generation process made explicit. You are not constructing the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra by listing its members one by one — that would be impossible, since it is uncountably large. You are constructing it by specifying its seed and the operations that close it. The intersection formula \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) = \bigcap\{\mathcal{F} : \mathcal{F} \text{ is a } \sigma\text{-algebra and } \mathcal{T} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\}\) is the mathematical statement that you take the tightest possible \(\sigma\)-algebra around the seed — the one that adds nothing beyond what logical consistency with the three axioms requires.

The layered building — ground floor, first floor, second floor, infinitely upward — is the Borel hierarchy. The ground floor is the open sets \(\mathcal{T}\) themselves, the raw sketches. The first floor of closed sets is produced by taking complements of open sets — axiom (ii) applied once. The \(G_\delta\) sets on the next level are countable intersections of open sets — axiom (iii) applied to complements. The \(F_\sigma\) sets are countable unions of closed sets — axiom (iii) applied directly. Each subsequent floor — \(G_{\delta\sigma}\), \(F_{\sigma\delta}\), and beyond — is produced by applying countable operations to the floor below. The full Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) is the entire building: every floor, every room, every set reachable by any finite or countably infinite sequence of these operations starting from the open sets.

The closed interval \([a,b]\) appearing when you take the complement of an open interval is a first-floor object — a closed set, which is also a \(G_\delta\) set. The explicit construction \([a,b] = \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty (a - \frac{1}{n}, b + \frac{1}{n})\) is the cartographic operation of taking a countable intersection of open sketches that are progressively tighter around the closed region. The closed interval is not a seed — it is a derived object, built from the open intervals by one application of countable intersection. The fact that it is also the complement of the open set \((-\infty, a) \cup (b, \infty)\) gives you two different paths to the same room in the building, which is a signal of the richness of the structure.

The rationals \(\mathbb{Q}\) are an \(F_\sigma\) set — a countable union of closed singletons \(\{q\}\) for each \(q \in \mathbb{Q}\). Each singleton \(\{q\}\) is a closed set, being the complement of the open set \((-\infty, q) \cup (q, \infty)\). Their countable union \(\bigcup_{q \in \mathbb{Q}} \{q\} = \mathbb{Q}\) is therefore an \(F_\sigma\) set, and hence a Borel set. The irrationals \(\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}\), being the complement of a Borel set, are also Borel — specifically a \(G_\delta\) set. Both are reachable from the open intervals by a finite sequence of operations. Neither requires any exotic construction.

The wilderness outside the building — the subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) that are not Borel — is the break moment made precise. The Vitali set construction shows that there exist subsets of \([0,1]\) that cannot be assigned a consistent Lebesgue measure. These sets are not reachable by any countable sequence of operations starting from open intervals. They live outside the building entirely. The break destroys the naive assumption that “close under countable operations starting from simple sets” eventually captures everything — it does not, because \(\mathbb{R}\) is uncountable, and countable operations starting from a countable seed can produce only countably many sets at each level, while \(2^\mathbb{R}\) is strictly larger than any countable tower can reach.

The Lebesgue \(\sigma\)-algebra being strictly larger than \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) is the nuance the break moment adds. The Lebesgue \(\sigma\)-algebra includes all subsets of Lebesgue null sets — sets of measure zero — even non-Borel ones. This is called completion of the measure, and it produces a \(\sigma\)-algebra that is slightly larger than \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) but still well-behaved for integration purposes. The Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is canonical because it is generated by the topology. The Lebesgue \(\sigma\)-algebra is larger because it has been completed. In most of the roadmap, the distinction will not matter — every set you encounter will be both Borel and Lebesgue measurable — but knowing the distinction exists prevents a subtle and common confusion.

The half-lines \((-\infty, x]\) as a generating collection for \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) is the bridge between the abstract Borel structure and the CDF you used in MATH 208. The cumulative distribution function \(F_X(x) = P(X \leq x) = \mu_X((-\infty, x])\) is the probability measure evaluated on these generating half-lines. The fact that the CDF completely determines the distribution \(\mu_X\) on all of \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) is a consequence of a general measure-theoretic principle: a measure is uniquely determined by its values on a generating collection, provided that collection satisfies a mild technical condition called a \(\pi\)-system. The CDF you computed in MATH 208 was always encoding the full probability measure on \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) — you were reading the measure through its simplest generating window without knowing the full structure behind it.

The distribution of a random variable \(\mu_X(B) = P(X \in B)\) for \(B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) is the full probability measure on the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra — the cartographer’s complete and consistent map. The measurability of \(X\) — the condition \(X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}\) for every Borel set \(B\) — is the guarantee that every room in the building corresponds to a legitimate question about the experiment. The random variable \(X\) is the translation device between the abstract probability space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\) and the canonical Borel space \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}), \mu_X)\). Measurability is the condition that this translation is faithful — that no information is smuggled in or lost.

The connection to MATH 213 (Analysis I) is now fully explicit in both directions. Every continuity argument you made — showing that a function pulls open sets back to open sets — was implicitly a Borel measurability argument, since continuous functions are Borel measurable and open sets are Borel. Every convergence argument involving sequences of real numbers was implicitly an argument inside \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))\), since every singleton, every interval, every open and closed set you ever mentioned is a Borel set. The real line you worked with in Analysis I was always secretly the Borel space \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))\) — you were navigating the building without a map of its floors.

The connection to MATH 208 (Statistics and Probability) closes the loop completely. Every distribution — uniform, normal, exponential, Poisson — is a probability measure on \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))\). Every event you wrote as \(\{X \leq x\}\) or \(\{a < X \leq b\}\) is a Borel set. Every CDF is the measure restricted to the generating half-lines. The probability theory of MATH 208 was always the theory of measures on Borel spaces. What you have now is the explicit architecture of the building that probability theory inhabits.

Looking forward across the roadmap, the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is the silent universal foundation. In Phase 1, the space of probability distributions that the Wasserstein distance operates on consists of measures on \((\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n))\) — Borel probability measures. The transport plans and couplings of Optimal Transport are measures on the product space \((\mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n) \otimes \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n))\), and the product \(\sigma\)-algebra identity \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n) = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})^{\otimes n}\) is what makes the coordinate structure of that product space well-defined. In Phase 3, when score matching learns the gradient of \(\log p(x)\), the density \(p(x)\) is a Borel measurable function — and its existence as a well-defined object is guaranteed by the Radon-Nikodym theorem, which requires the underlying \(\sigma\)-algebra to be exactly the kind of structure you have now built.

Every probability distribution on the roadmap lives in a Borel space. Every density function is a Borel measurable function. Every integral is a Lebesgue integral over a Borel measurable set. The cartographer’s building is not background scenery — it is the house that all of modern probability theory lives in.
