Topic 2 - Score Matching: Learning the Direction of Probability Mass

Introduction

Topic 1 established the architectural framework: a neural network \(f_\theta(x,t)\) parameterizes a time-dependent velocity field, an ODE solver integrates it to produce samples, the adjoint method trains it with constant memory, and the instantaneous change of variables formula computes exact log-likelihoods. The neural ODE is the engine. Topic 1 said nothing specific about what velocity field to learn — it only said how to learn it once you have defined a training objective.

Topic 2 answers the question that Topic 1 left open: what is the velocity field, and how do you teach a neural network to produce it without computing quantities that are intractable for high-dimensional data?

The answer is the score function. From Chapter 1’s Topic 1, you know that the Wasserstein gradient flow of entropy produces the heat equation, and the key object driving that flow is the velocity field \(v_t = -\nabla\log\rho_t\) — the negative gradient of the log-density, pointing from high-density regions toward low-density regions. From Chapter 1’s Topic 2, you know that the Anderson reverse-time SDE requires the score function \(s_t(x) = \nabla\log\rho_t(x)\) as the essential correction term that turns diffusion’s spreading tendency into concentration toward data. From Topic 1 of this chapter, you know that the probability flow ODE velocity is \(v_t = b_t - \frac{\sigma_t^2}{2}s_t\) — the score function determines the ODE that generates samples.

The score function is the thread connecting everything. It is the Wasserstein gradient of entropy. It is the reverse-time correction. It is half the probability flow ODE velocity. Learning the score function is sufficient to fully specify the generative model — both the stochastic sampler (reverse SDE) and the deterministic sampler (probability flow ODE). Score matching is the family of methods for learning the score function from data without computing the intractable normalizing constant that makes direct estimation impossible.

The central difficulty is this. The score function \(\nabla\log\rho_t(x)\) requires knowing \(\rho_t(x)\) — the probability density at every point in the data space. For the forward OU process, \(\rho_t\) is the convolution of the data distribution with a Gaussian, and this convolution is intractable for complex high-dimensional data distributions like images or molecular structures: you cannot write down a formula for \(\rho_t(x)\) that you can differentiate. You have samples from \(\rho_t\) — you can generate them by taking data points \(x_0\sim p_{\text{data}}\) and adding noise \(\alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t\epsilon\) — but you cannot evaluate \(\rho_t(x)\) at an arbitrary point \(x\) without integrating over all data points, which is intractable for large datasets.

Score matching resolves this by reformulating the training objective. Instead of directly minimizing \(\mathbb{E}[\|s_\theta(x,t) - \nabla\log\rho_t(x)\|^2]\) — which requires knowing \(\nabla\log\rho_t\) — it finds an equivalent objective expressible purely in terms of quantities computable from samples. The key mathematical trick, due to Hyvärinen (2005), is integration by parts: the Fisher divergence \(\mathbb{E}[\|s_\theta - \nabla\log\rho\|^2]\) can be rewritten as \(\mathbb{E}[\|s_\theta\|^2 + 2\nabla\cdot s_\theta] + C\), where the term involving the intractable \(\nabla\log\rho\) has vanished and been replaced by the divergence of \(s_\theta\) itself — which is computable. This is the original score matching objective. Denoising score matching, developed independently and shown to be equivalent, sidesteps even the divergence computation by using the known structure of the forward noising process to replace \(\nabla\log\rho_t\) with the known noise direction, making training a simple regression problem.

This topic has three components that build in strict logical sequence.

The first component is the Fisher divergence and the original score matching objective. The Fisher divergence \(\mathbb{E}_{\rho}[\|s_\theta(x) - \nabla\log\rho(x)\|^2]\) is the natural distance between a score network \(s_\theta\) and the true score \(\nabla\log\rho\). Hyvärinen’s score matching theorem shows that minimizing the Fisher divergence is equivalent to minimizing \(\mathbb{E}_\rho[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta(x)\|^2 + \nabla\cdot s_\theta(x)]\), where the divergence \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta = \text{tr}(\nabla_x s_\theta)\) is the trace of the Jacobian of \(s_\theta\). This equivalence holds by integration by parts — the same divergence theorem from MATH 201 that appeared in the Fokker-Planck derivation and the FFJORD likelihood computation. The original score matching objective is exact but computationally expensive in high dimensions: computing \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta\) requires the trace of the Jacobian of \(s_\theta\), which costs \(O(d)\) backward passes — the same Hutchinson bottleneck as in Topic 1. Sliced score matching resolves this by projecting the score onto random directions before computing the divergence, reducing the cost to \(O(1)\) backward passes at the expense of variance.

The second component is denoising score matching and its equivalence to the original objective. Vincent (2011) showed that the score matching objective for a smoothed density \(\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = \int\rho(x_0)\mathcal{N}(x; x_0, \sigma^2 I)\,dx_0\) — the original data distribution convolved with Gaussian noise — is equivalent to the denoising objective \(\mathbb{E}_{x_0\sim\rho, \epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)}[\|s_\theta(x_0+\sigma\epsilon,\sigma) - (-\epsilon/\sigma)\|^2]\): learning to predict the noise direction \(-\epsilon/\sigma\) from the noisy observation \(x_0+\sigma\epsilon\). This equivalence has two profound consequences. First, the training objective requires no Jacobian computation — it is a simple mean squared error regression problem, as computationally cheap as any standard supervised learning task. Second, by Tweedie’s formula from Chapter 1 Topic 2, learning to predict the noise direction \(-\epsilon/\sigma\) is equivalent to learning the score function \(\nabla\log\rho_\sigma(x)\) of the smoothed distribution. The denoising score matching objective is therefore simultaneously a simple regression problem and an exact score estimator for the smoothed density.

The third component is the noise-conditional score network and the unified training objective for diffusion models. Song and Ermon (2019) extended denoising score matching to a continuous range of noise levels \(\sigma\in[\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max}]\), training a single noise-conditional score network \(s_\theta(x,\sigma)\) to estimate \(\nabla\log\rho_\sigma(x)\) at all noise levels simultaneously. This produces the score function at every point in the noise schedule — not just at one fixed noise level — giving everything needed to run the reverse SDE or probability flow ODE at any time \(t\). Ho, Jain, and Abbeel (2020) showed that weighting the different noise levels appropriately and using the OU process as the forward noising mechanism gives DDPM — the denoising diffusion probabilistic model — whose training objective is precisely the noise-weighted sum of denoising score matching objectives across the noise schedule. The DDPM training procedure is score matching; the DDPM sampling procedure is numerical integration of the reverse SDE with the learned score; and the DDIM sampler is the probability flow ODE with the learned score, as established in Topic 1.

The connections to your curriculum are direct and concrete across three courses.

Your MATH 201 course on Advanced Calculus provides the integration by parts identity that is the mathematical heart of score matching. The Hyvärinen equivalence is proved by expanding \(\|s_\theta - \nabla\log\rho\|^2 = \|s_\theta\|^2 - 2s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho + \|\nabla\log\rho\|^2\), noting that \(\|\nabla\log\rho\|^2\) is a constant with respect to \(\theta\), and converting the cross-term \(-2\mathbb{E}[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho]\) to \(+2\mathbb{E}[\nabla\cdot s_\theta]\) via the divergence theorem — the same integration by parts used in the Fokker-Planck derivation. The divergence \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta = \sum_i\partial s_\theta^i/\partial x_i\) is the trace of the Jacobian from MATH 201’s multivariate calculus.

Your MATH 208 course on Statistics and Probability provides the probabilistic framework for denoising score matching. The conditional expectation \(\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|X_t = x] = -\sigma_t s_t(x)\) is Bayes’ theorem applied to the Gaussian forward process — Tweedie’s formula from Chapter 1. The denoising score matching loss is a mean squared error regression — the fundamental quantity from MATH 208’s estimation theory, measuring the expected squared difference between the predicted and true noise directions. The noise schedule \(\sigma_t\) and signal coefficient \(\alpha_t\) of the OU process determine the signal-to-noise ratio \(\text{SNR}(t) = \alpha_t^2/\sigma_t^2\) that weights the contribution of each noise level to the total training objective.

Your MSCS 202 course on Numerical Methods provides the discretization schemes — Euler-Maruyama for the stochastic reverse SDE, Euler and Runge-Kutta for the deterministic probability flow ODE — that convert the continuous-time score-based model into a discrete-time sampling algorithm. The error analysis of these discretization schemes — how many steps are needed for a given distributional accuracy — uses the same global error bounds from MATH 217 that appeared in Topic 1’s neural ODE analysis.

By the end of this topic, score matching will feel not like a clever computational trick for avoiding an intractable quantity but like the inevitable consequence of asking: how do you learn the direction of probability mass from samples alone, without computing the normalizing constant? The answer — use integration by parts to eliminate the intractable density from the objective, or use the forward process’s known structure to replace the intractable score with a computable noise direction — is the same mathematical move in two different forms. Both forms train the same function: the score \(\nabla\log\rho_t\), the Wasserstein gradient of entropy, the reverse-time correction, the half-velocity of the probability flow ODE. Learning the score is learning the geometry of the data distribution — the direction, at every point in the space and at every noise level, in which probability mass is most concentrated.

Layer 1 (Story)

It is 2022. A catastrophic glacial lake outburst flood — a GLOF event — has struck the Marsyangdi river valley in Manang district. The flood has displaced thousands of villagers across a rugged 200-square-kilometre search zone. Nepal’s Search and Rescue Coordination Centre in Kathmandu has activated its emergency response network, and you are the chief data scientist tasked with a specific problem: given the current distribution of rescue teams across the terrain, predict where survivors are most likely to be found, and direct the teams toward regions of highest survivor density.

The survivor distribution is unknown. You cannot see it directly. What you can observe are signals — smoke from fires, sound of voices, footprints in mud, thermal signatures from drones — that tell you, locally and noisily, which direction the survivor density is increasing. At any terrain point \(x\), a rescue team’s observations tell them not the survivor density \(\rho(x)\) itself but its gradient direction: are there more survivors uphill or downhill, to the left or to the right? The gradient of the log-density \(\nabla\log\rho(x)\) — the direction of steepest increase in survivor concentration — is what each team can estimate locally from their observations, even though none of them can estimate the total density \(\rho(x)\) without knowing how many survivors there are in total.

This is the score function: \(s(x) = \nabla\log\rho(x)\). Not the density. Not the count. The direction — at each location — in which survivor concentration is increasing most rapidly. Learning this direction field from local observations, without ever counting the total number of survivors, is score matching.

The normalizing constant problem: why you cannot count survivors.

The coordination centre receives a question from the government: how many survivors are there in total? You explain the difficulty. To know the total count, you would need to integrate the survivor density over the entire 200-square-kilometre search zone: \(Z = \int_\Omega\rho(x)\,dx\). But you do not know \(\rho(x)\) at every point — you only have noisy local observations from seventeen rescue teams scattered across the terrain. Computing the integral would require either surveying every point in the 200-square-kilometre zone (impossible) or fitting a global density model from the sparse observations (inaccurate and expensive).

Here is the key insight: you do not need the total count to direct the rescue teams. You need the gradient direction. Knowing that survivor density is increasing toward the northwest at a rate of 3 survivors per kilometre tells you to send teams northwest — without knowing whether the total survivor count is 500 or 5000. The gradient \(\nabla\log\rho(x) = \nabla\rho(x)/\rho(x)\) captures the relative rate of change — how much denser is one region relative to its neighbours — without requiring knowledge of the absolute density level.

The score function \(s(x) = \nabla\log\rho(x)\) is the rescue operation’s fundamental information object: it encodes everything you need to navigate toward survivors, even when you cannot enumerate them. This is why score matching — learning \(s(x)\) rather than \(\rho(x)\) — is both more tractable and more directly useful for the generative modeling problem: the reverse-time SDE and probability flow ODE both require \(s_t(x) = \nabla\log\rho_t(x)\), not \(\rho_t(x)\) itself.

The naive approach: direct score estimation and why it fails.

Your data science team’s junior analyst, Sushil, tries the direct approach. Fit a parametric model \(s_\theta(x)\) to approximate the true score \(s(x) = \nabla\log\rho(x)\) by minimizing the mean squared error between them:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{naive}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim\rho}\left[\|s_\theta(x) - \nabla\log\rho(x)\|^2\right]\]

Sushil writes the code and immediately hits the wall. The training data consists of survivor location observations — samples from \(\rho\). But the loss function requires evaluating \(\nabla\log\rho(x)\) at each observed location — and this requires knowing \(\rho(x)\), the density at \(x\), which is exactly what you are trying to learn. You cannot compute the loss without already knowing the answer.

The senior analyst, Maya, recognizes the problem immediately: the naive objective requires the ground truth score function as a label for each training point, but the ground truth score is the thing we are trying to estimate. This is circular. We need a training objective that measures how well \(s_\theta\) approximates \(\nabla\log\rho\) without ever computing \(\nabla\log\rho\) itself.

Hyvärinen’s insight: integration by parts removes the intractable term.

Maya recalls a mathematical trick from her Analysis II course. The problematic term in the naive loss is the cross-product \(-2\mathbb{E}[s_\theta(x)\cdot\nabla\log\rho(x)]\) — the inner product between the predicted score and the true score. This term requires knowing \(\nabla\log\rho\). What if you could rewrite it using integration by parts — moving the derivative from \(\log\rho\) to \(s_\theta\)?

The divergence theorem says: \(\int f\cdot\nabla g\,d\rho = -\int(\nabla\cdot f)g\,d\rho + \text{boundary terms}\). Applying this with \(f = s_\theta\) and \(g = \log\rho\), noting that \(\nabla g = \nabla\log\rho\) and \(d\rho = \rho\,dx\):

\[\mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta(x)\cdot\nabla\log\rho(x)] = -\mathbb{E}_\rho[\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x)] + \text{boundary terms}\]

The boundary terms vanish if \(\rho\) decays sufficiently fast at infinity — which it does for well-behaved distributions like the Gaussian-smoothed survivor density. The cross-term involving the intractable \(\nabla\log\rho\) has been converted into \(-\mathbb{E}[\nabla\cdot s_\theta]\) — the divergence of \(s_\theta\), which requires no knowledge of \(\rho\) whatsoever. You can compute \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x) = \sum_i\partial s_\theta^i/\partial x_i\) from the network \(s_\theta\) alone.

Expanding the original naive loss and substituting:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{naive}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\rho\left[\|s_\theta(x)\|^2 + 2\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x)\right] + C\]

where \(C = \mathbb{E}_\rho[\|\nabla\log\rho\|^2]\) is a constant independent of \(\theta\). Since minimizing over \(\theta\) ignores constants, the Hyvärinen score matching objective is:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{SM}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\rho\left[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta(x)\|^2 + \nabla\cdot s_\theta(x)\right]\]

No \(\rho\) appears anywhere. No \(\nabla\log\rho\) appears anywhere. You only need samples from \(\rho\) (the observed survivor locations) and the ability to evaluate \(s_\theta\) and its divergence at those locations. The intractable normalizing constant has been eliminated by integration by parts — the same mathematical move used to derive the Fokker-Planck equation from Itô’s formula.

The rescue team’s interpretation of the score matching objective.

Maya explains the objective to the rescue team coordinators using the terrain analogy.

The first term \(\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta(x)\|^2\) penalizes the score network for predicting large gradient magnitudes — for confidently pointing teams in a specific direction — at observed survivor locations. If the network claims the density is increasing steeply to the northwest at a known survivor location, it is asserting strong local structure in the density, which should be penalized unless the data supports it.

The second term \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x)\) is the divergence of the predicted score field at each observed location. Positive divergence means the predicted score field is spreading outward from that location — the network is predicting that density decreases in all directions away from the observed point. Negative divergence means the score field is converging toward the observed location — the network is predicting a local density maximum there. Adding the divergence to the objective pushes the network to create inward-pointing score fields at observed survivor locations — exactly the correct behavior for a distribution that concentrates mass at likely survivor positions.

Together, the two terms balance: the first prevents the network from predicting overly strong gradients, and the second encourages the network to predict score fields that concentrate toward the observed data. At the optimum, the balance produces a score field that exactly matches \(\nabla\log\rho\) — pointing toward density peaks everywhere.

The divergence bottleneck: a new computational problem.

Sushil implements the Hyvärinen objective and immediately discovers a new problem. Computing the divergence \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x) = \sum_{i=1}^d\partial s_\theta^i/\partial x_i\) requires computing \(d\) partial derivatives — one per spatial dimension. Each partial derivative requires one backward pass through the neural network \(s_\theta\). For the Marsyangdi search zone discretized into a 100-metre grid, \(d = 2000\) spatial dimensions. For a molecular structure generation problem, \(d\) might be thousands. Computing the divergence costs \(O(d)\) backward passes per training point — prohibitively expensive.

This is the same Jacobian trace bottleneck encountered in Topic 1’s FFJORD instantaneous change of variables formula. And the same fix applies: the Hutchinson estimator. Using a random vector \(z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\): \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x) = \text{tr}(\nabla_x s_\theta(x)) \approx z^\top\nabla_x s_\theta(x)z\), computable with one Jacobian-vector product. This is sliced score matching — it converts the exact Hyvärinen objective into a computationally tractable stochastic approximation, at the cost of variance in the gradient estimates.

The denoising breakthrough: a completely different approach.

While Sushil is debugging the sliced score matching implementation, Maya discovers a fundamentally different approach. Instead of estimating the score of \(\rho\) directly, add known Gaussian noise to the survivor locations to create a smoothed version of the survivor distribution, and estimate the score of the smoothed distribution. The smoothed distribution \(\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = \int\rho(x_0)\mathcal{N}(x;x_0,\sigma^2)\,dx_0\) is the original density convolved with a Gaussian — it represents the distribution of survivor locations plus random displacement noise of magnitude \(\sigma\).

Now here is the key observation. A survivor who is truly at location \(x_0\) appears at noisy observed location \(x = x_0 + \sigma\epsilon\) for \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\). The score of the smoothed distribution at the noisy location \(x\) is:

\[\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = -\frac{\epsilon}{\sigma} = -\frac{x - x_0}{\sigma^2}\]

This is a remarkable fact: the score of the smoothed distribution at the noisy observation \(x = x_0+\sigma\epsilon\) points directly toward the clean location \(x_0\), with magnitude \(1/\sigma\). The score tells you: go back toward where the survivor actually was, before the noise was added. The direction from the noisy observation to the clean signal is \(x_0 - x = -\sigma\epsilon\), and the score is this direction normalized by \(\sigma^2\).

More precisely, the score \(\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)\) can be computed from the conditional distribution \(p(x_0|x)\) via Tweedie’s formula:

\[\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = \frac{\mathbb{E}[x_0|x] - x}{\sigma^2}\]

The optimal denoiser — predicting the clean survivor location from the noisy observation — is directly related to the score function. Learning to denoise is learning the score.

The denoising score matching objective: regression without intractability.

This observation converts score estimation into a supervised learning problem. You have pairs \((x_0, x)\) — clean survivor location and noisy observation — generated by the process \(x = x_0 + \sigma\epsilon\). You want to train a network \(s_\theta(x,\sigma)\) to predict \(\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = -\epsilon/\sigma\). The training objective is:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x_0\sim\rho,\,\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)}\left[\left\|s_\theta(x_0 + \sigma\epsilon,\,\sigma) + \frac{\epsilon}{\sigma}\right\|^2\right]\]

This is mean squared error between the network’s score prediction and the known noise direction \(-\epsilon/\sigma\). No divergence computation. No Jacobian. No intractable density. Just regression on pairs \((x_0+\sigma\epsilon, -\epsilon/\sigma)\) — synthetic data generated from the known noising process.

The rescue team analogy: you teach the network where survivors really are by showing it noisy observations and telling it the direction toward the true location. After training, the network can estimate the score — the direction toward survivors — from any noisy observation, even without knowing the noise that was added.

The break moment: why one noise level is not enough.

You train the denoising score matching network at a fixed noise level \(\sigma = 500\) metres — a reasonable search radius for the rescue operation. The trained network \(s_\theta(x, 500\text{m})\) accurately predicts the score of the \(\sigma=500\text{m}\)-smoothed survivor distribution. You use it to direct rescue teams.

The teams find survivors efficiently at first. But as the search zone becomes sparser — most survivors found, only isolated individuals remaining in remote locations — the trained score network starts failing. The score at any observed location now points toward the nearest known survivor cluster, even if that cluster is 5 kilometres away. The network’s score predictions are dominated by large-scale density features and cannot resolve fine-scale local structure.

Here is the naive assumption that breaks: one noise level \(\sigma\) is sufficient to learn the score function for sampling.

It breaks in two ways. At large \(\sigma\) (heavy noise), the smoothed distribution \(\tilde\rho_\sigma\) is nearly Gaussian — it has lost all fine-grained structure of the original distribution. The score \(\nabla\log\tilde\rho_\sigma\) points toward the global density center with no information about local structure. This is useful for coarse navigation — getting teams into the right general area — but not for fine-grained search. At small \(\sigma\) (light noise), the score captures fine-grained local structure but the training data is sparse: most of the space has very few noisy observations near any particular \(x\), making the score estimate unreliable everywhere except near observed survivor locations.

The correct approach — established by Song and Ermon (2019) — is to train on many noise levels simultaneously, from large \(\sigma\) (coarse structure, reliable estimates) down to small \(\sigma\) (fine structure, sparse but informative). The noise-conditional score network \(s_\theta(x, \sigma)\) or equivalently \(s_\theta(x, t)\) for the time-indexed noise schedule, learns the score at every noise level, enabling a sampling procedure that navigates from the coarse to the fine: start at large noise (coarse Gaussian landscape, easy to navigate), gradually reduce noise (finer landscape, more complex terrain), and arrive at small noise (sharp survivor distribution, accurate local search). This is the annealed Langevin dynamics of Song-Ermon, the DDPM sampling schedule of Ho et al., and the reverse SDE of Anderson — all instances of multi-scale score learning navigating from large to small noise.

The noise-conditional score network: learning across all scales.

The training objective for the noise-conditional score network integrates across the noise schedule with a weighting function \(\lambda(\sigma)\):

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{NCSN}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{t\sim U[0,T]}\left[\lambda(t)\cdot\mathbb{E}_{x_0\sim p_{\text{data}},\,\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)}\left[\left\|s_\theta(\alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t\epsilon,\,t) + \frac{\epsilon}{\sigma_t}\right\|^2\right]\right]\]

where \(\alpha_t\) and \(\sigma_t\) are the signal and noise coefficients of the OU process. The weighting \(\lambda(t) = \sigma_t^2\) — chosen so that the loss is dimensionally consistent and all noise levels contribute equally to the total training signal — produces the DDPM objective of Ho et al. (2020) after reparameterization: \(\mathbb{E}_{t,x_0,\epsilon}[\|\epsilon_\theta(\alpha_t x_0+\sigma_t\epsilon, t) - \epsilon\|^2]\), where \(\epsilon_\theta = -\sigma_t s_\theta\) is the noise prediction network.

The rescue operation analogy: train the rescue coordination network on observations from all scales simultaneously — some teams searching in 5-kilometre radius sectors (large \(\sigma\), coarse search), some in 500-metre sectors (medium \(\sigma\)), some in 50-metre sectors (small \(\sigma\), fine search). The network learns a consistent score field across all scales, enabling a sampling procedure that starts with large-scale navigation and progressively zooms in toward precise survivor locations.

Three pictures to carry into the mathematics.

The first picture: the score field as a direction field over the search terrain — at each location \(x\), an arrow pointing toward higher survivor density. The score function \(s(x) = \nabla\log\rho(x)\) is this direction field. Rescue teams follow the arrows. The generative model generates samples by following the reverse-time score field from noise to data.

The second picture: the Hyvärinen integration by parts — the problematic cross-term \(\mathbb{E}[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho]\) converted to \(-\mathbb{E}[\nabla\cdot s_\theta]\) by the divergence theorem, eliminating \(\nabla\log\rho\) from the objective entirely. The boundary terms vanish because \(\rho\) decays at infinity. The resulting objective \(\mathbb{E}[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta\|^2 + \nabla\cdot s_\theta]\) requires only samples from \(\rho\) and evaluations of \(s_\theta\).

The third picture: the denoising equivalence — a noisy observation \(x = x_0 + \sigma\epsilon\) paired with the noise direction \(-\epsilon/\sigma\) pointing back toward the clean location. Training \(s_\theta(x,\sigma)\) to predict \(-\epsilon/\sigma\) is simultaneously learning the score of the smoothed distribution \(\tilde\rho_\sigma\). Multi-scale training across all \(\sigma\) gives the noise-conditional score network, enabling annealed sampling from coarse to fine.

Layer 2 (Mathematics)

We build the formalism in six stages: the Fisher divergence and score matching objective, Hyvärinen’s integration by parts theorem, denoising score matching and Vincent’s equivalence theorem, the noise-conditional score network and DDPM, the connection between score matching and the reverse SDE, and the unified framework of continuous-time score-based generative models.

The Fisher Divergence and Score Matching

Definition (Score function). For a differentiable density \(\rho: \mathbb{R}^d\to\mathbb{R}_{>0}\), the score function is:

\[s(x) = \nabla_x\log\rho(x) = \frac{\nabla_x\rho(x)}{\rho(x)}\]

Definition (Fisher divergence). The Fisher divergence between densities \(p\) and \(q\) is:

\[\mathcal{F}(p\|q) = \mathbb{E}_p\!\left[\|\nabla\log p(x) - \nabla\log q(x)\|^2\right] = \int_{\mathbb{R}^d}\|\nabla\log p - \nabla\log q\|^2\,p\,dx\]

Unlike KL divergence, \(\mathcal{F}(p\|q)\) depends only on the scores of \(p\) and \(q\), not on their densities directly.

Score matching objective. Given data density \(\rho\) and score network \(s_\theta: \mathbb{R}^d\to\mathbb{R}^d\), the explicit score matching objective is:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\rho\!\left[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta(x) - \nabla\log\rho(x)\|^2\right] = \frac{1}{2}\mathcal{F}(\rho\|q_\theta)\]

where \(q_\theta\) is the model whose score is \(s_\theta\).

Expanding the objective:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\rho\!\left[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta(x)\|^2 - s_\theta(x)\cdot\nabla\log\rho(x) + \frac{1}{2}\|\nabla\log\rho(x)\|^2\right]\]

The last term is constant in \(\theta\). The problematic term is \(-\mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta(x)\cdot\nabla\log\rho(x)]\) — it requires knowing \(\nabla\log\rho(x)\).

Hyvärinen’s Score Matching Theorem

Theorem (Hyvärinen 2005). Under the regularity conditions that \(\rho\) is differentiable, \(\rho(x)\to 0\) as \(\|x\|\to\infty\) sufficiently fast, and \(s_\theta\) satisfies \(\rho(x)\|s_\theta(x)\|\to 0\) as \(\|x\|\to\infty\):

\[\mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta(x)\cdot\nabla\log\rho(x)] = -\mathbb{E}_\rho[\nabla_x\cdot s_\theta(x)]\]

Proof. Write \(\nabla\log\rho = \nabla\rho/\rho\):

\[\mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho] = \int s_\theta(x)\cdot\nabla\rho(x)\,dx = \int s_\theta(x)\cdot\nabla\rho(x)\,dx\]

Apply the integration by parts formula (divergence theorem in \(\mathbb{R}^d\)):

\[\int s_\theta\cdot\nabla\rho\,dx = -\int(\nabla\cdot s_\theta)\rho\,dx + \oint_{\partial\Omega}(s_\theta\cdot n)\rho\,dS\]

As the domain \(\Omega\to\mathbb{R}^d\), the boundary term \(\oint_{\partial\Omega}(s_\theta\cdot n)\rho\,dS\to 0\) by the decay assumption \(\rho(x)\|s_\theta(x)\|\to 0\). Therefore:

\[\mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho] = -\int(\nabla\cdot s_\theta)\rho\,dx = -\mathbb{E}_\rho[\nabla\cdot s_\theta] \qquad\square\]

Corollary (Implicit score matching objective — Hyvärinen 2005). Minimizing \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}}(\theta)\) is equivalent to minimizing:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{ISM}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\rho\!\left[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta(x)\|^2 + \nabla_x\cdot s_\theta(x)\right]\]

since \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ISM}}(\theta) + C\) where \(C = \frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}_\rho[\|\nabla\log\rho\|^2]\) is \(\theta\)-independent.

Proof. Substitute \(\mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho] = -\mathbb{E}_\rho[\nabla\cdot s_\theta]\) into the expanded \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}}\):

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}} = \mathbb{E}_\rho\!\left[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta\|^2\right] - \mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho] + C = \mathbb{E}_\rho\!\left[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta\|^2 + \nabla\cdot s_\theta\right] + C = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ISM}} + C \qquad\square\]

Computational cost. Computing \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x) = \sum_{i=1}^d\partial s_\theta^i/\partial x_i\) requires \(d\) Jacobian-vector products: \(\partial s_\theta^i/\partial x_i = e_i^\top\nabla_x s_\theta(x)e_i\) for standard basis vectors \(e_i\). Total cost: \(O(d)\) backward passes — the Hutchinson bottleneck.

Sliced Score Matching

Definition (Sliced score matching — Song et al. 2020). For \(v\sim p_v\) with \(\mathbb{E}[vv^\top]=I\):

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{SSM}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim\rho,\,v\sim p_v}\!\left[\frac{1}{2}(v^\top s_\theta(x))^2 + v^\top\nabla_x(v^\top s_\theta(x))\right]\]

Theorem. \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{SSM}}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ISM}}(\theta) + C'\) for a constant \(C'\) independent of \(\theta\).

Proof. \(\mathbb{E}_v[v^\top\nabla_x(v^\top s_\theta)] = \mathbb{E}_v[v^\top(\nabla_x s_\theta)v] = \text{tr}(\nabla_x s_\theta\cdot\mathbb{E}_v[vv^\top]) = \text{tr}(\nabla_x s_\theta) = \nabla\cdot s_\theta\). Similarly \(\mathbb{E}_v[(v^\top s_\theta)^2] = \mathbb{E}_v[v^\top s_\theta s_\theta^\top v] = \text{tr}(s_\theta s_\theta^\top\mathbb{E}[vv^\top]) = \|s_\theta\|^2\). \(\square\)

Cost: One JVP \(\nabla_x(v^\top s_\theta(x)) = \nabla_x s_\theta(x)\cdot v\) per sample — \(O(p)\) for a network with \(p\) parameters. Total cost reduced from \(O(dp)\) to \(O(p)\).

Denoising Score Matching

Setup. Let \(\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = \int\rho(x_0)\mathcal{N}(x;x_0,\sigma^2 I)\,dx_0\) be the noise-perturbed density — the original density convolved with \(\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I)\).

Score of the perturbed density. For \(x = x_0 + \sigma\epsilon\), \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\):

\[\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = \frac{\nabla_x\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)}{\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)} = \frac{\int\rho(x_0)\nabla_x\mathcal{N}(x;x_0,\sigma^2)\,dx_0}{\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)}\]

\[= \frac{\int\rho(x_0)\frac{-(x-x_0)}{\sigma^2}\mathcal{N}(x;x_0,\sigma^2)\,dx_0}{\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)} = -\frac{x - \mathbb{E}[x_0|x]}{\sigma^2} = -\frac{\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|x]}{\sigma}\]

This is Tweedie’s formula applied to the Gaussian perturbation model.

Theorem (Vincent 2011 — DSM equivalence). Minimizing the denoising score matching objective:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}}(\theta,\sigma) = \mathbb{E}_{x_0\sim\rho,\,\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)}\!\left[\left\|s_\theta(x_0+\sigma\epsilon,\,\sigma) + \frac{\epsilon}{\sigma}\right\|^2\right]\]

is equivalent to minimizing the implicit score matching objective \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ISM}}(\theta)\) for the perturbed density \(\tilde\rho_\sigma\).

Proof. Expand:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}} = \mathbb{E}_{x_0,\epsilon}\!\left[\left\|s_\theta(x,\sigma) - \nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)\right\|^2\right] - \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left\|\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)\right\|^2\right] + \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left\|\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) + \frac{\epsilon}{\sigma}\right\|^2\right]\]

The last term equals \(\mathbb{E}[\|\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) - (-\epsilon/\sigma)\|^2\cdot\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)/p(x,x_0)]\) — which by Tweedie’s formula satisfies \(\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|x] = -\sigma\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)\), so the last term is a constant in \(\theta\). Therefore \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}}(\theta,\sigma) = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}}^{\tilde\rho_\sigma}(\theta) + C\). \(\square\)

Computational cost: \(O(p)\) per sample — evaluate \(s_\theta(x_0+\sigma\epsilon,\sigma)\) and compare to \(-\epsilon/\sigma\). No Jacobian, no divergence.

The Noise-Conditional Score Network and DDPM

Multi-scale score matching. For the OU forward process \(X_t = \alpha_t X_0 + \sigma_t\epsilon\), \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\), the marginal density is:

\[\rho_t(x) = \int p_{\text{data}}(x_0)\mathcal{N}(x;\alpha_t x_0,\sigma_t^2 I)\,dx_0\]

with score:

\[\nabla_x\log\rho_t(x) = -\frac{x - \alpha_t\mathbb{E}[X_0|X_t=x]}{\sigma_t^2} = -\frac{\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|X_t=x]}{\sigma_t}\]

Definition (NCSN objective — Song-Ermon 2019). The noise-conditional score network (NCSN) training objective:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{NCSN}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{t\sim U[0,T]}\!\left[\lambda(t)\cdot\mathbb{E}_{X_0\sim p_{\text{data}},\,\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)}\!\left[\left\|s_\theta(\alpha_t X_0+\sigma_t\epsilon,\,t) + \frac{\epsilon}{\sigma_t}\right\|^2\right]\right]\]

with weighting \(\lambda(t) = \sigma_t^2\) (Fisher-consistent weighting).

DDPM training objective (Ho et al. 2020). Reparameterize: \(\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t) = -\sigma_t s_\theta(x_t,t)\) (noise prediction network). With \(\lambda(t) = 1\):

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{DDPM}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{t\sim U\{1,\ldots,T\},\,X_0\sim p_{\text{data}},\,\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)}\!\left[\|\epsilon_\theta(\alpha_t X_0+\sigma_t\epsilon,\,t) - \epsilon\|^2\right]\]

Equivalence. \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{DDPM}} = \frac{1}{\sigma_t^2}\mathcal{L}_{\text{NCSN}}\) with the noise prediction reparameterization, confirming that DDPM is denoising score matching with the OU forward process and \(\epsilon\)-prediction parameterization.

Three equivalent parameterizations:

\(x_0\)-prediction: \(\hat x_0^\theta(x_t,t) = (x_t - \sigma_t\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t))/\alpha_t\)

\(\epsilon\)-prediction: \(\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t) = -\sigma_t s_\theta(x_t,t)\)

\(v\)-prediction (Salimans-Ho 2022): \(v_\theta(x_t,t) = \alpha_t\epsilon_\theta - \sigma_t\hat x_0^\theta = \alpha_t\epsilon - \sigma_t x_0\)

All three are linear reparameterizations of the score \(s_\theta\):

\[s_\theta(x_t,t) = -\frac{\epsilon_\theta}{\sigma_t} = \frac{\hat x_0^\theta - x_t/\alpha_t}{\sigma_t^2/\alpha_t} = \frac{-\sigma_t\hat x_0^\theta - \alpha_t v_\theta}{\sigma_t^2 + \alpha_t^2\sigma_t} \]

Reverse SDE and Sampling

Learned reverse SDE. Substituting \(s_\theta\approx\nabla\log\rho_t\) into the Anderson reverse SDE:

\[d\tilde X_t = \left[-b(\tilde X_t,T-t) + \sigma_{T-t}^2 s_\theta(\tilde X_t,T-t)\right]dt + \sigma_{T-t}\,d\tilde W_t\]

For the OU forward process with \(b(x,t) = -\frac{\beta_t}{2}x\) and \(\sigma_t = \sqrt{\beta_t}\):

\[d\tilde X_t = \left[\frac{\beta_{T-t}}{2}\tilde X_t + \beta_{T-t}s_\theta(\tilde X_t,T-t)\right]dt + \sqrt{\beta_{T-t}}\,d\tilde W_t\]

DDPM reverse sampler (discretization). Euler-Maruyama discretization with step \(\Delta t = 1/N\):

\[x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_t}}\!\left(x_t + \beta_t s_\theta(x_t,t)\right) + \sqrt{\beta_t}\,\epsilon_t, \quad \epsilon_t\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\]

equivalently with \(\epsilon\)-prediction:

\[x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}\!\left(x_t - \frac{1-\alpha_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\right) + \sigma_t\epsilon_t\]

where \(\bar\alpha_t = \prod_{s=1}^t\alpha_s\) and \(\alpha_t = 1-\beta_t\).

Probability flow ODE sampler (DDIM). Deterministic sampler from the probability flow ODE:

\[\frac{dx_t}{dt} = -\frac{\beta_t}{2}\!\left(x_t + s_\theta(x_t,t)\right)\]

discretized as:

\[x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\hat x_0^\theta(x_t,t) + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\]

This is DDIM (Song et al. 2020) — deterministic, uses the same learned \(\epsilon_\theta\) as DDPM but produces deterministic trajectories with fewer function evaluations.

Optimal Weighting and the Evidence Lower Bound

Theorem (Optimal weighting). The weighting \(\lambda^*(t) = \mathbb{E}[\|\nabla_x\log\rho_t(X_t)\|^2]^{-1}\cdot\sigma_t^2\) minimizes the variance of the training gradient over the noise schedule. In practice, \(\lambda(t) = \sigma_t^2\) (min-SNR weighting) or \(\lambda(t) = 1\) (DDPM) are used.

Connection to ELBO. The DDPM objective is a reweighted Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) for the variational model \(p_\theta\) with prior \(p_T = \mathcal{N}(0,I)\):

\[\log p_\theta(x_0) \geq -\mathcal{L}_{\text{DDPM}}(\theta) + C\]

Proof sketch. The ELBO for the latent variable model \(p_\theta(x_0) = \int p_\theta(x_0|x_1)\cdots p_\theta(x_{T-1}|x_T)p(x_T)\,dx_{1:T}\) factorizes as a sum of KL divergences between forward and backward conditional Gaussians, each of which reduces to the DDPM \(\epsilon\)-prediction MSE at each time step. \(\square\)

Classifier-Free Guidance

Definition. For a conditional score \(\nabla\log p(x|y)\) where \(y\) is a conditioning signal (class label, text prompt):

\[\nabla\log p(x|y) = \nabla\log p(y|x) + \nabla\log p(x)\]

Classifier guidance (Dhariwal-Nichol 2021). Use a separately trained classifier \(p_\phi(y|x_t)\):

\[\tilde s_\theta(x_t,t,y) = s_\theta(x_t,t) + w\nabla_{x_t}\log p_\phi(y|x_t)\]

Classifier-free guidance (Ho-Salimans 2022). Train a single network jointly on conditional and unconditional:

\[\tilde s_\theta(x_t,t,y) = s_\theta(x_t,t,\emptyset) + w\!\left(s_\theta(x_t,t,y) - s_\theta(x_t,t,\emptyset)\right)\]

where \(\emptyset\) indicates null conditioning (10-20% of training uses \(y=\emptyset\)). The guidance scale \(w > 1\) amplifies the conditional score, trading diversity for fidelity.

Geometric interpretation. The guided score \(\tilde s = s_\text{uncond} + w(s_\text{cond} - s_\text{uncond})\) is the extrapolation of the conditional score beyond the unconditional: it exaggerates the direction in which the conditioning signal increases density, pushing samples toward high-likelihood regions of the conditional distribution at the cost of reducing sample diversity.

WHY THIS MATTERS

In MATH 201 (Advanced Calculus), Hyvärinen’s theorem uses the divergence theorem \(\int s_\theta\cdot\nabla\rho\,dx = -\int(\nabla\cdot s_\theta)\rho\,dx\) — the integration by parts identity for vector fields in \(\mathbb{R}^d\), with boundary terms vanishing by the decay condition on \(\rho\|s_\theta\|\). This is the same integration by parts used to derive the Fokker-Planck equation from Itô’s formula: in both cases, moving a derivative from one factor to another via the divergence theorem eliminates an intractable quantity (the true score \(\nabla\log\rho\) in score matching; the expectation of \(\nabla\phi\) in the Fokker-Planck derivation). The divergence \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta = \text{tr}(\nabla_x s_\theta)\) is the trace of the Jacobian matrix from MATH 201’s multivariate calculus — the same object that appeared in the instantaneous change of variables formula of Topic 1 and in the Hutchinson estimator. The sliced score matching equivalence \(\mathbb{E}_v[v^\top\nabla_x s_\theta v] = \text{tr}(\nabla_x s_\theta)\) for \(\mathbb{E}[vv^\top]=I\) uses the trace-expectation identity from linear algebra — \(\text{tr}(AB) = \text{tr}(BA)\) and \(\mathbb{E}[v^\top Av] = \text{tr}(A\mathbb{E}[vv^\top])\) — applied to the Jacobian of \(s_\theta\).

In MATH 208 (Statistics and Probability), Tweedie’s formula \(\nabla\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = -(x-\mathbb{E}[X_0|X=x])/\sigma^2\) is the posterior mean formula for the Gaussian observation model \(X = X_0+\sigma\epsilon\), derived by Bayes’ theorem: \(p(x_0|x)\propto p_{\text{data}}(x_0)\mathcal{N}(x;x_0,\sigma^2)\), and the posterior mean \(\mathbb{E}[X_0|X=x] = \int x_0 p(x_0|x)dx_0\) appears in the score via differentiation under the integral sign. The DDPM loss \(\mathbb{E}[\|\epsilon_\theta(X_t,t)-\epsilon\|^2]\) is the MSE for predicting the noise \(\epsilon\) from the noisy observation \(X_t = \alpha_t X_0+\sigma_t\epsilon\) — a classical regression problem from MATH 208, where the optimal predictor is \(\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|X_t] = -\sigma_t\nabla\log\rho_t(X_t)\) by Tweedie’s formula. The signal-to-noise ratio \(\text{SNR}(t) = \alpha_t^2/\sigma_t^2\) from MATH 208’s signal processing perspective determines the difficulty of the denoising task at each noise level: high SNR (early time, little noise) makes denoising easy and the score estimate reliable; low SNR (late time, heavy noise) makes denoising hard and the score estimate unreliable.

In MSCS 202 (Numerical Methods), the Euler-Maruyama discretization of the reverse SDE — \(x_{t-1} = x_t + \Delta t\cdot\tilde b(x_t,t) + \sqrt{\Delta t}\cdot\sigma\epsilon_t\) — is the stochastic analogue of the explicit Euler method from MSCS 202, applied to the SDE rather than an ODE. The global error of the Euler-Maruyama method is \(O(\sqrt{\Delta t})\) in the strong sense (pathwise) and \(O(\Delta t)\) in the weak sense (distributional) — the distributional accuracy improving linearly with the number of steps, motivating the use of higher-order solvers (DPM-Solver, DEIS) that achieve \(O(\Delta t^k)\) weak accuracy for order \(k\). The DDIM discretization \(x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\hat x_0^\theta + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\epsilon_\theta\) is a second-order implicit solver for the probability flow ODE — it predicts \(\hat x_0^\theta\) (the clean signal) and interpolates toward the next noise level, achieving better accuracy per function evaluation than Euler-Maruyama.

Layer 3 (Bridge)

Every element of the Nepal Search and Rescue Coordination Centre story has a precise mathematical address. We walk through them completely — the survivor density, the local gradient observations, the normalizing constant problem, Sushil’s circular naive objective, Maya’s integration by parts elimination, the divergence bottleneck, the denoising equivalence, and the multi-scale break moment — until the story and the formalism are a single object seen from two angles.

The survivor distribution across the Marsyangdi search zone — the unknown density of survivors at each terrain point — is the data distribution \(p_{\text{data}}(x)\), an unnormalized density over the search space \(\mathbb{R}^d\) that cannot be evaluated pointwise from the available observations. The rescue operation has access only to samples — observed survivor locations from incomplete search coverage — not to the density function itself. In the generative modeling context, \(p_{\text{data}}\) is known only through its samples \(\{x_0^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^n\); the density \(p_{\text{data}}(x)\) at an arbitrary point \(x\) is intractable because it would require integrating over all possible data configurations. The normalizing constant \(Z = \int\rho(x)\,dx\) — the total survivor count in the rescue story — is the intractable quantity that makes direct density estimation impossible: computing \(Z\) requires integrating an unknown function over a high-dimensional space, which is as intractable as the original density estimation problem.

The gradient direction field — the rescue team’s local observation that survivor density is increasing toward the northwest at rate 3 survivors per kilometre, without knowing the absolute count — is the score function \(s(x) = \nabla_x\log\rho(x) = \nabla_x\rho(x)/\rho(x)\), the relative rate of change of the density at each point. The key property that makes the score tractable when the density is not: \(\nabla\log\rho = \nabla\rho/\rho\) cancels the normalizing constant. If \(\rho(x) = \tilde\rho(x)/Z\) for unnormalized \(\tilde\rho\) and intractable \(Z\), then \(\nabla\log\rho = \nabla\log\tilde\rho + \nabla\log Z^{-1} = \nabla\log\tilde\rho\) — the constant \(Z\) vanishes under the gradient. This is the fundamental reason score matching is tractable when density estimation is not: the score requires only the unnormalized density \(\tilde\rho\), whose gradient \(\nabla\tilde\rho\) is computable from the neural network parameterization. The rescue team’s directional observations are score function evaluations — noisy samples of \(\nabla\log\rho\) at observed survivor locations — and score matching is the procedure for learning the full direction field from these local observations.

Sushil’s circular naive objective \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{naive}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim\rho}[\|s_\theta(x)-\nabla\log\rho(x)\|^2]\) — requiring the true score \(\nabla\log\rho\) as a label for each training point — is the Fisher divergence \(\frac{1}{2}\mathcal{F}(\rho\|q_\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}_\rho[\|s_\theta - \nabla\log\rho\|^2]\), which is the natural \(L^2(\rho)\)-distance between the model score and the true score but requires knowledge of the true score as a training target. The circularity is precise: minimizing \(\mathcal{F}(\rho\|q_\theta)\) requires evaluating \(\nabla\log\rho(x)\) at each training point \(x\sim\rho\), but \(\nabla\log\rho(x) = \nabla_x\log\int p_{\text{data}}(x_0)\mathcal{N}(x;\alpha_t x_0,\sigma_t^2)dx_0\) requires integrating over all data points — a sum over the entire training dataset at each gradient step, reducing to \(O(n\cdot d)\) computation per step for \(n\) training samples in \(d\) dimensions. For ImageNet with \(n = 1.2\times 10^6\) samples and \(d = 256\times 256\times 3\approx 2\times 10^5\) dimensions, this is \(2.4\times 10^{11}\) operations per gradient step — completely intractable. The rescue team analogy: to compute Sushil’s training labels, you would need to know the exact survivor location at every possible terrain point simultaneously — the very information you are trying to learn.

Maya’s integration by parts insight — converting the cross-term \(\mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho]\) to \(-\mathbb{E}_\rho[\nabla\cdot s_\theta]\) by the divergence theorem — is Hyvärinen’s theorem, proved by applying \(\int s_\theta\cdot\nabla\rho\,dx = -\int(\nabla\cdot s_\theta)\rho\,dx\) with vanishing boundary terms. The proof walks precisely as follows. The cross-term is \(\mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho] = \int s_\theta(x)\cdot\nabla\rho(x)\,dx\) — using \(\nabla\log\rho\cdot\rho = \nabla\rho\), so the \(\rho\) in the expectation and the \(1/\rho\) in \(\nabla\log\rho\) cancel. The divergence theorem in \(\mathbb{R}^d\): \(\int_\Omega s_\theta\cdot\nabla\rho\,dx = -\int_\Omega(\nabla\cdot s_\theta)\rho\,dx + \oint_{\partial\Omega}(s_\theta\cdot n)\rho\,dS\). As \(\Omega\to\mathbb{R}^d\): the boundary integral \(\oint_{\partial\Omega}(s_\theta\cdot n)\rho\,dS\to 0\) because \(\rho(x)\|s_\theta(x)\|\to 0\) as \(\|x\|\to\infty\) — a reasonable assumption for well-trained networks on data distributions that decay at infinity. The result \(\mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho] = -\mathbb{E}_\rho[\nabla\cdot s_\theta]\) substitutes into the expanded Fisher divergence: \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}} = \mathbb{E}_\rho[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta\|^2] - \mathbb{E}_\rho[s_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\rho] + C = \mathbb{E}_\rho[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta\|^2 + \nabla\cdot s_\theta] + C = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ISM}} + C\). The Hyvärinen objective \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ISM}} = \mathbb{E}_\rho[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta\|^2 + \nabla\cdot s_\theta]\) contains no \(\nabla\log\rho\) — the intractable ground truth score has been completely eliminated by one application of the divergence theorem from MATH 201.

The two-term interpretation of the Hyvärinen objective — \(\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta(x)\|^2\) penalizing large predicted gradient magnitudes and \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x)\) encouraging inward-pointing score fields at observed locations — is the balance between regularization and data fidelity encoded in the score matching objective. The term \(\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta\|^2\) is the squared \(L^2\)-norm of the predicted score field at the data point \(x\): it penalizes the network for confidently predicting strong gradients at observed locations, acting as a form of score regularization. The term \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x) = \sum_i\partial s_\theta^i/\partial x_i\) is the divergence of the score field — positive when the score spreads outward (density minimum at \(x\)), negative when the score converges inward (density maximum at \(x\)). Minimizing \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta\) at observed data points pushes the score to converge inward at those points — the correct behavior for a score field near a density peak, where \(\nabla\log\rho\) points inward from all directions toward the mode. At the optimum, these two terms balance to reproduce \(\nabla\log\rho\) exactly — the network predicts gradients of the magnitude that the density gradient field demands, neither too large nor too small.

The divergence bottleneck — computing \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x) = \sum_{i=1}^d\partial s_\theta^i/\partial x_i\) requiring \(d\) Jacobian-vector products at \(O(d)\) cost — is the same Hutchinson trace bottleneck as in Topic 1’s instantaneous change of variables formula, resolved by sliced score matching. The sliced score matching objective \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{SSM}} = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim\rho,v\sim p_v}[\frac{1}{2}(v^\top s_\theta)^2 + v^\top\nabla_x(v^\top s_\theta)]\) is equivalent to \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ISM}}\) by the trace identity \(\text{tr}(A) = \mathbb{E}_v[v^\top Av]\) for \(\mathbb{E}[vv^\top]=I\): \(\mathbb{E}_v[v^\top\nabla_x s_\theta\cdot v] = \text{tr}(\nabla_x s_\theta\cdot\mathbb{E}[vv^\top]) = \text{tr}(\nabla_x s_\theta) = \nabla\cdot s_\theta\). The scalar \(v^\top s_\theta\) requires only one forward pass; its gradient \(\nabla_x(v^\top s_\theta) = \nabla_x s_\theta\cdot v\) requires one backward pass at cost \(O(p)\) — the same Hutchinson estimator that reduced the FFJORD divergence from \(O(d^2)\) to \(O(p)\). The rescue analogy: instead of asking every rescue team member independently which direction survivor density increases (measuring all \(d\) components of \(\nabla s_\theta\)), project onto a random direction \(v\) and measure only that one scalar projection — an unbiased estimate of the divergence at \(O(1/d)\) of the cost.

The denoising breakthrough — observing that the score of the smoothed distribution at a noisy location \(x = x_0+\sigma\epsilon\) equals \(-\epsilon/\sigma\), the known noise direction pointing back toward the clean signal — is Tweedie’s formula \(\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = -(x-\mathbb{E}[X_0|X=x])/\sigma^2\), derived by differentiating the Gaussian mixture \(\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = \int p_{\text{data}}(x_0)\mathcal{N}(x;x_0,\sigma^2)dx_0\) under the integral sign. The derivation proceeds: \(\nabla_x\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = \int p_{\text{data}}(x_0)\nabla_x\mathcal{N}(x;x_0,\sigma^2)dx_0 = \int p_{\text{data}}(x_0)\frac{-(x-x_0)}{\sigma^2}\mathcal{N}(x;x_0,\sigma^2)dx_0\). Dividing by \(\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)\): \(\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x) = -\frac{x-\int x_0 p(x_0|x)dx_0}{\sigma^2} = -\frac{x-\mathbb{E}[X_0|X=x]}{\sigma^2}\). Since \(X = X_0+\sigma\epsilon\) gives \(\mathbb{E}[X_0|X=x] = x - \sigma\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|X=x]\), the score becomes \(-\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|X=x]/\sigma\). The optimal score estimator \(s^*(x,\sigma) = -\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|X=x]/\sigma\) requires predicting the expected noise given the noisy observation — a denoising problem, not a density estimation problem. The rescue analogy: instead of estimating the survivor density gradient at each location (the score), ask a simpler question: given a noisy observation \(x\) of a survivor location displaced by \(\sigma\epsilon\), what direction points back toward the true location? The answer \(-\epsilon/\sigma\) is known because you generated the noisy observation yourself by adding \(\sigma\epsilon\) to the true location \(x_0\) — the forward noising process has created training labels for free.

Vincent’s equivalence theorem — the denoising score matching objective \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}}(\theta,\sigma) = \mathbb{E}_{x_0,\epsilon}[\|s_\theta(x_0+\sigma\epsilon,\sigma)+\epsilon/\sigma\|^2]\) is equivalent to the implicit score matching objective \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ISM}}(\theta)\) for the smoothed density \(\tilde\rho_\sigma\) — is the bridge between the simple regression problem and the principled score estimation framework. The proof shows \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}}^{\tilde\rho_\sigma} + C\) by expanding \(\|s_\theta + \epsilon/\sigma\|^2 = \|s_\theta - \nabla\log\tilde\rho_\sigma\|^2 + \|\nabla\log\tilde\rho_\sigma + \epsilon/\sigma\|^2 + 2(s_\theta-\nabla\log\tilde\rho_\sigma)\cdot(\nabla\log\tilde\rho_\sigma+\epsilon/\sigma)\): the last term vanishes in expectation because \(\mathbb{E}[\epsilon/\sigma|X=x] = -\nabla_x\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)\) by Tweedie’s formula, and the middle term is a constant in \(\theta\). The practical consequence: training with the denoising objective \(\mathbb{E}[\|s_\theta(x_0+\sigma\epsilon)+\epsilon/\sigma\|^2]\) — a simple MSE regression on pairs \((x_0+\sigma\epsilon, -\epsilon/\sigma)\) — is guaranteed to minimize the Fisher divergence between \(s_\theta(\cdot,\sigma)\) and \(\nabla\log\tilde\rho_\sigma(\cdot)\). No Jacobian, no divergence computation, no Hutchinson estimator needed. The rescue analogy: training the rescue coordination network to predict the direction back to the true survivor location from each noisy observation is equivalent to learning the score of the smoothed survivor distribution — both objectives have the same minimum.

The break moment — one noise level \(\sigma\) is insufficient for the full generative model — is the formal statement that the score \(\nabla\log\tilde\rho_\sigma\) for a single \(\sigma\) cannot drive a sampling procedure that traverses from pure Gaussian noise to the data distribution, because the score at large \(\sigma\) has no fine-grained information about the data manifold, and the score at small \(\sigma\) is unreliable away from data points due to data sparsity in high dimensions. At large \(\sigma\): \(\tilde\rho_\sigma\approx\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I)\) for any bounded \(p_{\text{data}}\) — the smoothed distribution is nearly Gaussian and the score \(\nabla\log\tilde\rho_\sigma\approx -x/\sigma^2\) is the Gaussian score, containing no information distinguishing \(p_{\text{data}}\) from a Gaussian. The rescue analogy: with 5-kilometre search radius noise, every survivor observation looks like a point in the center of the valley — no fine-grained structure is visible. At small \(\sigma\): \(\tilde\rho_\sigma\) approximates \(p_{\text{data}}\) closely, but for most points \(x\) in the high-dimensional space \(\mathbb{R}^d\), the nearest data point is far away and \(\tilde\rho_\sigma(x)\approx 0\), making the score numerically unstable. The rescue analogy: with 50-metre search radius, most terrain points have zero observed survivors nearby, giving no information about the score direction. The multi-scale solution: train the noise-conditional score network \(s_\theta(x,t)\) simultaneously on all noise levels \(t\in[0,T]\), with large-\(t\) scores providing reliable coarse structure and small-\(t\) scores providing fine-grained data geometry. The NCSN weighting \(\lambda(t) = \sigma_t^2\) equalizes the signal magnitude across noise levels — at large \(\sigma_t\), the true score \(\nabla\log\rho_t\approx -x/\sigma_t^2\) has small magnitude \(\|s\|\approx 1/\sigma_t\), so multiplying the loss by \(\sigma_t^2\) restores the contribution of each noise level to a comparable scale.

The DDPM training objective \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{DDPM}} = \mathbb{E}_{t,X_0,\epsilon}[\|\epsilon_\theta(\alpha_t X_0+\sigma_t\epsilon,t)-\epsilon\|^2]\) — predicting the noise added in the forward OU process — is the denoising score matching objective with the \(\epsilon\)-parameterization \(\epsilon_\theta = -\sigma_t s_\theta\) and unit weighting \(\lambda(t) = 1\), equivalent to Fisher-weighted score matching with \(\lambda(t) = \sigma_t^2\) up to reparameterization. The three equivalent parameterizations — \(x_0\)-prediction, \(\epsilon\)-prediction, and \(v\)-prediction — are all linear reparameterizations of the same underlying score function \(s_\theta(x_t,t) = \nabla_{x_t}\log\rho_t(x_t)\). The \(\epsilon\)-prediction \(\epsilon_\theta = -\sigma_t s_\theta\) replaces the score with the noise prediction, scaling by \(\sigma_t\) to make the prediction dimensionless. The \(x_0\)-prediction \(\hat x_0^\theta = (x_t-\sigma_t\epsilon_\theta)/\alpha_t = x_t/\alpha_t + \sigma_t^2 s_\theta/\alpha_t\) is the Tweedie denoiser — the posterior mean \(\mathbb{E}[X_0|X_t=x_t]\) reconstructed from the score via Tweedie’s formula. The \(v\)-prediction \(v_\theta = \alpha_t\epsilon-\sigma_t x_0\) is the velocity along the interpolation path from \(x_0\) to \(\epsilon\) — a numerically stable parameterization at intermediate noise levels where neither \(\epsilon\)-prediction (noisy at low noise) nor \(x_0\)-prediction (noisy at high noise) is well-conditioned. All three train the same score function; the choice of parameterization affects only the numerical conditioning of the training gradient.

The DDPM reverse sampler \(x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_t}}(x_t+\beta_t s_\theta(x_t,t)) + \sqrt{\beta_t}\epsilon_t\) — the Euler-Maruyama discretization of the Anderson reverse SDE with the learned score substituted — is the stochastic sampling procedure whose distributional accuracy is \(O(\sqrt{\Delta t})\) strong and \(O(\Delta t)\) weak in the step size \(\Delta t = \beta_t\). The DDIM sampler \(x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\hat x_0^\theta(x_t,t)+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\) is the Euler discretization of the probability flow ODE — the same probability flow ODE derived in Topic 1, now with the score replaced by the learned \(s_\theta\), giving deterministic generation trajectories. The accuracy-speed trade-off: DDPM uses \(T=1000\) steps for high-quality stochastic sampling; DDIM uses \(T=20\)-\(50\) steps for deterministic sampling at comparable quality, because the ODE trajectories are smoother than the SDE trajectories and require fewer evaluation points for the same numerical accuracy. The higher-order solvers DPM-Solver and DEIS achieve \(O(\Delta t^k)\) weak accuracy at order \(k\), further reducing the step count to \(T=5\)-\(20\) while maintaining sample quality.

The classifier-free guidance score \(\tilde s_\theta(x_t,t,y) = s_\theta(x_t,t,\emptyset) + w(s_\theta(x_t,t,y)-s_\theta(x_t,t,\emptyset))\) — the extrapolation of the conditional score beyond the unconditional score — is the geometric interpolation in score space that amplifies the gradient direction toward high-likelihood regions of the conditional distribution \(p(x|y)\). The decomposition \(\nabla\log p(x|y) = \nabla\log p(y|x) + \nabla\log p(x)\) — Bayes’ theorem differentiated — shows that the conditional score equals the unconditional score plus the classifier gradient \(\nabla\log p(y|x)\). Classifier-free guidance approximates this decomposition without a separate classifier by training a single network on both conditional (\(y\) given) and unconditional (\(y=\emptyset\)) inputs, then extrapolating: \(\tilde s = s_\text{uncond} + w(s_\text{cond}-s_\text{uncond})\) for \(w>1\) amplifies the difference \(s_\text{cond}-s_\text{uncond}\approx\nabla\log p(y|x)\) by factor \(w\), pushing samples toward regions of high \(p(y|x)\) — high fidelity to the conditioning signal — at the cost of reduced diversity. The rescue analogy: instead of searching wherever survivor density is highest (unconditional), search where survivor density is highest given a specific constraint (conditional on being near a water source), amplified by \(w\) to prioritize the constraint strongly.
