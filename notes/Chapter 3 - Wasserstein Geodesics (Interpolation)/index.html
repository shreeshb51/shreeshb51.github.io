Chapter 3 - Wasserstein Geodesics: The Geometry of Interpolation

Introduction

Chapters 1 and 2 answered two questions. Chapter 1 answered the theoretical question: what is the right way to measure the distance between two probability distributions, and what is the mathematical structure of the optimal transport plan that achieves this distance? Chapter 2 answered the computational question: how do you calculate this distance efficiently, stably, and differentiably at machine learning scale? You now have both the Wasserstein distance as a geometric object and the Sinkhorn algorithm as the engine that computes it.

Chapter 3 asks a third question, which is in some ways the deepest of the three: given two probability distributions \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) at Wasserstein distance \(W_2(\mu, \nu)\) apart, what does the straight-line path between them look like?

This question sounds geometric, and it is — but its answer is not obvious, and the naive answer is badly wrong. The naive answer is: interpolate linearly between the density functions. Average the pixels. Mix the histograms. Take the weighted mean \(t\mu + (1-t)\nu\) for \(t \in [0,1]\). This is the Euclidean interpolation — the straight line in the space of functions — and it produces blurry, physically meaningless intermediate distributions. Two Gaussians separated by a large distance, interpolated linearly, produce a bimodal distribution for all intermediate \(t\) that never resembles either Gaussian. The mass does not move — it duplicates and fades.

The correct interpolation is the displacement interpolation — the path along which mass actually moves from \(\mu\) to \(\nu\) following the optimal transport plan, each grain of mass traveling in a straight line from its source to its destination, all grains moving simultaneously at constant velocity. The intermediate distributions along this path are the pushforwards of \(\mu\) under the maps \(T_t = (1-t)\text{id} + tT^*\), where \(T^*\) is the Brenier map from \(\mu\) to \(\nu\). At \(t=0\) you have \(\mu\). At \(t=1\) you have \(\nu\). At intermediate \(t\), you have a distribution that genuinely interpolates the geometric structure of \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) — a Gaussian between two Gaussians, a crescent between a circle and a crescent, a face between two faces.

This path is a geodesic — the shortest path between \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) in the Wasserstein metric space. The space of probability distributions equipped with the \(W_2\) distance is an infinite-dimensional metric space with a rich geometric structure, and the displacement interpolation is its notion of a straight line. This space is called the Wasserstein space, and understanding its geometry — its geodesics, its notion of curvature, its Riemannian structure — is the subject of Chapter 3.

The chapter has two topics corresponding to the two bullet points of the roadmap’s syllabus.

The first topic is Displacement Interpolation — the precise construction of the geodesic between two distributions in Wasserstein space. The displacement interpolation is defined via the optimal coupling: given the optimal coupling \(\pi^*\) between \(\mu\) and \(\nu\), the interpolated distribution at time \(t\) is the pushforward of \(\pi^*\) under the map \((x,y) \mapsto (1-t)x + ty\). For quadratic cost with absolutely continuous \(\mu\), the optimal coupling is the Brenier map \(T^*\), and the interpolation simplifies to the pushforward of \(\mu\) under \(T_t = (1-t)\text{id} + tT^*\). The key properties — that the path has constant speed, that its length equals \(W_2(\mu,\nu)\), and that it is the unique geodesic when \(\mu\) is absolutely continuous — follow from the Riemannian structure of Wasserstein space established by Otto’s calculus. The displacement interpolation is not merely a mathematical curiosity: it is the geometric foundation of every morphing, interpolation, and data augmentation operation in generative AI that respects the underlying geometry of the data space.

The second topic is Wasserstein Barycenters — the extension of displacement interpolation from two distributions to finitely many. Given distributions \(\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_k\) with weights \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k\) summing to one, the Wasserstein barycenter is the distribution \(\mu^*\) that minimizes the weighted sum of squared Wasserstein distances \(\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i W_2^2(\mu^*, \mu_i)\). This is the Fréchet mean in Wasserstein space — the generalization of the arithmetic mean to the curved geometry of the Wasserstein metric. The barycenter captures the geometric average of multiple distributions simultaneously: the average shape of a collection of organs in medical imaging, the average style of a collection of images in style transfer, the average molecular conformation of a protein ensemble in computational biology. Computing the barycenter requires iterating the Sinkhorn algorithm across multiple marginals simultaneously — a direct algorithmic generalization of Chapter 2 — and the fixed-point equations that characterize the barycenter are the Wasserstein-space analogue of the normal equations characterizing the Euclidean mean.

By the end of Chapter 3, the Wasserstein distance will no longer be just a number. It will be the organizing principle of a full geometric space — a space with straight lines, averages, and a notion of curvature — and you will understand why every modern generative model that claims to respect data geometry is implicitly working in this space. The displacement interpolation is the geometric operation that separates models that move mass intelligently from models that blur it naively, and the Wasserstein barycenter is the averaging operation that separates geometry-aware aggregation from geometry-blind mixing. Phase 1 ends here. Phase 2 builds the neural architectures that operate on this geometric foundation.
