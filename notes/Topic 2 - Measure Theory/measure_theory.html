    Topic 2 - Measure Theory

    Introduction

    You have now built a solid understanding of what it means for a space to have a notion of distance — metric spaces gave you that. You know what it means for a set to be open or closed, for a space to have no escape routes, and for a space to have no holes. That is the geometry of spaces. But the roadmap has a different and deeper question waiting: what does it mean to assign a size to a subset of a space?

    This question sounds almost trivially simple at first. You have been computing areas and volumes since secondary school. The area of a rectangle is length times width. The volume of a sphere is \(\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\). What could possibly go wrong?

    What goes wrong is probability. The moment you try to assign probabilities to events in a continuous space — not a die with six faces, but a random variable that can take any real value — you are no longer counting discrete outcomes. You are assigning sizes to subsets of the real line, or of \(\mathbb{R}^n\), or of some even stranger space. And the moment you try to do that naively, without any formal framework, you run into subsets so pathological that no coherent notion of size can be assigned to them at all. They are the mathematical equivalent of regions of land whose boundaries are so jagged and infinite that no ruler can measure them — the very problem raised in the Chapter 1 introduction.

    Measure Theory is the framework that resolves this crisis completely. It does so by making a careful, deliberate decision at the very beginning: rather than trying to assign sizes to every possible subset — which turns out to be impossible without contradiction — it identifies precisely which subsets are allowed to have a size, and it specifies the rules that any coherent notion of size must obey. The allowed subsets are organized into a structure called a \(\sigma\)-algebra. The coherent notion of size is called a measure. And the particular measure that assigns sizes consistent with our geometric intuition of length, area, and volume is called the Lebesgue measure.

    This topic has four subtopics that build on each other in a strict logical order, and each one closes a gap that the previous one opens.

    The first two subtopics, \(\sigma\)-algebras and Borel sets, answers the question: which subsets of a space are allowed to have a size assigned to them? The answer is not “all of them” — and understanding why not is the first conceptual breakthrough.

    The second subtopic, Lebesgue measure, answers the question: once you have decided which subsets are allowed, how do you actually assign sizes to them in a way that is consistent, general, and more powerful than the Riemann integration you used in MATH 201?

    The third subtopic, Lebesgue vs. Riemann integration, sharpens the contrast between the old framework you already know and the new one being built. It reveals exactly where Riemann integration silently fails, and why the Lebesgue integral is the right tool for probability theory and modern analysis.

    The fourth subtopic, the Radon-Nikodym theorem, is the payoff. It answers the question: when can one measure be expressed as a rescaling of another, and what does that rescaling look like? The answer is the Radon-Nikodym derivative \(\frac{dQ}{dP}\), which is the rigorous foundation of conditional probability, likelihood ratios, and the density functions that appear in every generative model on the roadmap.

    Taken together, these four subtopics transform your understanding of probability from a counting exercise into a geometric one. By the end of Topic 2, when someone writes \(\mathbb{P}(X \in A) = \int_A p(x) \, d\mu(x)\), you will not see a formula — you will see mass being summed over an allowed region of a space, according to rules that were carefully designed to avoid every pathological failure that naive intuition would stumble into.
