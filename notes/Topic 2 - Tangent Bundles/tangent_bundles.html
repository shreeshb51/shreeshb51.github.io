    Topic 2 - Tangent Bundles

    Introduction

    You have just built the precise language for describing curved spaces — the atlas of overlapping charts, the smooth transition maps, the diffeomorphisms that preserve the smooth structure. You know what a manifold is, you know how to do calculus on it through coordinate representations, and you know that the smooth map is the correct notion of a function between manifolds.

    What you do not yet have is a notion of direction.

    On a flat Euclidean space, direction is simple. A vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is an arrow — a magnitude and a direction — and it lives in the same space as the points it connects. You can add vectors, scale them, compare vectors at different points, and transport a vector from one location to another without ambiguity. The vector space structure of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is global and uniform: the same vector space lives at every point, and vectors at different points are directly comparable.

    On a curved manifold, this global uniformity breaks down. Consider the surface of the Earth. At Kathmandu, the direction “due north” points toward the Himalayan crest. At the equator, “due north” points in a completely different direction in three-dimensional space, even though both are locally the same concept — the direction of increasing latitude. Vectors at different points on a curved manifold live in different spaces — the tangent plane at Kathmandu is a different plane than the tangent plane at the equator — and comparing them directly, without specifying how to transport one to the other, is geometrically meaningless. On a curved surface, direction is local, not global.

    The tangent bundle is the mathematical structure that makes this precise. At each point \(p\) of the manifold \(M\), there is a tangent space \(T_pM\) — the vector space of all directions of motion through \(p\), the flat space that best approximates the manifold near \(p\). The tangent space at \(p\) is the set of all velocity vectors of smooth curves passing through \(p\). It is an \(n\)-dimensional vector space — the same dimension as the manifold — and it is the correct arena for derivatives, gradients, and directional derivatives at \(p\).

    The tangent bundle \(TM\) is the disjoint union of all tangent spaces: \(TM = \coprod_{p \in M} T_pM\). It is not merely a set — it is itself a smooth manifold of dimension \(2n\), equipped with a projection map \(\pi: TM \to M\) that sends each tangent vector to its base point. The tangent bundle packages all the local vector spaces into a single global geometric object, and its smooth structure is what allows vector fields — assignments of a tangent vector to every point of the manifold — to be smooth in a well-defined sense.

    The roadmap labels this topic with a precise description: vectors \(v \in T_xM\) are velocity, and the manifold \(M\) is position. This distinction is not cosmetic. In every dynamical system, ODE, flow, and diffusion process that appears in Phases 1 through 3, there are two separate geometric objects: the state of the system — a point on the manifold — and the rate of change of the state — a tangent vector at that point. The state lives on \(M\). The velocity lives in \(T_pM\). The equation of motion — whether it is a neural ODE, a Fokker-Planck equation, a geodesic equation, or a score-based diffusion — is a relationship between positions on \(M\) and velocities in \(TM\). Without the tangent bundle, you cannot write these equations correctly on a curved space.

    This topic has two subtopics that build the tangent bundle from the ground up.

    The first subtopic is Tangent Vectors and Tangent Spaces — what a tangent vector actually is at a point on a smooth manifold, how the tangent space \(T_pM\) is defined intrinsically without reference to any ambient Euclidean space, and how the coordinate basis vectors \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}|_p\) provide a concrete basis for \(T_pM\) in any chart. The definition of a tangent vector as a derivation — a linear functional on smooth functions satisfying the Leibniz rule — is the intrinsic, coordinate-free definition that makes tangent vectors genuine geometric objects rather than artifacts of a particular coordinate system.

    The second subtopic is The Tangent Bundle and Vector Fields — how the disjoint union of all tangent spaces assembles into a smooth \(2n\)-dimensional manifold \(TM\), how the projection \(\pi: TM \to M\) makes \(TM\) a vector bundle over \(M\), and how smooth vector fields are the smooth sections of this bundle. A smooth vector field is an assignment \(X: M \to TM\) with \(\pi \circ X = \text{id}_M\) — it assigns to each point \(p\) a tangent vector \(X(p) \in T_pM\), smoothly varying from point to point. Vector fields are the velocity fields of flows — the dynamical systems on manifolds — and they are the central objects of every differential equation on a manifold.

    By the end of this topic, when you see a neural ODE written as \(\frac{dx}{dt} = f_\theta(x, t)\), you will understand exactly what this means on a manifold: \(x(t)\) is a smooth curve on \(M\), its velocity \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) is a tangent vector in \(T_{x(t)}M\) at each time \(t\), and \(f_\theta(\cdot, t)\) is a time-dependent vector field on \(M\) — a smooth section of \(TM\) for each \(t\). The neural ODE is an equation in the tangent bundle, matching positions to velocities, and its solution is the integral curve of the vector field. The tangent bundle is not the background scenery of Phase 3 — it is the stage on which every dynamical generative model performs.

    ALL TWO SUBTOPICS ARE TREATED AS A SINGLE TOPIC FOR EASY COMPREHENSION.

    Layer 1 (Story)

    You are a rafting guide on the Trishuli River, leading a group of kayakers through the rapids between Charaudi and Fishling. The Trishuli is not a gentle river. It cuts through gorges, swirls in eddies, accelerates through narrows, and fans out across broad shallow sections. At every point along the river, the water is moving — and at each point it is moving in a specific direction at a specific speed. Your job as guide is not just to know where you are on the river — your position — but to understand, at each position, how the water is moving — the velocity. Position and velocity are two separate pieces of information, and you need both simultaneously to navigate safely.

    Now suppose a colleague asks you to compare the current at two different points on the river. At the Charaudi entry point, the water is moving roughly northeast, at about 3 metres per second. At the Fishling takeout, the water is moving roughly east-southeast, at about 1 metre per second. Your colleague asks: is the velocity at Charaudi the same as at Fishling?

    The question is subtler than it appears. The speeds are different. The directions are different. But more fundamentally, the two velocities are vectors at two different points on the river’s surface — and the river’s surface is not flat. It curves through the gorge, follows the valley’s contours, bends around rock faces. The tangent plane to the river’s surface at Charaudi — the flat space that locally approximates the water surface there — is a different plane than the tangent plane at Fishling. Comparing a vector in one tangent plane directly with a vector in another tangent plane is like comparing apples and oranges: the two vectors live in different spaces, and direct comparison requires a rule for transporting one to the other.

    This is the foundational insight of the tangent bundle: at every point on a curved surface, velocity is a local concept. It lives in the tangent space at that point — the flat plane that best approximates the surface locally — not in some global vector space shared by all points.

    Let us build the picture carefully, starting with the simplest possible question.

    What is a velocity vector on a curved surface?

    Imagine a kayaker paddling a specific route through the Trishuli — a smooth path \(\gamma(t)\) on the river’s surface, parameterized by time \(t\). At time \(t = 0\), the kayaker is at position \(p = \gamma(0)\) on the surface. The kayaker is moving — the position \(\gamma(t)\) is changing with time. The velocity of the kayaker at time \(t = 0\) is the rate at which the position is changing — and this rate of change is a vector.

    But what kind of vector? Not a vector in three-dimensional ambient space pointing off the surface. Not a vector in some abstract global coordinate system. The velocity is tangent to the surface — it points along the surface, in the direction the kayaker is actually moving, staying within the two-dimensional world of the river’s surface. At the point \(p = \gamma(0)\), the velocity \(\gamma'(0)\) is a vector in the tangent plane to the surface at \(p\) — the flat two-dimensional plane that best approximates the river’s surface near the point \(p\).

    This tangent plane is the tangent space \(T_pM\) at the point \(p\). It is the set of all possible velocity vectors of smooth curves through \(p\) — all possible directions of motion along the surface at \(p\). For a two-dimensional surface, \(T_pM\) is a two-dimensional vector space. For an \(n\)-dimensional manifold, \(T_pM\) is an \(n\)-dimensional vector space.

    The coordinate basis — the two natural directions at each point

    Your kayaking guide uses a local chart — a flat coordinate map of the Trishuli gorge. In this chart, every point on the river’s surface has coordinates \((x^1, x^2)\) — say, distance along the river and distance across the river. The two coordinate functions \(x^1\) and \(x^2\) give two natural directions at every point: the direction of increasing \(x^1\) holding \(x^2\) fixed, and the direction of increasing \(x^2\) holding \(x^1\) fixed.

    These two directions are the coordinate basis vectors \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^1}\big|_p\) and \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^2}\big|_p\) of the tangent space \(T_pM\) at the point \(p\). They are the velocity vectors of the coordinate curves through \(p\) — the curves \(t \mapsto \phi^{-1}(x^1_0 + t, x^2_0)\) and \(t \mapsto \phi^{-1}(x^1_0, x^2_0 + t)\) that move in the two coordinate directions from \(p\).

    Any velocity vector \(v \in T_pM\) — any direction of motion at \(p\) — can be written as a linear combination of these two basis vectors:

    \[v = v^1 \frac{\partial}{\partial x^1}\bigg|_p + v^2 \frac{\partial}{\partial x^2}\bigg|_p\]

    The components \((v^1, v^2)\) are the coordinates of the velocity vector in the chart’s basis. They tell you how fast the kayaker is moving in the along-river direction and how fast in the across-river direction. Different charts give different components for the same velocity vector — just as the same wind direction in Nepal looks different when described in terms of compass coordinates versus valley-aligned coordinates — but the velocity vector itself is the same geometric object regardless of which chart is used to express it.

    The tangent bundle — assembling all tangent spaces into a single space

    Now your guide company wants to describe the entire current field of the Trishuli — not just the velocity at one point, but the velocity at every point simultaneously. At each point \(p\) on the river’s surface, there is a velocity vector \(X(p) \in T_pM\). The full collection of all these velocity vectors — one tangent vector at every point — is a vector field on the manifold.

    But where does a vector field live? Each velocity vector \(X(p)\) lives in the tangent space \(T_pM\) at its own point \(p\). Different tangent spaces are different vector spaces. A vector field is simultaneously a point on the manifold (the position \(p\)) and a vector in the tangent space at that point (the velocity \(X(p)\)). It lives neither on \(M\) alone nor in a single tangent space alone — it lives in the space that combines all tangent spaces together.

    This combined space is the tangent bundle \(TM\). It is the disjoint union of all tangent spaces:

    \[TM = \bigsqcup_{p \in M} T_pM\]

    A point of \(TM\) is a pair \((p, v)\) where \(p \in M\) is a base point and \(v \in T_pM\) is a tangent vector at \(p\). The tangent bundle \(TM\) is the space of all position-velocity pairs. For a two-dimensional river surface, \(TM\) is a four-dimensional space: two dimensions for position \((x^1, x^2)\) and two dimensions for velocity \((v^1, v^2)\).

    The projection map \(\pi: TM \to M\) sends each pair \((p, v)\) to its base point \(p\): \(\pi(p, v) = p\). The projection forgets the velocity and remembers only the position. The fiber over a point \(p\) — the set \(\pi^{-1}(p) = T_pM\) — is the tangent space at \(p\), the set of all velocity vectors at that position. The tangent bundle is a bundle of vector spaces over the manifold: over each point \(p \in M\), the fiber is the \(n\)-dimensional vector space \(T_pM\).

    The current field — the river’s velocity at every point — is a vector field on the manifold

    The Trishuli’s current is a perfect example of a vector field. At every point \(p\) on the river’s surface, the water has a velocity — a direction and a speed of flow. This velocity is a tangent vector \(X(p) \in T_pM\). The assignment \(p \mapsto X(p)\) — a tangent vector at every point — is the vector field of the current.

    For the current to be physically reasonable — for the velocity to vary smoothly across the river’s surface without sudden jumps — the vector field must be smooth: the map \(p \mapsto X(p)\) from the manifold to the tangent bundle must be a smooth map. Smoothness of the vector field means that in every local chart, the component functions \((v^1(p), v^2(p))\) are smooth functions of the coordinates \((x^1, x^2)\). The current changes direction and speed gradually — there are no discontinuous jumps in velocity from one point to the next.

    A smooth vector field on \(M\) is a smooth map \(X: M \to TM\) satisfying \(\pi \circ X = \text{id}_M\) — meaning the velocity at every point is indeed a tangent vector at that point, not a tangent vector from some other point. The condition \(\pi \circ X = \text{id}_M\) is the section condition: \(X\) is a smooth section of the tangent bundle. The river current satisfies this: at each point, the water’s velocity is a vector in the tangent space at that point, not floating free in some ambient space.

    Integral curves — following the current from any starting point

    Given the current field \(X\) on the Trishuli, what path does a kayaker follow if they stop paddling and simply drift with the current? Starting from a point \(p_0\) on the river, the kayaker moves in the direction \(X(p_0)\) at speed \(\|X(p_0)\|\). After an instant \(dt\), they are at a new point \(p_1 \approx p_0 + X(p_0) \, dt\). From \(p_1\), they follow the current direction \(X(p_1)\). And so on.

    The resulting path \(\gamma: (-\epsilon, \epsilon) \to M\) is the integral curve of the vector field \(X\) starting at \(p_0\). It satisfies the ODE:

    \[\gamma'(t) = X(\gamma(t)), \quad \gamma(0) = p_0\]

    The integral curve is the path that exactly follows the current — the path whose velocity at every moment equals the vector field at the current position. The existence and uniqueness of integral curves — the guarantee that from any starting point \(p_0\), there is exactly one path that follows the current exactly — is the ODE existence and uniqueness theorem applied to the coordinate expression of the vector field. On a smooth manifold with a smooth vector field, integral curves always exist locally and are unique.

    The integral curves of the current are the paths that drifting kayakers follow. The integral curves of a neural ODE’s velocity field are the paths that noise samples follow as they are transformed into data samples. The integral curves of the score function are the paths that run from noise toward regions of higher data density. Every flow, every diffusion, every dynamical generative model is built from integral curves of a vector field on a manifold.

    The flow — moving every point simultaneously

    If you let every point on the river’s surface drift with the current simultaneously — not just one kayaker but the entire water surface — you get the flow of the vector field. For each time \(t\), the flow \(\Phi_t: M \to M\) is the map that sends each starting point \(p\) to the position reached after flowing along the current for time \(t\):

    \[\Phi_t(p) = \gamma_p(t)\]

    where \(\gamma_p\) is the integral curve starting at \(p\). The flow is a one-parameter family of diffeomorphisms of \(M\) — at each time \(t\), the map \(\Phi_t\) is a smooth bijection from \(M\) to itself, representing the total displacement of the river’s surface under the current after time \(t\).

    The flow satisfies two key properties. First, \(\Phi_0 = \text{id}_M\) — at time zero, nothing has moved. Second, \(\Phi_{t+s} = \Phi_t \circ \Phi_s\) — flowing for time \(t+s\) is the same as flowing for time \(s\) and then for time \(t\). These two properties make \(\{\Phi_t\}_{t \in \mathbb{R}}\) a one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms — the group structure of time translation encoded in the geometry of flows.

    This is the mathematical heart of flow matching in Phase 3. The generative model learns a vector field \(X_\theta\) on the data manifold such that the flow \(\Phi_t\) of \(X_\theta\) carries the noise distribution at time \(t=0\) to the data distribution at time \(t=1\). The vector field is learned by regression — fitting \(X_\theta(\gamma(t))\) to \(\gamma'(t)\) along sampled trajectories — and the learned vector field’s integral curves are the generation paths. The flow of the vector field is the push-forward of the noise distribution by the map \(\Phi_1\), which is the diffeomorphism from noise space to data space that the model has learned.

    Now here is the break moment. The naive assumption is that a vector field on a manifold is just an assignment of a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) to each point — since charts give each point coordinates in \(\mathbb{R}^n\), surely the velocity vector at each point is just a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) as well?

    This breaks immediately when you change charts. Suppose the guide company switches from river-aligned coordinates to compass-aligned coordinates. The coordinate basis vectors change — what was “along river” and “across river” becomes a linear combination of “north-south” and “east-west.” A kayaker moving at 3 metres per second along the river has the same physical velocity regardless of which coordinate system you use, but the components of that velocity change when you change coordinates. The components \((v^1, v^2)\) in the old chart and the components \((\tilde{v}^1, \tilde{v}^2)\) in the new chart are related by the Jacobian of the transition map:

    \[\begin{pmatrix} \tilde{v}^1 \\ \tilde{v}^2 \end{pmatrix} = J_{\tau} \begin{pmatrix} v^1 \\ v^2 \end{pmatrix}\]

    where \(J_\tau\) is the Jacobian matrix of the transition function \(\tau = \tilde\phi \circ \phi^{-1}\) between the two charts. A tangent vector is not a fixed array of numbers — it is a geometric object whose coordinate representation transforms by the Jacobian when you change charts. If you treat tangent vectors as fixed arrays of numbers, you get answers that depend on your choice of coordinate system — a physically meaningless result. The break moment reveals that the coordinate representation of a tangent vector is chart-dependent, and the correct notion of a tangent vector is the geometric object that transforms correctly under chart changes.

    This is why the intrinsic definition — a tangent vector as a derivation on smooth functions, which we will formalize in Layer 2 — is necessary. The intrinsic definition makes the tangent vector a geometric object that exists before any coordinate system is chosen, and the coordinate representation follows from the geometric object, not the other way around. The break moment is the motivation for the entire formal apparatus of tangent spaces.

    Hold four pictures simultaneously.

    The Trishuli current: a smooth vector field on the river’s two-dimensional surface manifold, assigning to each point a velocity vector in the local tangent plane, smoothly varying from the rushing narrows to the broad shallow stretches.

    The tangent bundle: the four-dimensional space of all position-velocity pairs — two dimensions for position on the river’s surface, two dimensions for the direction and speed of flow at each position — with the projection map sending each pair to its base point.

    The integral curve: the path a drifting kayaker follows, the unique curve whose velocity at every moment exactly equals the current at that point, solving the ODE \(\gamma'(t) = X(\gamma(t))\).

    The flow: the simultaneous drift of every point on the river’s surface with the current, the one-parameter family of diffeomorphisms generated by the vector field, the mathematical object that flow matching in Phase 3 learns to approximate.

    Layer 2 (Mathematics)

    We build the formalism in six stages: the intrinsic definition of tangent vectors as derivations, the tangent space as an \(n\)-dimensional vector space, the tangent bundle as a smooth \(2n\)-manifold, vector fields as smooth sections, the flow of a vector field and the Lie derivative, and the pushforward of vector fields under smooth maps.

    Tangent Vectors as Derivations

    The intrinsic definition of a tangent vector — independent of any coordinate system — uses the algebraic concept of a derivation on smooth functions.

    Let \(M\) be a smooth \(n\)-manifold and \(p \in M\). Denote by \(C^\infty(M)\) the algebra of smooth real-valued functions on \(M\). A tangent vector at \(p\) is a linear map \(v: C^\infty(M) \to \mathbb{R}\) satisfying the Leibniz rule (product rule):

    \[v(fg) = f(p) \cdot v(g) + g(p) \cdot v(f) \quad \forall f, g \in C^\infty(M)\]

    Such a linear map is called a derivation at \(p\). The set of all derivations at \(p\) is the tangent space \(T_pM\).

    Interpretation. A tangent vector \(v\) acts on smooth functions by measuring their rate of change in the direction \(v\). The Leibniz rule ensures that \(v\) behaves like a directional derivative — it satisfies the product rule that every derivative satisfies. If \(\gamma: (-\epsilon, \epsilon) \to M\) is a smooth curve with \(\gamma(0) = p\), the velocity vector \(\gamma'(0)\) is the derivation:

    \[\gamma'(0)(f) = (f \circ \gamma)'(0) = \frac{d}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0} f(\gamma(t))\]

    This is the directional derivative of \(f\) along the curve \(\gamma\) at \(p\). Every tangent vector arises this way — for any derivation \(v\) at \(p\), there exists a smooth curve \(\gamma\) with \(\gamma(0) = p\) and \(\gamma'(0) = v\).

    Properties of derivations. Two immediate consequences of the Leibniz rule:

    \[v(c) = 0 \quad \forall c \in \mathbb{R} \quad \text{(constants have zero derivative)}\] \[v(f^2) = 2f(p) \cdot v(f) \quad \text{(special case of Leibniz)}\]

    The Tangent Space as a Vector Space

    The tangent space \(T_pM\) is a vector space under pointwise addition and scalar multiplication:

    \[(v + w)(f) = v(f) + w(f), \qquad (\lambda v)(f) = \lambda \cdot v(f)\]

    Theorem. Let \((U, \phi)\) be a chart near \(p\) with coordinate functions \(x^1, \ldots, x^n\). The coordinate basis vectors:

    \[\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p: C^\infty(M) \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p(f) = \frac{\partial(f \circ \phi^{-1})}{\partial u^i}\bigg|_{\phi(p)}\]

    form a basis for \(T_pM\). Therefore \(\dim T_pM = n = \dim M\).

    The coordinate basis vector \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}|_p\) is the derivation that differentiates \(f\) in the \(i\)-th coordinate direction — it is the velocity vector of the coordinate curve \(t \mapsto \phi^{-1}(\phi(p) + te_i)\) at \(t = 0\).

    Coordinate representation. Every tangent vector \(v \in T_pM\) has a unique expansion:

    \[v = \sum_{i=1}^n v^i \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p\]

    where the components \(v^i = v(x^i)\) are the values of the derivation on the coordinate functions. The components \(v^i\) depend on the chart — they are the coordinates of \(v\) in the basis \(\{\partial/\partial x^i|_p\}\).

    Transformation law under chart changes. Let \((U, \phi)\) and \((V, \psi)\) be two charts overlapping at \(p\), with coordinates \(x^i\) and \(\tilde{x}^j\) respectively, and transition map \(\tilde{x}^j = \tau^j(x^1, \ldots, x^n)\). The basis vectors transform by:

    \[\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial \tilde{x}^j}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_{\phi(p)} \frac{\partial}{\partial \tilde{x}^j}\bigg|_p\]

    Therefore the components of \(v = \sum_i v^i \partial/\partial x^i|_p = \sum_j \tilde{v}^j \partial/\partial \tilde{x}^j|_p\) transform as:

    \[\tilde{v}^j = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial \tilde{x}^j}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_{\phi(p)} v^i\]

    In matrix form: \(\tilde{v} = J_\tau \cdot v\) where \(J_\tau = \left(\frac{\partial \tilde{x}^j}{\partial x^i}\right)\) is the Jacobian of the transition map. This is the break moment made precise: tangent vector components transform by the Jacobian under chart changes. Objects that transform by the Jacobian are called contravariant vectors — the defining property of tangent vectors in classical tensor calculus.

    The Tangent Bundle as a Smooth Manifold

    The tangent bundle is the disjoint union of all tangent spaces:

    \[TM = \bigsqcup_{p \in M} T_pM = \{(p, v) : p \in M, v \in T_pM\}\]

    with the projection map \(\pi: TM \to M\) defined by \(\pi(p, v) = p\).

    Smooth structure on \(TM\). Every chart \((U, \phi)\) on \(M\) induces a chart \((\pi^{-1}(U), \tilde\phi)\) on \(TM\):

    \[\tilde\phi: \pi^{-1}(U) \to \phi(U) \times \mathbb{R}^n \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{2n}\] \[\tilde\phi\left(p, \sum_i v^i \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p\right) = (\phi(p), v^1, \ldots, v^n) = (x^1(p), \ldots, x^n(p), v^1, \ldots, v^n)\]

    The \(2n\) coordinates \((x^1, \ldots, x^n, v^1, \ldots, v^n)\) are the natural coordinates on \(TM\) induced by the chart \((U,\phi)\) on \(M\) — the first \(n\) are the base point coordinates, the last \(n\) are the fiber coordinates (components of the tangent vector).

    The transition maps between overlapping charts \((\pi^{-1}(U), \tilde\phi)\) and \((\pi^{-1}(V), \tilde\psi)\) are:

    \[(\tilde\psi \circ \tilde\phi^{-1})(x, v) = \left(\tau(x), J_\tau(x) \cdot v\right)\]

    where \(\tau = \psi \circ \phi^{-1}\) is the transition map on \(M\) and \(J_\tau(x)\) is its Jacobian at \(x\). The base point transforms by \(\tau\) and the fiber transforms by the Jacobian \(J_\tau\) — exactly the transformation law for tangent vector components. Since \(\tau\) is a diffeomorphism, \(J_\tau\) is everywhere invertible, so these transition maps are smooth diffeomorphisms on \(\mathbb{R}^{2n}\).

    Theorem. \(TM\) with the smooth atlas \(\{(\pi^{-1}(U_\alpha), \tilde\phi_\alpha)\}\) induced from a smooth atlas \(\{(U_\alpha, \phi_\alpha)\}\) on \(M\) is a smooth manifold of dimension \(2n\).

    The projection \(\pi: TM \to M\) is a smooth map — its coordinate representation is \((x,v) \mapsto x\), the projection onto the first \(n\) coordinates, which is clearly smooth.

    Vector bundle structure. The tangent bundle \(TM\) is a vector bundle over \(M\) with fiber \(\mathbb{R}^n\): over each point \(p \in M\), the fiber \(\pi^{-1}(p) = T_pM\) is an \(n\)-dimensional vector space, and the vector space structures vary smoothly from fiber to fiber. The local trivialization over a chart \(U\) is the diffeomorphism \(\pi^{-1}(U) \cong U \times \mathbb{R}^n\) given by \(\tilde\phi\) — the tangent bundle looks like a product over each chart, but globally it may be twisted.

    Vector Fields as Smooth Sections

    A vector field on \(M\) is a smooth map \(X: M \to TM\) satisfying the section condition \(\pi \circ X = \text{id}_M\). This means \(X\) assigns to each \(p \in M\) a tangent vector \(X(p) \in T_pM\) at \(p\), and the assignment varies smoothly.

    In a chart \((U, \phi)\) with coordinates \(x^i\), a vector field \(X\) is expressed as:

    \[X = \sum_{i=1}^n X^i(x) \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\]

    where \(X^i: U \to \mathbb{R}\) are smooth functions — the component functions of \(X\) in the chart. Smoothness of \(X\) as a map \(M \to TM\) is equivalent to smoothness of all component functions in every chart.

    The set of all smooth vector fields on \(M\) is written \(\mathfrak{X}(M)\). It is a module over \(C^\infty(M)\) — vector fields can be added and multiplied by smooth functions:

    \[(fX + gY)(p) = f(p)X(p) + g(p)Y(p)\]

    Action on functions. A vector field \(X\) acts on smooth functions \(f \in C^\infty(M)\) by:

    \[(Xf)(p) = X(p)(f) = \sum_{i=1}^n X^i(p) \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p\]

    The function \(Xf: M \to \mathbb{R}\) is the directional derivative of \(f\) in the direction of \(X\), varying smoothly over \(M\). This action makes \(X\) a derivation on \(C^\infty(M)\) — a linear map \(X: C^\infty(M) \to C^\infty(M)\) satisfying \(X(fg) = (Xf)g + f(Xg)\).

    The Lie bracket. Given two vector fields \(X, Y \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\), their Lie bracket \([X, Y]\) is the vector field defined by:

    \[[X, Y](f) = X(Yf) - Y(Xf) \quad \forall f \in C^\infty(M)\]

    In coordinates:

    \[[X, Y]^k = \sum_{i=1}^n \left(X^i \frac{\partial Y^k}{\partial x^i} - Y^i \frac{\partial X^k}{\partial x^i}\right)\]

    The Lie bracket measures the failure of two vector fields to commute — the extent to which flowing along \(X\) then \(Y\) differs from flowing along \(Y\) then \(X\). It satisfies:

    \[[X,Y] = -[Y,X] \quad \text{(antisymmetry)}\] \[[X,[Y,Z]] + [Y,[Z,X]] + [Z,[X,Y]] = 0 \quad \text{(Jacobi identity)}\]

    making \(\mathfrak{X}(M)\) a Lie algebra — the infinitesimal version of the diffeomorphism group \(\text{Diff}(M)\).

    Integral Curves and Flows

    Integral curves. An integral curve of a vector field \(X\) through \(p \in M\) is a smooth curve \(\gamma: (-\epsilon, \epsilon) \to M\) satisfying:

    \[\gamma(0) = p \quad \text{and} \quad \gamma'(t) = X(\gamma(t)) \quad \forall t \in (-\epsilon, \epsilon)\]

    In a chart with coordinates \(x^i\), this is the ODE system:

    \[\frac{d}{dt}x^i(\gamma(t)) = X^i(x^1(\gamma(t)), \ldots, x^n(\gamma(t))), \quad i = 1, \ldots, n\]

    Theorem (Existence and Uniqueness). For any smooth vector field \(X\) and any \(p \in M\), there exists \(\epsilon > 0\) and a unique smooth integral curve \(\gamma_p: (-\epsilon, \epsilon) \to M\) with \(\gamma_p(0) = p\).

    This is the ODE existence and uniqueness theorem (Picard-Lindelöf) applied to the coordinate expression of \(X\). Smoothness of \(X\) guarantees local Lipschitz continuity, which is the condition needed for uniqueness.

    The flow. The flow of a complete vector field \(X\) (one whose integral curves extend to all of \(\mathbb{R}\)) is the map:

    \[\Phi: \mathbb{R} \times M \to M, \quad \Phi(t, p) = \gamma_p(t)\]

    For each fixed \(t\), the map \(\Phi_t = \Phi(t, \cdot): M \to M\) is a diffeomorphism. The flow satisfies:

    \[\Phi_0 = \text{id}_M \quad \text{and} \quad \Phi_{t+s} = \Phi_t \circ \Phi_s\]

    making \(\{\Phi_t\}_{t \in \mathbb{R}}\) a one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms of \(M\). The vector field \(X\) is the infinitesimal generator of the flow: \(X(p) = \frac{d}{dt}|_{t=0} \Phi_t(p)\).

    Relationship between vector fields and flows. There is a bijection between smooth complete vector fields and one-parameter groups of diffeomorphisms. Every vector field generates a flow, and every smooth one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms \(\{\Phi_t\}\) has an infinitesimal generator \(X(p) = \frac{d}{dt}|_{t=0}\Phi_t(p)\). This bijection is the fundamental relationship between the infinitesimal (vector field) and the global (diffeomorphism) perspectives on dynamics.

    The Lie Derivative

    The Lie derivative of a vector field \(Y\) along a vector field \(X\) measures the rate of change of \(Y\) under the flow of \(X\):

    \[\mathcal{L}_X Y = \frac{d}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0} (\Phi_{-t})_* Y = [X, Y]\]

    The Lie derivative equals the Lie bracket. This identity has a geometric meaning: \([X, Y](p)\) measures how much \(Y\) fails to be preserved by the flow of \(X\). If \([X, Y] = 0\), then \(Y\) is invariant under the flow of \(X\) — the two vector fields commute, meaning you get the same result flowing along \(X\) then \(Y\) as flowing along \(Y\) then \(X\).

    For a smooth function \(f\):

    \[\mathcal{L}_X f = Xf = \sum_i X^i \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}\]

    The Lie derivative of a function is its directional derivative along \(X\).

    The Pushforward of Vector Fields

    Let \(F: M \to N\) be a smooth map. The pushforward (or differential) of \(F\) at \(p\) is the linear map \(dF_p: T_pM \to T_{F(p)}N\) defined by:

    \[(dF_p(v))(g) = v(g \circ F) \quad \forall g \in C^\infty(N)\]

    This extends the earlier definition: \(dF_p\) pushes a derivation at \(p\) forward to a derivation at \(F(p)\) by composing test functions with \(F\).

    In coordinates — with \(\phi\) a chart near \(p\) on \(M\) with coordinates \(x^i\), and \(\psi\) a chart near \(F(p)\) on \(N\) with coordinates \(y^j\) — the pushforward is:

    \[dF_p\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p\right) = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial F^j}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_{\phi(p)} \frac{\partial}{\partial y^j}\bigg|_{F(p)}\]

    where \(F^j = y^j \circ F\) are the component functions of \(F\) in coordinates. The matrix \(\left(\frac{\partial F^j}{\partial x^i}\right)\) is the Jacobian of the coordinate representation \(\hat{F} = \psi \circ F \circ \phi^{-1}\) — the pushforward is the Jacobian matrix acting on tangent vectors.

    Chain rule for pushforwards. If \(F: M \to N\) and \(G: N \to P\) are smooth, then:

    \[d(G \circ F)_p = dG_{F(p)} \circ dF_p\]

    The chain rule is the composition of linear maps — Jacobians multiply.

    Pushforward of vector fields. When \(F: M \to N\) is a diffeomorphism, the pushforward of a vector field \(X \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\) is the vector field \(F_*X \in \mathfrak{X}(N)\) defined by:

    \[(F_*X)(q) = dF_{F^{-1}(q)}(X(F^{-1}(q)))\]

    For a diffeomorphism \(F\), \(F_*X\) is the unique vector field on \(N\) whose integral curves are the \(F\)-images of the integral curves of \(X\): if \(\gamma\) is an integral curve of \(X\), then \(F \circ \gamma\) is an integral curve of \(F_*X\).

    The Cotangent Bundle — The Dual

    The cotangent space at \(p\) is the dual vector space of the tangent space:

    \[T_p^*M = (T_pM)^* = \{w: T_pM \to \mathbb{R} : w \text{ linear}\}\]

    Elements of \(T_p^*M\) are called covectors or 1-forms at \(p\). The dual basis to \(\{\partial/\partial x^i|_p\}\) is \(\{dx^i|_p\}\), defined by:

    \[dx^i\bigg|_p\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}\bigg|_p\right) = \delta^i_j\]

    The cotangent bundle \(T^*M = \bigsqcup_{p \in M} T_p^*M\) is the dual bundle to \(TM\) — a \(2n\)-dimensional smooth manifold whose sections are differential 1-forms. A smooth 1-form \(\omega \in \Omega^1(M)\) assigns to each \(p\) a covector \(\omega_p \in T_p^*M\). The exterior derivative of a smooth function \(f\) is the 1-form \(df\) defined by \(df_p(v) = v(f)\) for \(v \in T_pM\). In coordinates: \(df = \sum_i \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i} dx^i\).

    The Riemannian metric — the subject of the next topic — is a smooth assignment of an inner product to each tangent space, equivalently a smooth section of the symmetric tensor product \(T^*M \otimes T^*M\).

    Neural ODEs and Flow Matching — The Tangent Bundle Formulation

    The neural ODE \(\frac{dx}{dt} = f_\theta(x,t)\) is now precisely stated in tangent bundle language.

    A time-dependent vector field on a manifold \(M\) is a smooth map \(F: M \times \mathbb{R} \to TM\) with \(\pi \circ F(\cdot, t) = \text{id}_M\) for each \(t\). For each fixed \(t\), \(F(\cdot, t) \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\) is a vector field on \(M\). The neural ODE states: the curve \(x: [0,1] \to M\) satisfies \(x'(t) = F(x(t), t) \in T_{x(t)}M\) — the velocity at each time is the vector field evaluated at the current position.

    The flow map \(\Phi_1: M \to M\) of the neural ODE — the map that sends the initial condition \(x(0)\) to the terminal state \(x(1)\) — is the diffeomorphism learned by a normalizing flow. Its pushforward \((\Phi_1)_\#\mu_0\) is the distribution at time \(t=1\) obtained by pushing the noise distribution \(\mu_0\) through the flow.

    Flow matching learns the time-dependent vector field \(F_\theta\) by minimizing:

    \[\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \int_0^1 \mathbb{E}_{x(t)}\left[\|F_\theta(x(t), t) - \dot{x}(t)\|_{T_{x(t)}M}^2\right] dt\]

    The norm \(\|\cdot\|_{T_{x(t)}M}\) is the Riemannian metric on the tangent space at \(x(t)\) — a norm on tangent vectors, defined by the next topic. The loss function penalizes the discrepancy between the learned vector field \(F_\theta\) and the true velocity \(\dot{x}(t)\) along the target trajectories, measured in the tangent space norm. The tangent bundle is not a background structure — it is the space in which the loss is evaluated and the gradients are computed.

    WHY THIS MATTERS

    In MATH 217 (ODEs and PDEs), you solved systems of ODEs \(\dot{x} = f(x,t)\) for \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\). These are integral curve equations for time-dependent vector fields on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) — the simplest manifold, with a single global chart. The existence and uniqueness theorem you used (Picard-Lindelöf) is the same theorem that guarantees the existence of integral curves on smooth manifolds — the manifold version is the coordinate-invariant formulation of the same result. In MATH 201 (Advanced Calculus), the directional derivative \(D_v f(p) = \nabla f(p) \cdot v\) is the action of the tangent vector \(v\) (here an element of \(\mathbb{R}^n\), the tangent space of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) at \(p\)) on the smooth function \(f\) — it is the derivation at \(p\) applied to \(f\). The gradient \(\nabla f(p)\) is an element of the cotangent space \(T_p^*\mathbb{R}^n\) — a covector, not a vector, though in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with the Euclidean metric the distinction is blurred because the metric identifies \(T_p\mathbb{R}^n\) with \(T_p^*\mathbb{R}^n\). In MATH 103 (Linear Algebra), every linear map \(A: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) is the tangent map \(dF_p\) of the smooth map \(F(x) = Ax\) at any point \(p\) — because linear maps are their own best linear approximations. The matrix multiplication \(Av\) is the Jacobian \(J_F \cdot v\), the tangent map applied to the tangent vector \(v\).

    Layer 3 (Bridge)

    Every element of the Trishuli river story has a precise mathematical address. Let us walk through them one by one, until the kayakers, the current field, the drifting paths, and the full formal apparatus are completely fused into a single picture.

    The Trishuli river’s surface — the curved two-dimensional terrain through which the water flows — is the smooth manifold \(M\). It is not a flat plane. It curves through the gorge, bends around rock faces, tilts as the gradient changes from steep narrows to broad shallows. The manifold \(M\) is the intrinsic two-dimensional world of the river’s surface — the space in which all positions, velocities, and trajectories live. The kayakers navigate on \(M\), not in the three-dimensional ambient space of the gorge. The rocks, the air above the water, the riverbed below — all of these are outside \(M\). Only the water’s surface, as a two-dimensional curved space, is \(M\).

    The kayaker’s position at any moment — a specific point on the river’s surface — is a point \(p \in M\). Position and velocity are two separate pieces of information, as your guide insisted from the beginning. The point \(p\) encodes where the kayaker is. It carries no information about how fast or in which direction the kayaker is moving. The manifold \(M\) is the space of positions — the space of the first kind of information.

    A smooth route through the rapids — the path \(\gamma: (-\epsilon, \epsilon) \to M\) traced by a kayaker navigating from one point to another — is a smooth curve on the manifold. At each time \(t\), the kayaker is at position \(\gamma(t) \in M\). The curve \(\gamma\) is a smooth assignment of positions to times — a continuous, differentiable trajectory on the river’s surface. The starting position \(\gamma(0) = p\) is the base point. The curve is the geometric object from which the tangent vector is extracted.

    The kayaker’s velocity at time zero — the rate at which position is changing, measured as a tangent direction to the river’s surface at \(p\) — is the tangent vector \(\gamma'(0) \in T_pM\). The velocity is not a vector in three-dimensional ambient space pointing through the gorge wall — it is a vector tangent to the river’s surface, lying in the flat plane that best approximates the surface at \(p\). The tangent vector \(\gamma'(0)\) is the derivation that acts on smooth functions by differentiation along the curve: \(\gamma'(0)(f) = (f \circ \gamma)'(0)\). This action measures the rate of change of any smooth function \(f\) on the river’s surface in the direction the kayaker is moving. The derivation definition is the intrinsic, coordinate-free formalization of the intuition that velocity is the rate of change of position in a given direction.

    The Leibniz rule \(v(fg) = f(p) \cdot v(g) + g(p) \cdot v(f)\) is the product rule for rates of change along the kayaker’s path. If \(f\) and \(g\) are two smooth functions on the river — say, temperature and depth — then the rate of change of their product \(fg\) along the kayaker’s route satisfies the product rule exactly. The derivation definition encodes this product rule as an axiom, ensuring that every tangent vector behaves like a genuine directional derivative. Constants have zero rate of change along any path: \(v(c) = 0\). The Leibniz rule is the algebraic expression of this physical fact.

    The tangent plane to the river’s surface at \(p\) — the flat two-dimensional plane that locally approximates the curved surface at that point — is the tangent space \(T_pM\). It is the set of all possible velocity vectors at \(p\) — all directions and speeds of motion through \(p\) along the surface. It is a two-dimensional vector space: you can add velocity vectors (if you paddle northeast at 2 m/s and separately paddle northwest at 1 m/s, the combined velocity is their sum) and scale them (paddling twice as fast in the same direction doubles the velocity vector). The dimension of \(T_pM\) equals the dimension of the manifold — two for the river’s surface. The tangent space is not the surface itself — it is a flat vector space that approximates the surface near \(p\).

    The guide’s local coordinate map — the chart \((U, \phi)\) covering a patch of the river — induces the coordinate basis vectors \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^1}|_p\) and \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^2}|_p\) that span \(T_pM\). The coordinate function \(x^1\) measures distance along the river. The coordinate function \(x^2\) measures distance across the river. The basis vector \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^1}|_p\) is the velocity of a kayaker moving along the river at unit speed with no lateral deviation — the direction of increasing \(x^1\) holding \(x^2\) fixed. The basis vector \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^2}|_p\) is the velocity of a kayaker moving across the river at unit speed — the direction of increasing \(x^2\) holding \(x^1\) fixed. Any velocity vector at \(p\) is a linear combination \(v = v^1 \frac{\partial}{\partial x^1}|_p + v^2 \frac{\partial}{\partial x^2}|_p\), where \(v^1\) is the along-river speed and \(v^2\) is the across-river speed.

    The break moment — switching from river-aligned coordinates to compass-aligned coordinates and seeing the velocity components change — is the transformation law \(\tilde{v}^j = \sum_i \frac{\partial \tilde{x}^j}{\partial x^i} v^i\). The physical velocity of the kayaker does not change when the guide company switches coordinate systems. The kayaker is still moving in the same direction at the same speed. But the components of that velocity — the numbers representing it in the coordinate system — change according to the Jacobian of the transition map. A kayaker moving at 3 m/s due northeast has components \((v^1, v^2) = (2.12, 2.12)\) in compass coordinates (north-south and east-west) but different components in river-aligned coordinates if the river runs northeast. The geometric object — the velocity vector — is the same. Its numerical representation changes with the coordinate system. The tangent vector is the geometric object, not its coordinate representation. The transformation law is not an inconvenience — it is the definition of what makes something a genuine tangent vector rather than an arbitrary array of numbers that happens to be attached to a point.

    The complete collection of all position-velocity pairs — every point on the river combined with every possible velocity at that point — is the tangent bundle \(TM = \bigsqcup_{p \in M} T_pM\). A point of \(TM\) is a pair \((p, v)\): a position \(p\) on the river’s surface and a velocity \(v\) in the tangent space \(T_pM\) at that position. For the two-dimensional river surface, \(TM\) is four-dimensional: two dimensions for position (along-river and across-river) and two dimensions for velocity (along-river speed and across-river speed). A kayaker’s complete state at any moment — position and velocity — is a single point in \(TM\).

    The natural coordinates \((x^1, x^2, v^1, v^2)\) on \(TM\) induced by the chart \((U, \phi)\) on \(M\) are the coordinates of the four-dimensional tangent bundle. The chart \((\pi^{-1}(U), \tilde\phi)\) on \(TM\) assigns to each state \((p, v)\) four numbers: the position coordinates \(\phi(p) = (x^1(p), x^2(p))\) and the velocity components \((v^1, v^2)\) in the coordinate basis. The transition maps between overlapping charts on \(TM\) transform position by the transition map of \(M\) and velocity by its Jacobian: \((\tilde\phi \circ \tilde\psi^{-1})(x,v) = (\tau(x), J_\tau(x) \cdot v)\). The position transforms as a point on \(M\); the velocity transforms as a tangent vector — by the Jacobian. The tangent bundle inherits its smooth structure from \(M\) through exactly this coordinate construction.

    The projection map \(\pi: TM \to M\) — sending each state \((p,v)\) to the position \(p\) and forgetting the velocity — is the formal expression of “where is the kayaker?” without asking “how fast are they going?” The projection forgets the velocity and remembers only the position. The fiber over a point \(p\) — the set \(\pi^{-1}(p) = T_pM\) of all states with position \(p\) — is the set of all possible velocities at that position. The tangent bundle is a vector bundle over \(M\): over each position, the fiber of possible velocities is an \(n\)-dimensional vector space.

    The Trishuli’s current — the assignment of a water velocity to every point on the river’s surface — is a smooth vector field \(X \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\). At each position \(p\), the water has a velocity \(X(p) \in T_pM\). The assignment \(p \mapsto X(p)\) is a section of the tangent bundle: a smooth map \(X: M \to TM\) with \(\pi \circ X = \text{id}_M\), meaning the velocity at each position is genuinely a tangent vector at that position. In local river-aligned coordinates, the current is expressed as \(X = X^1(x^1, x^2) \frac{\partial}{\partial x^1} + X^2(x^1, x^2) \frac{\partial}{\partial x^2}\), where \(X^1\) and \(X^2\) are smooth functions giving the along-river and across-river components of the current at each location. The smoothness of the vector field is the smoothness of these component functions — the current changes direction and speed gradually, without sudden jumps.

    The action of the current \(X\) on a smooth function \(f\) — computing \(Xf = \sum_i X^i \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}\) — is the rate at which \(f\) changes as the water flows. If \(f\) is the temperature of the water, then \(Xf\) is the rate at which temperature changes at each point due to the current advecting water from upstream. If \(f\) is the depth of the river, \(Xf\) is the rate at which the apparent depth changes along flow lines. The vector field is a first-order differential operator — it differentiates smooth functions in the direction of the current.

    The Lie bracket \([X, Y]\) of two vector fields — measuring the failure of their flows to commute — is the current’s interaction with another vector field \(Y\) on the river. Suppose \(Y\) is the wind field over the river’s surface, and \(X\) is the water current. The Lie bracket \([X, Y]\) measures: if a leaf floats downstream with the current \(X\) for a short time, then drifts sideways with the wind \(Y\) for a short time, then goes upstream against \(X\), then against \(Y\) — does it return to where it started? If \([X,Y] = 0\), yes. If \([X,Y] \neq 0\), the leaf ends up displaced by the Lie bracket — a displacement that measures the infinitesimal non-commutativity of the two flows.

    The path of a drifting kayaker — the unique curve \(\gamma_p\) starting at \(p\) whose velocity at every moment equals the current at that position — is the integral curve of \(X\) through \(p\). The ODE \(\gamma_p'(t) = X(\gamma_p(t))\), \(\gamma_p(0) = p\) has a unique smooth solution by the Picard-Lindelöf theorem — the ODE existence and uniqueness theorem from MATH 217, now applied to the coordinate expression of \(X\) in any chart. The integral curve is not the current itself — it is the trajectory traced by following the current from a specific starting point. The current at every point tells the kayaker which way to go; the integral curve is the result of following those instructions from the initial position.

    The simultaneous drift of every point on the river’s surface with the current — moving the entire water surface forward in time — is the flow \(\Phi_t: M \to M\) of the vector field \(X\). At each time \(t\), the flow map \(\Phi_t\) sends each starting position \(p\) to the position \(\gamma_p(t)\) reached after drifting with the current for time \(t\). The flow satisfies \(\Phi_0 = \text{id}_M\) — at time zero nothing has moved — and \(\Phi_{t+s} = \Phi_t \circ \Phi_s\) — flowing for time \(t+s\) is the same as flowing for time \(s\) and then for time \(t\). Each \(\Phi_t\) is a diffeomorphism of \(M\) — a smooth bijection with smooth inverse — because the current is smooth and the inverse flow \(\Phi_{-t}\) is generated by the reversed current \(-X\).

    The vector field \(X\) is the infinitesimal generator of the flow \(\{\Phi_t\}\) — the velocity field is the derivative of the flow at time zero: \(X(p) = \frac{d}{dt}|_{t=0} \Phi_t(p)\). This relationship — between the infinitesimal object (the vector field) and the global object (the one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms) — is the mathematical heart of every dynamical system. Knowing the vector field is knowing the infinitesimal rule of motion; knowing the flow is knowing the global consequence of following that rule from every starting point simultaneously. The bijection between complete vector fields and one-parameter groups of diffeomorphisms is the precise statement that infinitesimal and global are equivalent perspectives on the same dynamics.

    The neural ODE \(\frac{dx}{dt} = f_\theta(x,t)\) is the integral curve equation for a time-dependent vector field \(F_\theta(\cdot,t) \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\). The state \(x(t)\) is a point on the manifold \(M\) at each time \(t\) — the kayaker’s position. The velocity \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) is a tangent vector in \(T_{x(t)}M\) at each time — the kayaker’s speed and direction. The neural network \(f_\theta\) is the vector field — it assigns to each position-time pair \((x,t)\) a tangent vector in \(T_xM\), specifying the direction and speed of motion at that position at that time. The ODE says: the velocity of the state equals the vector field evaluated at the current state. The solution \(x(t)\) for \(t \in [0,1]\) is the integral curve of this time-dependent vector field, tracing the path from the initial noise sample \(x(0)\) to the generated data sample \(x(1)\).

    The flow map \(\Phi_1: M \to M\) of the neural ODE — the diffeomorphism that sends every noise sample to its corresponding data sample after flowing for time 1 — is what the normalizing flow model learns. The flow matching loss \(\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \int_0^1 \mathbb{E}[\|F_\theta(x(t),t) - \dot{x}(t)\|^2] \, dt\) penalizes the discrepancy between the learned vector field \(F_\theta\) and the true velocity \(\dot{x}(t)\) along target trajectories. The norm \(\|\cdot\|\) is taken in the tangent space \(T_{x(t)}M\) — it requires the Riemannian metric, which is the next topic. The entire flow matching framework lives in the tangent bundle: positions on \(M\), velocities in \(TM\), the learned map as a vector field, and the training loss as an \(L^2\) discrepancy in the fiber of \(TM\) over each trajectory point.

    The pushforward of a vector field under a diffeomorphism \(F: M \to N\) — the map \((F_*X)(q) = dF_{F^{-1}(q)}(X(F^{-1}(q)))\) — is the change-of-variables formula for vector fields. In the kayaking story: if the river guide uses a different coordinate chart for the same stretch of river, the current \(X\) expressed in new coordinates is \(F_*X\) where \(F\) is the coordinate change map. The current’s geometric content is unchanged — the water flows the same way regardless of the chart — but its coordinate representation changes by the Jacobian. The pushforward is the correct way to change the representation of a vector field under a diffeomorphism, and it is the mechanism by which the equivariance conditions of Phase 2 are stated: a network is equivariant under a diffeomorphism \(F\) if the network’s output vector field is the pushforward of the input vector field under \(F\).

    The cotangent bundle \(T^*M\) — the bundle of covectors dual to the tangent vectors — is the arena of gradients and 1-forms. In the kayaking story: while the current \(X\) is a vector field (a tangent vector at each point), the temperature gradient \(dT\) is a covector field — an element of \(T_p^*M\) at each point \(p\). The temperature gradient \(dT_p\) acts on velocity vectors by \(dT_p(v) = v(T)\) — it measures the rate of temperature change in the direction \(v\). In flat Euclidean space, the distinction between vectors and covectors is hidden because the Euclidean metric identifies \(T_pM\) with \(T_p^*M\). On a curved manifold without a metric, vectors and covectors are genuinely different objects. The Riemannian metric — the next topic — is precisely the tool that identifies them: it converts covectors (gradients) to vectors (gradient vectors), making the gradient of a function a tangent vector rather than a covector.

    The connection to MATH 217 (ODEs and PDEs) is now total. Every ODE system \(\dot{x} = f(x,t)\) you solved in MATH 217 was the integral curve equation for a time-dependent vector field on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) — the simplest manifold, with a single global chart where the tangent bundle is trivial: \(T\mathbb{R}^n = \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^n\). The Picard-Lindelöf existence and uniqueness theorem you used to prove ODE solutions exist is the same theorem that guarantees integral curves exist on smooth manifolds — applied to the coordinate representation of the vector field in a chart. The phase portrait you drew to visualize ODE solutions — arrows at each point indicating the direction and speed of the flow — is the coordinate representation of a vector field on \(\mathbb{R}^2\). Every phase portrait is a vector field. Every solution curve is an integral curve. The manifold theory extends every concept from MATH 217 to curved spaces.

    The connection to MATH 201 (Advanced Calculus) runs through the directional derivative. The directional derivative \(D_v f(p) = \nabla f(p) \cdot v\) that you computed repeatedly in MATH 201 is the action of the tangent vector \(v \in T_p\mathbb{R}^n\) on the smooth function \(f\) — the derivation \(v(f) = \nabla f(p) \cdot v\). The gradient vector \(\nabla f(p)\) is the coordinate representation of the covector \(df_p \in T_p^*\mathbb{R}^n\), which is identified with a tangent vector by the Euclidean metric. Every gradient computation in MATH 201 was a covector computation in \(T_p^*\mathbb{R}^n\). The Hessian matrix \(\nabla^2 f(p)\) is the coordinate representation of a symmetric bilinear form on \(T_p\mathbb{R}^n\) — a section of \(T^*\mathbb{R}^n \otimes T^*\mathbb{R}^n\). The entire calculus of MATH 201, restated in the language of tangent bundles, is the coordinate expression of coordinate-free differential geometry on the simplest possible manifold.

    Looking forward across the roadmap, the tangent bundle is the arena of every dynamical process in Phases 1 through 3. In Phase 1, the Wasserstein geodesic between two distributions — the displacement interpolation — is the integral curve of the velocity field of the optimal transport map, living in the tangent bundle of the Wasserstein manifold. The JKO scheme’s gradient descent in Wasserstein space moves the distribution in the direction of the negative gradient of the energy functional — a tangent vector in the Wasserstein tangent bundle at the current distribution. In Phase 2, the message passing updates of graph neural networks are vector field evaluations: the update function at each node is a tangent vector in the feature tangent bundle, and the network’s forward pass is the flow of this vector field for a fixed number of steps. The equivariance conditions are conditions on the pushforward of the vector field under symmetry diffeomorphisms. In Phase 3, the score function \(\nabla \log p_t(x)\) is a covector field on the data manifold — an element of \(T_p^*M\) at each \(p\) — and score matching learns this covector field by regression. The Fokker-Planck equation governs the evolution of the probability density under the flow of a stochastic vector field — it is a PDE on the manifold whose solutions are probability densities evolving along integral curves. The neural ODE, the score function, the diffusion process, and the flow matching velocity field are all vector fields on manifolds, all living in the tangent bundle, all generating flows whose pushforwards convert noise distributions into data distributions.

    The Trishuli’s current — assigned to every point on the river’s surface, generating the unique drift path from every starting position, flowing the entire water surface forward in time as a one-parameter family of diffeomorphisms — is not a metaphor for the tangent bundle. It is the tangent bundle, told in the language of a guide who needs to navigate a curved surface using velocity information that is local, geometric, and coordinate-free.
