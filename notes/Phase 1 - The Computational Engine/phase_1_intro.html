    Phase 1 - The Computational Engine (Optimal Transport)

    Introduction

    Phase 0 gave you the arsenal. You now speak the language of mathematics at the level the roadmap demands. Measure theory gave you the precise framework for probability as mass distributed over space. Functional analysis gave you the tools for optimizing over functions and exploiting duality. Differential geometry gave you the language of curved spaces, tangent bundles, Riemannian metrics, and geodesics. Advanced linear algebra gave you the spectral and singular value decompositions that underlie every matrix computation in machine learning. The arsenal is complete.

    Phase 1 puts the arsenal to work on a single, focused problem: how do you compare two probability distributions, and how do you move one into another at minimum cost?

    This question sounds narrow. It is not. It is one of the deepest and most practically consequential questions in modern mathematics, and the framework built to answer it — Optimal Transport — has become the geometric foundation of generative AI, statistical inference, domain adaptation, image processing, and computational biology. The roadmap names this phase The Computational Engine precisely because Optimal Transport is not merely a theoretical framework — it is a computable, implementable engine for geometric operations on distributions that would otherwise be intractable.

    The core concept, stated plainly in the roadmap: comparing distributions is a transportation problem. When you have two probability distributions — say, the distribution of words in English text and the distribution of words in French text, or the distribution of pixel intensities in one image and another, or the distribution of molecular conformations before and after a chemical reaction — you want to measure how different they are. But you want to measure this difference geometrically, in a way that respects the underlying space in which the distributions live.

    The naive approach is to compute something like the \(L^2\) distance between the density functions, or the KL divergence, or the total variation distance. These are all valid dissimilarity measures, but they have a critical flaw: they are blind to the geometry of the underlying space. Two distributions that place all their mass at locations that are very close to each other — say, a Gaussian centered at \(0\) and a Gaussian centered at \(0.001\) — look almost identical under any of these measures, as they should. But two distributions that place their mass at very different locations — a Gaussian at \(0\) and a Gaussian at \(100\) — look maximally different under the KL divergence and total variation, even if one can be obtained from the other by a simple, coherent shift of all the mass. The KL divergence and total variation cannot distinguish between “the mass went somewhere completely different and incoherent” and “the mass shifted coherently by a large but geometrically simple displacement.” Optimal Transport can.

    The Wasserstein distance — the central object of Phase 1 — measures the minimum amount of work required to move all the mass from one distribution to the other, where work is measured as mass times distance. It is the Earth Mover’s Distance, the cost of the optimal transportation plan. Distributions that differ by a coherent global shift are close in Wasserstein distance, because the shift costs little work. Distributions that are genuinely geometrically different are far in Wasserstein distance, because moving the mass requires crossing large distances in the underlying space. The Wasserstein distance is blind to the parameterization of the distributions — it sees only their geometric relationship in the underlying space.

    Phase 1 develops the theory and computation of Optimal Transport across three chapters, each one building on the previous.

    The first chapter is The Static Problem: Monge and Kantorovich. This is the foundational chapter — it poses the Optimal Transport problem in its two classical formulations. The Monge formulation asks for a deterministic map \(T\) that moves every grain of mass from its starting location to a destination, minimizing the total transport cost. The Kantorovich relaxation replaces the deterministic map with a probabilistic coupling — a joint distribution over starting and ending locations whose marginals are the two distributions being compared. The Kantorovich relaxation is a linear program, and its dual — the Kantorovich dual — expresses the Wasserstein distance as a supremum over pairs of potential functions. The Legendre-Fenchel transform from Chapter 3 of Phase 0 is the engine of this duality. The Wasserstein distance itself emerges as the value of this optimization, and the KKT conditions from Chapter 2 of Phase 0 characterize the optimal coupling through complementary slackness.

    The second chapter is The Computational Breakthrough: Sinkhorn. The Kantorovich linear program has \(O(n^2)\) variables and \(O(n)\) constraints when the distributions are discretized over \(n\) points, and solving it exactly using the simplex method or interior point methods costs \(O(n^3)\) — far too slow for the high-dimensional settings of machine learning. The Sinkhorn algorithm resolves this by adding an entropic regularization term — a KL divergence penalty that smoothes the optimal coupling and makes the problem strongly convex. The entropic regularization converts the linear program into a smooth, strongly convex problem whose unique solution can be found by Sinkhorn-Knopp matrix scaling — a remarkably simple iterative algorithm that alternately normalizes the rows and columns of a kernel matrix. The resulting algorithm runs in \(O(n^2)\) iterations, each costing \(O(n^2)\) operations, and converges geometrically fast. The log-sum-exp trick from numerical analysis stabilizes the computation for small regularization parameters.

    The third chapter is Wasserstein Geodesics: Interpolation. With the Wasserstein distance in hand, the phase turns to the geometry of the Wasserstein manifold — the infinite-dimensional Riemannian manifold of probability distributions equipped with the Wasserstein-2 metric that you encountered at the end of Chapter 3 of Phase 0. The geodesics on this manifold are the displacement interpolations — the paths that move mass from one distribution to another along straight lines in the underlying space. The displacement interpolation is not the pixel-by-pixel average of two images — it is the image obtained by moving each pixel’s mass to the geometrically optimal destination, producing visually coherent morphing rather than blurry averaging. The Wasserstein barycenters — the Fréchet means of multiple distributions under the Wasserstein metric — extend this to averaging multiple distributions simultaneously, producing the geometric mean of a collection of shapes, images, or point clouds.

    By the end of Phase 1, you will have built, from scratch, the complete theory and computational implementation of Optimal Transport. The mathematics of Phase 0 was preparation. Phase 1 is the first use of that mathematics in full force — measure theory to define the coupling space, functional analysis and duality to derive the Kantorovich dual, differential geometry to interpret the Wasserstein manifold and its geodesics, and linear algebra to implement and analyze the Sinkhorn algorithm. Every chapter of Phase 0 finds its application in Phase 1.
