    Phase 3 - The Grand Synthesis: Generative AI and Flows

    Introduction

    Three phases of preparation are now complete. Phase 0 gave you the mathematical language: the measure theory that makes probability rigorous, the functional analysis that makes optimization over infinite-dimensional spaces tractable, the differential geometry that makes curved spaces computable, and the linear algebra that makes high-dimensional data manipulable. Phase 1 gave you the geometric engine: the Optimal Transport framework that measures the cost of transforming one probability distribution into another, the Sinkhorn algorithm that makes this computation tractable, and the Wasserstein geometry that turns the space of probability distributions into a metric space with its own geodesics and barycenters. Phase 2 gave you the architectural chassis: the equivariant neural network designs that respect the symmetry and geometry of structured data, from the \(S_n\)-equivariant message passing of graph neural networks through the \(SO(3)\)-equivariant molecular networks to the gauge-equivariant geodesic convolutions and Laplacian eigenmap embeddings of manifold-valued data.

    Phase 3 assembles the vehicle and makes it drive.

    The synthesis is not merely additive — not simply the combination of three previously separate tools. It is the discovery that Phase 1 and Phase 2 were always describing the same thing from two different angles. Phase 1 described how probability distributions move through the space of distributions — how mass flows from noise to data, measured by the Wasserstein metric, along paths called displacement interpolations. Phase 2 described how neural networks should be structured to respect the geometry of the data domain — equivariant to the symmetry group, faithful to the manifold structure, respectful of the curvature. The synthesis of Phase 3 is this: the neural networks of Phase 2 are the velocity fields that drive the probability flows of Phase 1, and the geometry of Phase 1’s Wasserstein space constrains the geometry of Phase 2’s equivariant architectures. The generative model is the vehicle; the Wasserstein geometry is the road; the equivariant neural network is the engine.

    The central problem that Phase 3 addresses is generative modeling: learning a probability distribution \(p_{\text{data}}\) from a finite set of samples, and then drawing new samples from the learned distribution. This is one of the fundamental problems of machine learning. It underlies image synthesis, protein structure prediction, drug molecule design, weather forecasting, and scientific simulation. The challenge is that \(p_{\text{data}}\) is a distribution over a high-dimensional space — images live in \(\mathbb{R}^{256\times256\times3}\), protein structures live in the space of backbone dihedral angles, drug molecules live in the space of molecular graphs — and directly parameterizing such a distribution is computationally impossible.

    The generative modeling revolution of the past decade has been built on a single insight: instead of parameterizing the distribution directly, parameterize the transformation from a simple distribution — typically a standard Gaussian \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) — to the data distribution. Learn the map, not the distribution. The map is a neural network. The training objective teaches the map to transport probability mass from the Gaussian to the data distribution. The generation procedure applies the learned map to a fresh Gaussian sample, producing a new data point.

    Phase 3 develops the three most mathematically principled families of such transformations, each emerging from a different perspective on the same underlying geometry.

    Phase 3 is organized into three chapters, corresponding to the three bullet points of the roadmap’s Phase 3 syllabus, each building on the previous.

    The first chapter is Gradient Flows in Wasserstein Space. This is the theoretical foundation of Phase 3 — the mathematical framework that shows how the training dynamics of generative models can be understood as gradient descent in the Wasserstein metric space of probability distributions. The central objects are the JKO scheme and the Fokker-Planck equation. The JKO scheme — named after Jordan, Kinderlehrer, and Otto, who established it in 1998 — is the variational principle that characterizes the evolution of a probability distribution under gradient flow in Wasserstein space: each step of the evolution is the solution to a proximal optimization problem that minimizes a functional of the distribution plus a Wasserstein regularization term penalizing large distributional steps. The Fokker-Planck equation is the partial differential equation that governs the time evolution of probability densities under stochastic dynamics — the continuous-time limit of the JKO scheme. The deep insight of this chapter is that training a neural generative model is not merely an optimization problem in the parameters of the neural network: it is a gradient flow in the infinite-dimensional space of probability distributions, governed by the Wasserstein geometry that Phase 1 built. The heat equation — the simplest diffusion process — is the gradient flow of entropy in Wasserstein space. Diffusion models are gradient flows of more complex functionals. Understanding this geometric picture is what separates a practitioner who uses diffusion models from a researcher who can derive them from first principles.

    The second chapter is Flow Matching and Diffusion. This is the computational heart of Phase 3 — the family of algorithms that implement Wasserstein gradient flows as trainable neural network objectives. The chapter covers three interconnected topics. Neural ODEs parameterize the transformation from noise to data as the solution of an ordinary differential equation \(dx/dt = v_t(x,\theta)\), where \(v_t\) is a time-dependent velocity field parameterized by a neural network — the continuous-time analogue of a normalizing flow. Score matching learns the score function \(\nabla\log p_t(x)\) — the gradient of the log-density, which points in the direction of increasing probability mass — by minimizing a denoising objective that does not require computing the intractable normalizing constant of \(p_t\). Rectified flows straighten the probability flow trajectories using Optimal Transport couplings between noise and data — applying the Phase 1 machinery of displacement interpolation directly to the problem of learning efficient generative paths, producing straight-line flows that are faster to simulate and easier to train than the curved paths of standard diffusion models. Together, these three topics give the complete algorithmic toolkit of modern generative modeling, derived from the geometric foundations of Phases 1 and 2.

    The third chapter is Riemannian Flow Matching. This is the frontier of Phase 3 — the extension of flow matching to data that lives on Riemannian manifolds rather than Euclidean space. For protein backbone generation, the relevant space is the torus \(\mathbb{T}^{2n}\) of backbone dihedral angles — a flat manifold with periodic boundary conditions. For molecular orientation generation, the relevant space is the rotation group \(SO(3)\) — a curved manifold whose geometry is exactly the Lie group structure of Chapter 1’s group theory. For general structural biology, the relevant space is a product of Euclidean spaces, tori, and rotation groups. Standard flow matching operates in Euclidean space and cannot be directly applied to these curved domains: straight lines do not stay on the manifold, Gaussian distributions are not well-defined on non-flat spaces, and the score function requires the Riemannian gradient rather than the Euclidean gradient. Riemannian flow matching extends the Neural ODE and flow matching frameworks to arbitrary Riemannian manifolds by replacing Euclidean straight-line interpolations with geodesic interpolations, Euclidean Gaussians with heat kernel distributions on the manifold, and Euclidean score functions with Riemannian score functions involving the Laplace-Beltrami operator from Phase 2’s Chapter 3. This is the chapter where every thread of the roadmap converges: the Wasserstein geometry of Phase 1, the Riemannian geometry of Phase 0, the equivariant architectures of Phase 2, and the spectral theory of the Laplace-Beltrami operator from Chapter 3 of Phase 2 all synthesize into a single coherent framework for generating structured geometric data that respects both the data’s symmetry group and its manifold structure.

    The trajectory of the entire roadmap is now visible in one glance. Phase 0 built the mathematical language. Phase 1 built the engine for moving distributions through distribution space. Phase 2 built the architectures for processing structured geometric data. Phase 3 uses the Phase 2 architectures as the velocity fields that drive the Phase 1 distribution flows, producing generative models that create new structured geometric data — molecules, proteins, 3D shapes — by learning to transport Gaussian noise to the data distribution along paths that respect the geometry and symmetry of the data domain. The car is assembled. Phase 3 makes it drive.

    The connections to your curriculum reach their maximum density in Phase 3, because the synthesis draws simultaneously on every mathematical thread your courses have developed. The Fokker-Planck equation is a PDE from MATH 217. The Wasserstein gradient flow is a variational problem from functional analysis in MATH 325. The score function is a gradient from multivariate calculus in MATH 201. The neural ODE is an ODE from MATH 217. The Riemannian flow matching uses geodesics from Phase 0’s differential geometry, which in turn uses the ODE theory of MATH 217 for the geodesic equation. The equivariant velocity field uses the representation theory of \(SO(3)\) from Phase 2’s Chapter 1. The Laplace-Beltrami spectral theory for heat kernels on manifolds uses the PDE theory of MATH 217 and the functional analysis of MATH 325. Phase 3 is not the end of the learning journey. It is the moment when every thread pulled taut and woven together.
