Topic 3 - The Weisfeiler-Lehman Isomorphism Test: The Theoretical Limits of GNN Expressive Power

Introduction

Topic 1 gave you the operational engine of graph neural networks — the message passing framework, derived from first principles as the unique \(S_n\)-equivariant architecture respecting sparse local graph structure. Topic 2 gave you the spectral foundation beneath it — the graph Fourier transform, the ChebNet polynomial approximation, and the GCN as a first-order low-pass filter derived from the Laplace-Beltrami operator. Both topics built upward: here is what GNNs can do, here is why they work, here is the mathematical machinery that makes them principled.

Topic 3 turns the question around. Not what can GNNs do — but what can they not do? Not how do they work — but where do they fail, and why, and with what mathematical precision can we characterize the boundary?

Every powerful tool has a fundamental limit. The Fourier transform cannot localize a signal simultaneously in time and frequency — the Heisenberg uncertainty principle is the precise quantification of that limit. The Turing machine cannot decide its own halting problem — Rice’s theorem is the precise quantification of that limit. The message passing GNN cannot distinguish certain pairs of non-isomorphic graphs — the Weisfeiler-Lehman test is the precise quantification of that limit. In each case, the limit is not a failure of engineering. It is a structural theorem about what the computational model can represent, proved mathematically and not escapable by clever implementation.

The Weisfeiler-Lehman test is a classical algorithm from combinatorics, developed in 1968 by Boris Weisfeiler and Andrei Lehman as a heuristic for the graph isomorphism problem — the computational problem of deciding whether two graphs are structurally identical up to node relabeling. The test works iteratively: assign initial color labels to nodes based on their features, then repeatedly update each node’s color by hashing together its current color and the multiset of its neighbors’ colors. If at any iteration the two graphs produce different color histograms, they are declared non-isomorphic. If the color histograms stabilize to the same distribution, the test declares the graphs potentially isomorphic — it cannot confirm isomorphism, only rule it out.

The connection between this 1968 combinatorics algorithm and modern deep learning was established in 2019 by Xu, Hu, Leskovec, and Jegelka in the paper “How Powerful are Graph Neural Networks?” The central theorem of that paper is precise and bilateral: any message-passing GNN is at most as powerful as the WL test at distinguishing non-isomorphic graphs, and there exists a specific GNN — the Graph Isomorphism Network, which you already met in Topic 1 — that achieves exactly that power. The WL test is simultaneously the ceiling and the target of message-passing expressiveness.

Understanding this theorem requires understanding three things in sequence, which are the three components of this topic.

The first component is the Weisfeiler-Lehman test itself — its algorithmic definition, its execution on concrete graphs, and the precise class of graphs it cannot distinguish. The WL test is not an abstraction invented for the purpose of analyzing GNNs. It is a concrete, executable algorithm with a specific computational history and a well-studied failure mode: there exist pairs of non-isomorphic graphs — graphs with genuinely different structure — that the WL test assigns identical color histograms to, and therefore cannot tell apart. The most important such pair is the class of regular graphs — graphs where every node has the same degree — where the WL test collapses to comparing degree sequences and cannot detect any structural differences among graphs with the same degree sequence. Understanding which graphs fool the WL test is equivalent to understanding which graph structures are invisible to any message-passing GNN.

The second component is the equivalence theorem — the precise statement and proof that any message-passing GNN is at most as powerful as the WL test, and that GIN achieves exactly WL power. The proof of the upper bound — that GNNs cannot exceed WL — proceeds by showing that two nodes receiving identical WL colorings at every iteration will also receive identical feature vectors from any message-passing GNN at every layer. The proof of the lower bound — that GIN achieves WL — proceeds by showing that GIN’s injective aggregation (sum over neighborhood features, applied through a universal function approximator) exactly simulates the WL color refinement procedure. Together, the two bounds give the tight characterization: message-passing GNNs occupy exactly the WL complexity class, no more and no less.

The third component is the hierarchy beyond WL — the \(k\)-dimensional WL test, higher-order GNNs, and the open landscape of expressiveness beyond the standard message-passing paradigm. The 1-WL test, which is what is usually meant by the WL test, has a natural generalization: the \(k\)-WL test colors \(k\)-tuples of nodes rather than individual nodes, with dramatically increased distinguishing power. The 3-WL test can distinguish any pair of graphs that the 1-WL test cannot, and so on up the hierarchy. The architectural counterpart — the \(k\)-order invariant graph network — achieves \(k\)-WL power, at the cost of \(O(n^k)\) computational complexity. Understanding the WL hierarchy gives you the complete map of the expressiveness landscape: where standard message-passing GNNs sit, what lies above them, and what the computational cost of climbing the hierarchy is.

The connection to your curriculum here runs through two courses simultaneously, and the connection is deeper than usual.

From your COMP 323 course on Graph Theory, you already know the graph isomorphism problem — the question of whether two graphs are structurally identical. You know that graph isomorphism is in NP but not known to be NP-complete, placing it in the rare and mysterious complexity class of problems that might be intermediate between P and NP-complete. The WL test is one of the classical polynomial-time heuristics for graph isomorphism — it solves the problem correctly for almost all graphs (the fraction of \(n\)-node graphs it fails on shrinks to zero as \(n\) grows) but fails on specific structured families. The GNN expressiveness result connects this classical combinatorics question directly to the representational capacity of neural networks — a connection that would have seemed surprising in 1968 but is, in retrospect, the inevitable consequence of the fact that GNNs are \(S_n\)-equivariant functions on graphs, and \(S_n\)-equivariant functions on graphs must respect the same structural distinctions that the WL test makes.

From your MCSC 201 course on Discrete Structures, you know the counting arguments that characterize the distinguishing power of color refinement algorithms — the combinatorics of multisets, the pigeonhole principle applied to color classes, and the recursive structure of tree-like computation. The WL test is precisely a color refinement algorithm, and its analysis uses exactly the discrete mathematics tools from that course: injectivity of multiset functions, the relationship between tree unfoldings of graphs and WL colorings, and the counting of equivalence classes under the WL refinement.

By the end of this topic, the Weisfeiler-Lehman test will feel not like an obscure combinatorics algorithm imported into machine learning from a 1968 paper, but like the inevitable theoretical boundary that the architecture of message-passing GNNs draws around itself. You will be able to look at any two graphs and determine, in principle, whether any message-passing GNN can distinguish them. You will understand exactly which structural properties of graphs are invisible to local neighborhood aggregation — and therefore which tasks require going beyond the standard GNN framework to higher-order architectures. The WL test is the mirror that shows message-passing GNNs their own reflection — and the reflection reveals both what they are and, precisely, what they are not.

Layer 1 (Story)

It is 2005. The Government of Nepal has launched a national census of mountain villages across the Himalayan districts. The census bureau in Kathmandu has hired two independent teams — Team A and Team B — to survey the same set of districts and produce structural reports on the village networks. Each team travels to the villages, maps the connections between them, records local features, and returns with a graph describing the social and geographic structure of the region.

The bureau chief receives two reports. Each report is a graph: nodes are villages, edges are roads connecting them, and each node carries a label describing the village type — agricultural, trading, or administrative. The bureau chief’s question is simple: did the two teams survey the same network, or are these two genuinely different networks that merely look similar at first glance?

This is the graph isomorphism problem. Not a machine learning problem. Not a neural network problem. A pure structural question: are these two graphs, possibly with different node numberings, actually the same graph up to relabeling?

Why isomorphism is hard.

The bureau chief tries the obvious approach: compare the two graphs directly. Graph A has twelve villages and fifteen roads. Graph B also has twelve villages and fifteen roads. Same size, same number of connections. Possibly the same graph — but possibly not. Two graphs can have the same number of nodes and edges and still be structurally different.

The chief tries harder: compare the degree sequences. In Graph A, three villages have degree two (two roads each), six have degree three, and three have degree four. Graph B has exactly the same degree sequence. Still possibly the same graph — but a degree sequence comparison cannot distinguish graphs with the same degree profile but different connection patterns.

The chief considers the exhaustive approach: try all possible bijections between the twelve villages of Graph A and the twelve villages of Graph B, checking whether any bijection makes the adjacency matrices match. There are \(12! = 479{,}001{,}600\) possible bijections. At one check per microsecond, this would take nearly eight minutes — for twelve villages. For a network of a thousand villages, \(1000!\) is a number with over two thousand digits. Exhaustive search is computationally dead.

The bureau chief needs a heuristic — a practical procedure that can efficiently rule out the possibility that two graphs are the same, even if it cannot always confirm they are the same.

The census fieldworker’s insight: iterative neighborhood coloring.

A young fieldworker on Team A proposes a clever procedure. Instead of comparing the graphs globally, compare them locally and iteratively. Assign each village an initial color based only on its own label — agricultural, trading, or administrative. Now, repeatedly refine the colors: at each round, a village’s new color is determined by its current color and the multiset of its neighbors’ current colors. Two villages in the same graph, or across the two graphs, receive the same color at round \(k\) if and only if their entire \(k\)-hop neighborhood structure looks identical.

The fieldworker calls this the color refinement procedure. After enough rounds, the color histogram of Graph A — how many villages of each color there are — is compared to the color histogram of Graph B. If the histograms ever differ, the two graphs are definitively different: no relabeling of one graph can produce the other. If the histograms stabilize to the same distribution, the two graphs might be the same — or they might not be, but the procedure cannot tell the difference.

This is the Weisfeiler-Lehman test. Let us watch it execute on a concrete example.

Round Zero: Initial coloring by village type.

The two graphs each have four villages for simplicity. In Graph A: village 1 is a trading post (color T), village 2 is agricultural (color A), village 3 is agricultural (color A), village 4 is a trading post (color T). The edges are: 1-2, 2-3, 3-4, and 4-1 — a four-cycle with alternating village types.

In Graph B: village 1 is a trading post (color T), village 2 is a trading post (color T), village 3 is agricultural (color A), village 4 is agricultural (color A). The edges are: 1-3, 3-2, 2-4, and 4-1 — also a four-cycle, but the village types are arranged differently.

Initial color histograms: both graphs have two T-colored villages and two A-colored villages. The histograms match at Round Zero.

Round One: Refine by neighborhood multisets.

In Graph A, village 1 (color T) has neighbors village 2 (A) and village 4 (T). Its neighborhood multiset is \(\{A, T\}\). Village 2 (color A) has neighbors village 1 (T) and village 3 (A). Its neighborhood multiset is \(\{T, A\} = \{A, T\}\). Village 3 (color A) has neighbors village 2 (A) and village 4 (T). Its neighborhood multiset is \(\{A, T\}\). Village 4 (color T) has neighbors village 3 (A) and village 1 (T). Its neighborhood multiset is \(\{A, T\}\).

Every village in Graph A has the same neighborhood multiset \(\{A, T\}\), so after Round One, every village receives the same new color — call it color \(c_1\) derived from the hash of (current color, \(\{A, T\}\)). But wait: village 1 has current color T and multiset \(\{A, T\}\), while village 2 has current color A and multiset \(\{A, T\}\). The new color is the hash of the pair (current color, neighborhood multiset), so T-villages get a different new color than A-villages even if their neighborhood multisets are the same. Village 1 gets new color \(\text{hash}(T, \{A,T\})\) and village 2 gets new color \(\text{hash}(A, \{A,T\})\).

After Round One in Graph A: villages 1 and 4 (originally T) both get new color \(c_T = \text{hash}(T, \{A,T\})\). Villages 2 and 3 (originally A) both get new color \(c_A = \text{hash}(A, \{A,T\})\). Color histogram: two villages with color \(c_T\), two with color \(c_A\).

In Graph B, village 1 (T) has neighbors village 3 (A) and village 4 (A). Neighborhood multiset: \(\{A, A\}\). Village 2 (T) has neighbors village 3 (A) and village 4 (A). Neighborhood multiset: \(\{A, A\}\). Village 3 (A) has neighbors village 1 (T) and village 2 (T). Neighborhood multiset: \(\{T, T\}\). Village 4 (A) has neighbors village 1 (T) and village 2 (T). Neighborhood multiset: \(\{T, T\}\).

After Round One in Graph B: villages 1 and 2 (originally T) get new color \(\text{hash}(T, \{A,A\})\). Villages 3 and 4 (originally A) get new color \(\text{hash}(A, \{T,T\})\).

Now compare the histograms. Graph A has colors \(\{c_T, c_A, c_A, c_T\}\) where \(c_T = \text{hash}(T, \{A,T\})\) and \(c_A = \text{hash}(A, \{A,T\})\). Graph B has colors \(\{\text{hash}(T,\{A,A\}), \text{hash}(T,\{A,A\}), \text{hash}(A,\{T,T\}), \text{hash}(A,\{T,T\})\}\). Since \(\{A,T\} \neq \{A,A\}\) and \(\{A,T\} \neq \{T,T\}\), the new colors in Graph A and Graph B are completely different. The histograms do not match. The WL test has successfully distinguished these two graphs after one round — they are definitively non-isomorphic.

When the WL test succeeds: the typical case.

In the vast majority of real-world graphs — sparse random graphs, molecular graphs, social networks — the WL color refinement converges to a stable coloring within a small number of rounds, and the final color histogram correctly identifies whether the two graphs are isomorphic. The WL test is fast: each round costs \(O(n + m)\) time (one pass over all nodes and edges), and convergence typically happens within \(O(n)\) rounds. For almost all graphs, it is correct.

The bureau chief uses the WL test throughout the census. Two village networks that differ in any local structural property — degree sequence, triangle count, cycle lengths, neighborhood diversity — will be caught by the WL test within a small number of rounds. The test becomes the standard tool for checking whether two submitted reports describe the same or different networks.

The failure case: when the WL test goes blind.

Several months into the census, a peculiar pair of graphs arrives at the bureau. Both describe networks of six villages. Both have the same number of roads. Both have every village connected to exactly three other villages — every village is a degree-three node. The bureau chief runs the WL test.

Round Zero: all villages receive the same initial color, because all villages have the same type — let us say all are trading posts. Color histogram: six villages, all color T. Both graphs: identical histograms.

Round One: every village has current color T and three neighbors, all of color T. Every village in both graphs has neighborhood multiset \(\{T, T, T\}\). Every village in both graphs receives the new color \(\text{hash}(T, \{T,T,T\})\). Color histograms: both graphs have six villages of the same new color. Still identical.

Round Two: every village now has color \(\text{hash}(T, \{T,T,T\})\) and three neighbors, all with the same color. Every village in both graphs has the same neighborhood multiset. Every village receives the same new color. The coloring has stabilized — no further refinement is possible.

The WL test declares the two graphs potentially isomorphic. But the bureau chief, suspicious, draws them out carefully. Graph A is the utility graph \(K_{3,3}\) — six villages split into two groups of three, with every village in the first group connected to every village in the second group, and no connections within a group. Graph B is the prism graph — two triangles (three-cycles) connected by three parallel edges, one edge between corresponding vertices of the two triangles.

These two graphs are genuinely, structurally different. \(K_{3,3}\) is bipartite — its villages can be two-colored with no two same-colored villages sharing a road. The prism graph is not bipartite — it contains odd cycles. They have completely different topological properties. But the WL test cannot see the difference, because the WL test only looks at rooted neighborhoods, and every node in both graphs has a locally identical rooted neighborhood up to any finite depth. The test has gone blind.

Why the WL test goes blind: the rooted tree unfolding argument.

Here is the precise reason. The WL test, at round \(k\), assigns each node a color that encodes its entire \(k\)-hop rooted neighborhood — the subgraph seen by a depth-\(k\) BFS tree rooted at that node. Two nodes receive the same color at round \(k\) if and only if their depth-\(k\) BFS trees are isomorphic as rooted labeled trees.

For the \(K_{3,3}\) and prism graph example: root any node in \(K_{3,3}\). The depth-1 neighborhood is three nodes, all of the same color. The depth-2 neighborhood adds the two remaining nodes in the same partition — each of the three depth-1 nodes is connected to both. The depth-3 neighborhood cycles back to the root’s partition. Now root any node in the prism graph and trace the same BFS tree. At every depth, the rooted neighborhood structure is identical — the same number of nodes at each level, the same connection pattern between levels — because both graphs are 3-regular with the same girth properties at the local level. The WL test cannot see the global topology — it can only see local trees, and locally these two graphs look the same.

This is the fundamental limitation. The WL test is a local test. It sees trees, not cycles. It sees neighborhoods, not global structure. It cannot detect: whether a graph is bipartite, how many triangles a graph contains, whether a graph is planar, the length of the shortest odd cycle, or any other property that requires global structural information. These invisible properties define the boundary of what any message-passing GNN can compute — because, as the equivalence theorem will show, any message-passing GNN is exactly as blind as the WL test.

The connection to message passing: the census fieldworker sees the parallel.

The young fieldworker who proposed the color refinement procedure looks at the message passing framework from Topic 1 and immediately recognizes what she has built.

In Round \(k\) of the WL test, each village computes its new color by hashing its current color with the multiset of its neighbors’ current colors. In Layer \(k\) of a message passing GNN, each node computes its new feature vector by aggregating its neighbors’ current feature vectors and updating with its own current feature. The procedures are structurally identical: both propagate information one hop per round, both combine a node’s own information with an aggregated summary of its neighborhood, both produce a representation that encodes the entire \(k\)-hop rooted neighborhood after \(k\) rounds.

The difference is in the representation: the WL test uses discrete color labels (from a hash function), while the GNN uses continuous feature vectors (from learned neural network functions). The WL test’s hash function is injective by design — distinct inputs always produce distinct outputs, guaranteed. The GNN’s aggregation function may or may not be injective, depending on the specific choice of message, aggregation, and update functions. If the GNN’s aggregation is injective — if distinct neighborhood multisets always produce distinct aggregated features — then the GNN is at least as powerful as the WL test at distinguishing nodes. If the aggregation is not injective — if two different neighborhood multisets can produce the same aggregated feature — then the GNN is strictly weaker than the WL test.

This is the key insight of the 2019 theorem: the expressive power of a message-passing GNN is determined entirely by whether its aggregation function is injective on neighborhood multisets. Sum aggregation with a universal function approximator is injective — it is GIN, and it achieves exactly WL power. Mean aggregation is not injective — it collapses neighborhoods of different sizes with the same element-wise average to the same representation. Max aggregation is not injective — it collapses neighborhoods with the same maximum but different lower elements. The break moment from Topic 1’s aggregation discussion is now revealed to be not just a practical concern about counting neighbors, but the precise point at which a GNN either reaches or falls below the WL ceiling.

What lies above the WL ceiling: the higher-order census.

The bureau chief, frustrated by the WL test’s blindness to global structure, commissions a more powerful census procedure. Instead of assigning colors to individual villages, assign colors to pairs of villages — every ordered pair \((i, j)\) receives a color encoding the local structure around that pair. Then refine: update each pair’s color based on the multisets of colors of pairs sharing a village with it. This is the 2-dimensional WL test, or 2-WL. It is strictly more powerful than 1-WL: pairs of graphs that fool 1-WL can often be distinguished by 2-WL, because pair colorings encode edge-level structural information that node colorings cannot see.

The 3-WL test colors triples of villages. The \(k\)-WL test colors \(k\)-tuples. Each level of the hierarchy is strictly more powerful than the previous, at the cost of \(O(n^k)\) memory and computation — coloring all \(n^k\) possible \(k\)-tuples is expensive. The \(k\)-WL test can distinguish any two graphs that the \((k-1)\)-WL test cannot, for all \(k \geq 2\). The hierarchy is strict and infinite.

The architectural counterpart is the \(k\)-order invariant graph network of Maron et al. (2019), which uses tensor features indexed by \(k\)-tuples of nodes and linear layers that are \(S_n\)-equivariant with respect to simultaneous permutation of all \(k\) indices. These networks achieve \(k\)-WL expressive power, with the Bell number \(B_{2k}\) parameter count from Chapter 1’s Schur’s lemma analysis giving the dimension of the equivariant layer space. The census hierarchy — from individual villages to pairs to triples to \(k\)-tuples — is the expressiveness hierarchy of graph networks, from message-passing GNNs at the bottom to full \(k\)-order networks climbing toward graph isomorphism.

The landscape of invisible graph properties.

Hold the following map clearly before the mathematics. The WL test — and therefore any message-passing GNN — cannot detect:

Triangle counts: two graphs with the same degree sequence but different numbers of triangles can be WL-identical if their local tree neighborhoods look the same. Triangle counting requires seeing three-cycles, which are global structures invisible to rooted tree unfoldings.

Cycle lengths: the length of the shortest cycle in a graph (its girth) can differ between two WL-identical graphs. The \(K_{3,3}\) versus prism example shows exactly this — \(K_{3,3}\) has girth 4, the prism has girth 3, but WL cannot distinguish them.

Planarity: whether a graph can be drawn in the plane without crossing edges is a global topological property invisible to local neighborhood structure.

Bipartiteness: whether a graph’s nodes can be two-colored with no monochromatic edge — which the \(K_{3,3}\) versus prism example demonstrates directly.

Graph diameter: the longest shortest path between any two nodes in the graph requires global information that \(K\)-layer message passing can only approximate for \(K\) at least the diameter.

Each invisible property is a task that no message-passing GNN — regardless of depth, width, or choice of aggregation — can solve correctly on all graphs. These are not failures of training or architecture choice. They are information-theoretic impossibilities, as hard as asking a person to count the floors of a building they cannot see by interviewing only their immediate neighbors.

The WL test and its equivalence to message-passing GNNs is the theorem that places all of these impossibilities under one roof — a single, clean theoretical boundary that tells you exactly where the message-passing paradigm ends and where higher-order methods must begin.

Layer 2 (Mathematics)

We build the formalism in five stages: the precise algorithmic definition of the 1-WL test and its convergence properties, the graph isomorphism partial order and WL equivalence classes, the expressiveness theorem and its two-directional proof, the injectivity characterization of aggregation functions, and the \(k\)-WL hierarchy with its architectural counterparts.

The 1-WL Color Refinement Algorithm

Definition (Graph isomorphism). Two graphs \(G = (V_G, E_G, X_G)\) and \(H = (V_H, E_H, X_H)\) with node feature matrices \(X_G, X_H \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}\) are isomorphic, written \(G \cong H\), if there exists a bijection \(\phi: V_G \to V_H\) such that:

\[(u, v) \in E_G \Leftrightarrow (\phi(u), \phi(v)) \in E_H \quad \text{and} \quad X_G[u] = X_H[\phi(u)] \quad \forall u \in V_G\]

Graph isomorphism is in NP, not known to be in P or NP-complete — it occupies the rare intermediate complexity class GI.

Definition (1-WL test). The 1-dimensional Weisfeiler-Lehman color refinement algorithm operates on a graph \(G = (V, E, X)\) as follows.

Initialization: Assign initial colors based on node features:

\[c_v^{(0)} = \text{hash}(X[v]) \quad \forall v \in V\]

For graphs without node features, \(c_v^{(0)} = \text{hash}(\deg(v))\) or a uniform constant.

Refinement: For \(t = 1, 2, \ldots\) iterate:

\[c_v^{(t)} = \text{hash}\!\left(c_v^{(t-1)},\, \{\!\{ c_u^{(t-1)} : u \in \mathcal{N}(v) \}\!\}\right)\]

where \(\{\!\{ \cdot \}\!\}\) denotes a multiset (a set allowing repeated elements) and \(\text{hash}\) is an injective function mapping distinct inputs to distinct outputs.

Termination: The algorithm terminates at iteration \(T^*\) when the color partition stabilizes:

\[\{\!\{ c_v^{(T^*)} : v \in V \}\!\} = \{\!\{ c_v^{(T^*-1)} : v \in V \}\!\}\]

as multisets — no node changes color. For a graph with \(n\) nodes, convergence occurs within \(T^* \leq n\) iterations.

WL test decision: Given two graphs \(G\) and \(H\), the 1-WL test runs color refinement on both simultaneously. At each iteration \(t\): - If \(\{\!\{ c_v^{(t)} : v \in V_G \}\!\} \neq \{\!\{ c_v^{(t)} : v \in V_H \}\!\}\) as multisets: declare \(G \not\cong H\) (non-isomorphic). Terminate. - If both colorings have stabilized with equal histograms: declare \(G \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} H\) (WL-indistinguishable). Terminate.

Computational complexity. Each refinement iteration costs \(O((n + m)\log n)\) with appropriate hashing. Total: \(O(n(n+m)\log n)\), polynomial in graph size.

WL Equivalence and the Rooted Subtree Characterization

Definition (WL equivalence). Two graphs \(G\) and \(H\) are WL-equivalent, written \(G \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} H\), if the 1-WL test fails to distinguish them — their color histograms stabilize to identical multisets. Two nodes \(v \in V_G\) and \(u \in V_H\) are WL-equivalent, written \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\), if they receive identical color sequences \(c_v^{(t)} = c_u^{(t)}\) for all \(t \geq 0\).

Theorem (Rooted subtree characterization). Two nodes \(v \in V_G\) and \(u \in V_H\) satisfy \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(t\) iterations if and only if their rooted subtrees of depth \(t\) are isomorphic as rooted labeled trees.

Definition (Rooted subtree). The rooted subtree \(T_v^{(t)}\) of depth \(t\) at node \(v\) is the tree obtained by unrolling the BFS computation from \(v\) to depth \(t\): the root is \(v\) with label \(c_v^{(0)}\); the children of each node \(w\) at depth \(d < t\) are copies of \(w\)’s neighbors in \(G\), with labels \(c_{\cdot}^{(0)}\); cycles in \(G\) are broken by treating already-visited nodes as leaves.

Proof sketch. By induction on \(t\). Base case: \(c_v^{(0)} = \text{hash}(X[v])\), so \(c_v^{(0)} = c_u^{(0)}\) iff \(X[v] = X[u]\) iff the depth-0 trees (single labeled nodes) are isomorphic. Inductive step: \(c_v^{(t)} = \text{hash}(c_v^{(t-1)}, \{\!\{c_w^{(t-1)} : w \in \mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\})\). Since hash is injective, \(c_v^{(t)} = c_u^{(t)}\) iff \(c_v^{(t-1)} = c_u^{(t-1)}\) and \(\{\!\{c_w^{(t-1)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\} = \{\!\{c_w^{(t-1)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(u)\}\!\}\). By the inductive hypothesis, the first condition means depth-\((t-1)\) trees at \(v\) and \(u\) are isomorphic, and the second means the multisets of depth-\((t-1)\) trees of their neighbors are equal as multisets — exactly the condition for isomorphism of the depth-\(t\) trees. \(\square\)

Corollary. The 1-WL test cannot distinguish graphs that have the same multiset of rooted subtrees at every depth. Any graph property that cannot be expressed as a function of rooted subtree multisets is invisible to 1-WL.

The GNN Expressiveness Theorem

Setup. A message-passing GNN with \(K\) layers computes node representations via:

\[h_v^{(0)} = X[v]\]

\[h_v^{(k)} = \phi^{(k)}\!\left(h_v^{(k-1)},\, f^{(k)}\!\left(\{\!\{ h_u^{(k-1)} : u \in \mathcal{N}(v) \}\!\}\right)\right)\]

where \(\phi^{(k)}\) is the update function and \(f^{(k)}\) is the aggregation function over the neighborhood multiset. The graph-level representation is:

\[h_G = \rho\!\left(\{\!\{ h_v^{(K)} : v \in V \}\!\}\right)\]

for some permutation-invariant readout \(\rho\).

Theorem (Upper bound — Xu et al. 2019). Let \(\mathcal{A}\) be any message-passing GNN. If the 1-WL test determines that two graphs \(G\) and \(H\) are non-isomorphic, then \(\mathcal{A}\) maps \(G\) and \(H\) to different graph-level representations: \(h_G^{\mathcal{A}} \neq h_H^{\mathcal{A}}\). Equivalently: if \(G \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} H\) then \(h_G^{\mathcal{A}} = h_H^{\mathcal{A}}\) for any MPNN \(\mathcal{A}\).

Proof. We show by induction on \(k\) that if \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(k\) iterations, then \(h_v^{(k)} = h_u^{(k)}\) for any MPNN.

Base case (\(k=0\)): \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after 0 iterations iff \(c_v^{(0)} = c_u^{(0)}\) iff \(X[v] = X[u]\) iff \(h_v^{(0)} = X[v] = X[u] = h_u^{(0)}\). \(\checkmark\)

Inductive step: Assume \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(k\) iterations implies \(h_v^{(k)} = h_u^{(k)}\). Suppose \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(k+1\) iterations. Then: 1. \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(k\) iterations \(\Rightarrow h_v^{(k)} = h_u^{(k)}\) by inductive hypothesis. 2. \(\{\!\{c_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\} = \{\!\{c_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(u)\}\!\}\) since \(c_{v}^{(k+1)} = c_u^{(k+1)}\) requires equal neighbor color multisets. 3. By the inductive hypothesis applied to each neighbor, \(\{\!\{h_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\} = \{\!\{h_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(u)\}\!\}\) as multisets. 4. Therefore \(f^{(k+1)}(\{\!\{h_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\}) = f^{(k+1)}(\{\!\{h_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(u)\}\!\})\). 5. Therefore \(h_v^{(k+1)} = \phi^{(k+1)}(h_v^{(k)}, f^{(k+1)}(\cdots)) = \phi^{(k+1)}(h_u^{(k)}, f^{(k+1)}(\cdots)) = h_u^{(k+1)}\). \(\square\)

The contrapositive: if \(h_v^{(k)} \neq h_u^{(k)}\) for some MPNN, then \(v \not\stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) — the GNN can only distinguish nodes that WL distinguishes. MPNNs are at most as powerful as 1-WL.

The Injectivity Characterization

Lemma (Xu et al. 2019 — Characterization of injective multiset functions). Let \(\mathcal{X}\) be a countable set. A function \(f: \{\!\{\cdots\}\!\}_{\mathcal{X}} \to \mathbb{R}^d\) over multisets from \(\mathcal{X}\) is injective (maps distinct multisets to distinct outputs) if and only if there exists a function \(\phi: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^d\) such that:

\[f(\{\!\{x_1,\ldots,x_k\}\!\}) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \phi(x_i)\]

Proof.

\((\Leftarrow)\) If \(f(X) = \sum_{x\in X}\phi(x)\) with \(\phi\) injective on a countable domain embeddable in \(\mathbb{R}^d\), then for any two distinct multisets \(X \neq Y\), there exists an element \(x\) present in \(X\) with different multiplicity than in \(Y\). Since \(\phi(x)\) is nonzero and the sum tracks multiplicities, \(\sum_{x\in X}\phi(x) \neq \sum_{x\in Y}\phi(x)\).

\((\Rightarrow)\) Mean aggregation fails injectivity: \(f_{\text{mean}}(\{\!\{1,1\}\!\}) = 1 = f_{\text{mean}}(\{\!\{1\}\!\})\) — a two-element multiset and a one-element multiset with the same element produce the same mean. Max aggregation fails: \(f_{\text{max}}(\{\!\{1,2\}\!\}) = 2 = f_{\text{max}}(\{\!\{2\}\!\})\). Sum preserves both element values and multiplicities: \(\sum_{x\in\{\!\{1,1\}\!\}}\phi(x) = 2\phi(1) \neq \phi(1) = \sum_{x\in\{\!\{1\}\!\}}\phi(x)\) for \(\phi(1)\neq 0\). \(\square\)

Corollary (Failure modes of mean and max aggregation).

Mean aggregation: \(f_{\text{mean}}(\{\!\{a, a\}\!\}) = a = f_{\text{mean}}(\{\!\{a\}\!\})\) — cannot distinguish multisets differing only in element multiplicity. It represents the ratio of features, not the count.

Max aggregation: \(f_{\text{max}}(\{\!\{a, b\}\!\}) = \max(a,b) = f_{\text{max}}(\{\!\{a, b, b\}\!\})\) — cannot distinguish multisets with the same range but different element frequencies.

Sum aggregation: \(f_{\text{sum}}(\{\!\{x_1,\ldots,x_k\}\!\}) = \sum_i \phi(x_i)\) — injective on multisets from a countable space when \(\phi\) is injective, because it preserves both values and multiplicities.

The Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN)

Theorem (Lower bound — Xu et al. 2019). There exists a message-passing GNN that is as powerful as the 1-WL test: two graphs \(G \not\stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} H\) implies \(h_G^{\text{GIN}} \neq h_H^{\text{GIN}}\).

Construction: GIN layer.

\[h_v^{(k)} = \text{MLP}^{(k)}\!\left((1 + \epsilon^{(k)}) \cdot h_v^{(k-1)} + \sum_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} h_u^{(k-1)}\right)\]

where \(\epsilon^{(k)} \in \mathbb{R}\) is a learnable (or fixed) scalar and \(\text{MLP}^{(k)}\) is a multilayer perceptron.

Why \((1+\epsilon)h_v + \sum_{u\in\mathcal{N}(v)}h_u\) is injective on \((h_v, \{\!\{h_u\}\!\})\).

The combined aggregation \((h_v^{(k-1)}, \{\!\{h_u^{(k-1)}: u\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\}) \mapsto (1+\epsilon)h_v^{(k-1)} + \sum_{u\in\mathcal{N}(v)}h_u^{(k-1)}\) is injective when \(\epsilon \neq -1\): the term \((1+\epsilon)h_v^{(k-1)}\) ensures the self-feature contributes distinctly from the neighborhood sum (which has coefficient \(1\)), and the sum aggregation is injective on neighborhood multisets by the lemma above.

Proof of the lower bound. We show by induction on \(k\) that if \(v \not\stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(k\) iterations, then \(h_v^{(k)} \neq h_u^{(k)}\) in GIN.

Base case (\(k=0\)): \(v \not\stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after 0 iterations iff \(X[v] \neq X[u]\) iff \(h_v^{(0)} = X[v] \neq X[u] = h_u^{(0)}\). \(\checkmark\)

Inductive step: Suppose \(v \not\stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(k+1\) iterations. Then either:

  1. \(v \not\stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(k\) iterations: by inductive hypothesis \(h_v^{(k)} \neq h_u^{(k)}\), so \((1+\epsilon)h_v^{(k)} + \sum_w h_w^{(k)} \neq (1+\epsilon)h_u^{(k)} + \sum_w h_w^{(k)}\), and since MLP is injective on its argument (for an MLP of sufficient width and depth, by the universal approximation theorem), \(h_v^{(k+1)} \neq h_u^{(k+1)}\).

  2. \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(k\) iterations but \(\{\!\{c_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\} \neq \{\!\{c_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(u)\}\!\}\): by the inductive hypothesis, the neighborhood multisets \(\{\!\{h_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\} \neq \{\!\{h_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(u)\}\!\}\). By the injectivity lemma, \(\sum_{w\in\mathcal{N}(v)}h_w^{(k)} \neq \sum_{w\in\mathcal{N}(u)}h_w^{(k)}\), so \((1+\epsilon)h_v^{(k)} + \sum_{w\in\mathcal{N}(v)}h_w^{(k)} \neq (1+\epsilon)h_u^{(k)} + \sum_{w\in\mathcal{N}(u)}h_w^{(k)}\), and MLP injectivity gives \(h_v^{(k+1)} \neq h_u^{(k+1)}\). \(\square\)

GIN readout. For graph-level tasks, GIN uses sum readout at every layer and concatenates across layers:

\[h_G^{\text{GIN}} = \text{CONCAT}\!\left(\sum_{v\in V} h_v^{(k)} \,:\, k = 0, 1, \ldots, K\right)\]

This multi-scale readout captures structural information at every hop depth simultaneously — analogous to jumping knowledge connections — preventing the loss of fine-grained structural information at early layers.

Specific Graph Structures Invisible to 1-WL

Theorem (Triangle invisibility). There exist pairs of graphs \(G \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} H\) where \(G\) contains triangles and \(H\) does not.

Construction. The cycle graph \(C_6\) (six nodes in a ring) and the disjoint union of two triangles \(2K_3\) are WL-indistinguishable but not isomorphic: \(C_6\) has no triangles; \(2K_3\) has two.

WL indistinguishability: In \(C_6\), every node has degree 2 and two neighbors of degree 2. In \(2K_3\), every node has degree 2 and two neighbors of degree 2. The WL refinement produces identical color sequences at every iteration for both graphs. \(\square\)

Theorem (Regular graph indistinguishability). Any two \(r\)-regular graphs on \(n\) nodes (all nodes have degree \(r\)) with the same number of nodes are WL-indistinguishable if they have the same initial node features.

Proof. At round 0: all nodes have the same color (uniform features). At round 1: every node has \(r\) neighbors all of the same color — identical neighborhood multiset \(\{\!\{c^{(0)}, \ldots, c^{(0)}\}\!\}\) (\(r\) times). All nodes receive the same new color. Stabilization occurs at round 1. Both graphs have identical color histograms: \(n\) nodes of the same color. \(\square\)

Corollary. No message-passing GNN can correctly compute triangle counts, cycle lengths, or determine bipartiteness on all graphs.

The \(k\)-WL Hierarchy

Definition (\(k\)-WL test). The \(k\)-dimensional Weisfeiler-Lehman test operates on \(k\)-tuples of nodes rather than individual nodes.

Initialization: For each \(k\)-tuple \((v_1,\ldots,v_k) \in V^k\):

\[c_{(v_1,\ldots,v_k)}^{(0)} = \text{hash}(\text{isomorphism type of the induced subgraph on } \{v_1,\ldots,v_k\})\]

Refinement: For \(j = 1,\ldots,k\), define the \(j\)-th neighborhood of \((v_1,\ldots,v_k)\) as:

\[\mathcal{N}_j(v_1,\ldots,v_k) = \{(v_1,\ldots,v_{j-1}, w, v_{j+1},\ldots,v_k) : w \in V\}\]

the set of \(k\)-tuples obtained by replacing the \(j\)-th component with any node \(w\). The refinement is:

\[c_{(v_1,\ldots,v_k)}^{(t)} = \text{hash}\!\left(c_{(v_1,\ldots,v_k)}^{(t-1)},\, \left(\{\!\{ c_{\mathbf{v}'}^{(t-1)} : \mathbf{v}' \in \mathcal{N}_1(\mathbf{v}) \}\!\},\ldots,\{\!\{ c_{\mathbf{v}'}^{(t-1)} : \mathbf{v}' \in \mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{v}) \}\!\}\right)\right)\]

Theorem (Strict hierarchy). For all \(k \geq 1\): \((k+1)\)-WL is strictly more powerful than \(k\)-WL. There exist pairs of graphs distinguishable by \((k+1)\)-WL but not by \(k\)-WL.

Complexity. \(k\)-WL requires \(O(n^k)\) memory and \(O(n^{k+1})\) time per iteration — exponential growth in \(k\).

The \(k\)-order Invariant Graph Network (Maron et al. 2019). The \(k\)-IGN uses tensor features \(T \in (\mathbb{R}^n)^{\otimes k}\) and linear layers that are \(S_n\)-equivariant with respect to simultaneous permutation of all \(k\) indices. By Schur’s lemma:

\[\dim\!\left(\text{Hom}_{S_n}\!\left((\mathbb{R}^n)^{\otimes k}, (\mathbb{R}^n)^{\otimes k}\right)\right) = B_{2k}\]

where \(B_{2k}\) is the Bell number — the number of set partitions of \(\{1,\ldots,2k\}\). For \(k=1\): \(B_2 = 2\) parameters (the \(\alpha I + \beta\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top\) from Schur’s lemma). For \(k=2\): \(B_4 = 15\) parameters. For \(k=3\): \(B_6 = 203\) parameters.

Theorem (Maron et al. 2019). The \(k\)-IGN is as powerful as the \(k\)-WL test.

Practical Expressiveness Beyond 1-WL: Structural Features

Definition (Structural node features). Since 1-WL cannot detect certain graph properties, these properties can be precomputed and injected as additional node features:

Cycle membership: \(\mathbf{1}[v \text{ is on a cycle of length } \ell]\) for \(\ell = 3, 4, 5, \ldots\)

Subgraph counts: Number of triangles, four-cycles, or other subgraphs containing \(v\).

Random Walk features: \([\mathbf{P}^k]_{vv}\) for \(k = 1,\ldots,K\) where \(\mathbf{P} = D^{-1}A\) is the random walk transition matrix. The diagonal entry \([\mathbf{P}^k]_{vv}\) is the probability of returning to \(v\) after \(k\) steps — this encodes local cycle structure invisible to standard WL.

Distance encoding: \(\text{dist}(v, u)\) for all pairs — position-aware GNNs inject pairwise distances as edge features, breaking the locality barrier.

Theorem (Random walk features detect cycles). The random walk return probability \([\mathbf{P}^3]_{vv} = \sum_{u,w\in\mathcal{N}(v)} A_{uw}/(d_v^2 d_u)\) is proportional to the number of triangles containing \(v\) — it detects triangle structure invisible to 1-WL by encoding global cycle information as a local node feature.

WHY THIS MATTERS

In MCSC 201 (Discrete Structures), the multiset is a foundational combinatorial object — a generalization of a set allowing repeated elements, with equality defined by both element identity and multiplicity. The injectivity lemma’s core claim — that sum aggregation is the unique injective function on multisets from a countable space — is a theorem in combinatorics: the generating function \(\sum_{x\in X}\phi(x)\) encodes a multiset \(X\) as a polynomial with coefficients counting multiplicities, and two distinct multisets produce distinct polynomials when \(\phi\) maps elements to linearly independent values. The pigeonhole principle from Discrete Structures underlies the WL convergence guarantee: there are at most \(n\) distinct colors possible in an \(n\)-node graph (since colors are derived from \(n\) initial node features), so the color partition can refine at most \(n\) times before stabilizing — giving the \(O(n)\) iteration bound.

In COMP 323 (Graph Theory), the graph isomorphism problem is the canonical example of a problem in the mysterious complexity class GI — believed harder than P but no harder than NP, with no known polynomial algorithm and no known NP-hardness proof. The WL test is the most practically successful polynomial-time heuristic for graph isomorphism, correct on almost all graphs but failing on specific structured families including strongly regular graphs, Cayley graphs of abelian groups, and other highly symmetric objects. The Cai-Fürer-Immerman theorem (1992) proves that for every \(k\), the \(k\)-WL test fails to distinguish certain pairs of non-isomorphic graphs — the WL hierarchy is infinite and never reaches a complete graph isomorphism algorithm. This is the graph-theoretic counterpart of the impossibility results in computational complexity that your COMP 314 (Algorithm and Complexity) course develops.

In COMP 472 (Artificial Intelligence), the expressive power of neural networks is usually discussed in terms of the universal approximation theorem — an MLP of sufficient width can approximate any continuous function on a compact set. The WL expressiveness theorem is the graph-structured analogue: GIN with sufficient width MLPs can approximate any function on graphs that is computable by the 1-WL test, but no wider or deeper GIN can compute functions that 1-WL cannot distinguish. The boundary is not about approximation capacity — it is about the information available to the computation. A GNN cannot approximate a function it cannot even in principle distinguish the inputs of: two WL-equivalent graphs will always receive the same GNN output, regardless of the network’s width, depth, or training.

Layer 3 (Bridge)

Every element of the Government of Nepal census story has a precise mathematical address. We walk through them completely — the bureau chief’s isomorphism question, the fieldworker’s color refinement procedure, the four-cycle example, the \(K_{3,3}\) versus prism failure, the rooted subtree argument, the connection to message passing, and the hierarchy beyond WL — until the story and the formalism are a single object seen from two angles.

The bureau chief’s question — did the two census teams survey the same network, or are these genuinely different networks — is the graph isomorphism problem: does there exist a bijection \(\phi: V_G \to V_H\) preserving both edge structure and node features? The bijection \(\phi\) is the relabeling that maps one team’s village numbering to the other’s. The condition \((u,v)\in E_G \Leftrightarrow (\phi(u),\phi(v))\in E_H\) is the requirement that roads are preserved — if villages \(u\) and \(v\) are connected in Graph A, then their images \(\phi(u)\) and \(\phi(v)\) must be connected in Graph B. The condition \(X_G[u] = X_H[\phi(u)]\) is the requirement that village types are preserved — agricultural villages map to agricultural villages, trading posts to trading posts. Graph isomorphism is in NP because verifying a proposed bijection takes polynomial time, but finding one — or proving none exists — has no known polynomial algorithm. This is the precise complexity statement behind the bureau chief’s frustration: the exhaustive search over \(n!\) bijections is the only guaranteed method, and it is computationally dead for any non-trivial graph size.

The bureau chief’s three preliminary checks — node count, edge count, degree sequence — are three necessary but not sufficient conditions for graph isomorphism, each ruling out non-isomorphism without confirming it. Isomorphic graphs must have the same number of nodes (\(|V_G| = |V_H|\)), the same number of edges (\(|E_G| = |E_H|\)), and the same degree sequence (the multiset \(\{\!\{d_v : v \in V_G\}\!\} = \{\!\{d_v : v \in V_H\}\!\}\)). Each check is a graph invariant — a function of the graph that is the same for isomorphic graphs. The WL test is a stronger graph invariant: a function of the graph that the isomorphism-preserving bijection cannot change, capturing more structural information than degree sequences alone. But no known polynomial-time computable graph invariant is complete — the WL hierarchy approaches but never reaches a complete invariant, by the Cai-Fürer-Immerman theorem.

The fieldworker’s color refinement procedure — assign initial colors by village type, then repeatedly update each village’s color by hashing its current color with the multiset of its neighbors’ current colors — is the 1-WL algorithm with initialization \(c_v^{(0)} = \text{hash}(X[v])\) and refinement \(c_v^{(t)} = \text{hash}(c_v^{(t-1)}, \{\!\{c_u^{(t-1)}: u\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\})\). The hash function in the story — the procedure that assigns a new color to each (current color, neighborhood multiset) pair — is the injective hash function in the formal definition. Injectivity is essential: if two villages have different (current color, neighborhood multiset) pairs, they must receive different new colors. Without injectivity, the color refinement loses information — it fails to distinguish villages whose neighborhoods are genuinely different, making the test weaker than it should be. The multiset \(\{\!\{c_u^{(t-1)}: u\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\}\) rather than the set \(\{c_u^{(t-1)}: u\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\) is essential: a village with two trading-post neighbors must be distinguished from a village with one trading-post neighbor, and the multiset records multiplicities while the set does not. The termination condition — stabilization of the color histogram — occurs within at most \(n\) iterations by the pigeonhole principle from MCSC 201: there are at most \(n\) distinct colors derivable from \(n\) initial node features, so the color partition can refine at most \(n\) times before no further splitting is possible.

The four-cycle example — Graph A with alternating T-A-A-T coloring and Graph B with T-T-A-A coloring, distinguished after Round One — is an execution of the 1-WL test where the neighborhood multisets \(\{A,T\}\) in Graph A and \(\{A,A\}\) or \(\{T,T\}\) in Graph B produce distinct new colors, giving non-equal color histograms. The computation \(c_v^{(1)} = \text{hash}(c_v^{(0)}, \{\!\{c_u^{(0)}: u\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\})\) for node 1 in Graph A gives \(\text{hash}(T, \{\!\{A,T\}\!\})\), while for node 1 in Graph B gives \(\text{hash}(T, \{\!\{A,A\}\!\})\). Since \(\{\!\{A,T\}\!\} \neq \{\!\{A,A\}\!\}\) and the hash is injective, these are different colors. Graph A’s histogram after Round One is \(\{c_T, c_A, c_A, c_T\}\) with \(c_T = \text{hash}(T,\{\!\{A,T\}\!\})\) and \(c_A = \text{hash}(A,\{\!\{A,T\}\!\})\); Graph B’s histogram is \(\{\text{hash}(T,\{\!\{A,A\}\!\}), \text{hash}(T,\{\!\{A,A\}\!\}), \text{hash}(A,\{\!\{T,T\}\!\}), \text{hash}(A,\{\!\{T,T\}\!\})\}\). The histograms differ, so the WL test correctly declares these graphs non-isomorphic. The story’s Round One is the formal algorithm’s \(t=1\) iteration, and the histogram comparison is the multiset equality check \(\{\!\{c_v^{(1)}: v\in V_G\}\!\} \neq \{\!\{c_v^{(1)}: v\in V_H\}\!\}\).

The \(K_{3,3}\) versus prism graph failure — both 3-regular graphs on six nodes where the WL test stabilizes after one round with identical color histograms — is the formal instance of WL-equivalence \(K_{3,3} \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} \text{prism}\) despite \(K_{3,3} \not\cong \text{prism}\). The regularity theorem makes this precise: in any \(r\)-regular graph with uniform initial features, Round Zero assigns all nodes the same color, Round One assigns all nodes the same color (every node has \(r\) neighbors all of the same color, giving neighborhood multiset \(\{\!\{c^{(0)},\ldots,c^{(0)}\}\!\}\) with \(r\) copies, identical for every node), and the coloring has stabilized. Both graphs produce the color histogram \(\{n \text{ nodes of color } c^{(1)}\}\) — identical for any two \(r\)-regular graphs on \(n\) nodes with uniform initial features. The WL test declares them indistinguishable, but \(K_{3,3}\) is bipartite (contains no odd cycles, girth 4) while the prism graph is not bipartite (contains triangles, girth 3). Bipartiteness is a global topological property requiring the detection of odd cycles — structural information that rooted subtree unfoldings cannot encode because short cycles in the graph become long paths in the unrolled tree, indistinguishable from trees with no cycles at the same local depth.

The rooted subtree unfolding argument — that \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(t\) iterations if and only if their rooted subtrees of depth \(t\) are isomorphic as rooted labeled trees — is the precise theorem that explains why global structure is invisible to the WL test. The BFS tree unrolling from node \(v\) to depth \(t\) produces a tree \(T_v^{(t)}\) by expanding each node’s neighbors as children, breaking cycles by treating previously visited nodes as leaves. In \(K_{3,3}\): root any node \(v\), it has three neighbors (depth-1 children), each of those has two neighbors back in the other partition (depth-2 grandchildren, but these are the same nodes as the root and its siblings — however in the tree unrolling, cycles are broken, so they appear as new leaf nodes). In the prism graph: root any node \(v\), it has three neighbors, each of those has two further neighbors. At each depth, the rooted tree structure is identical between the two graphs — the same branching pattern, the same label distribution — because the difference between the two graphs (global cycle structure, bipartiteness) only becomes apparent when you follow long paths that return to previously visited nodes, which the tree unrolling eliminates by treating revisits as leaves. The inductive proof of the rooted subtree characterization formalizes this: \(c_v^{(t)} = c_u^{(t)}\) iff \(T_v^{(t)} \cong T_u^{(t)}\) as rooted labeled trees, by induction on \(t\) using the injectivity of the hash function at each step.

The fieldworker’s recognition that the WL color refinement and the message passing GNN are structurally identical — both propagate information one hop per round, combine self-information with an aggregated neighborhood summary — is the content of the upper bound theorem: \(G \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} H\) implies \(h_G^{\mathcal{A}} = h_H^{\mathcal{A}}\) for any MPNN \(\mathcal{A}\). The proof proceeds by the same induction that establishes the rooted subtree characterization. At layer \(k=0\): \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after 0 iterations means \(X[v] = X[u]\), so \(h_v^{(0)} = X[v] = X[u] = h_u^{(0)}\) — identical initial features give identical initial representations. At layer \(k\): if \(v \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} u\) after \(k+1\) iterations, then (1) \(h_v^{(k)} = h_u^{(k)}\) by inductive hypothesis, and (2) \(\{\!\{h_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\} = \{\!\{h_w^{(k)}: w\in\mathcal{N}(u)\}\!\}\) because WL-equivalent neighbors produce identical features. Therefore the aggregation \(f^{(k+1)}\) receives identical multiset inputs and produces identical outputs, and the update \(\phi^{(k+1)}\) receives identical inputs \((h_v^{(k)}, \bar{m}_v) = (h_u^{(k)}, \bar{m}_u)\) and produces identical outputs. The MPNN cannot see any difference the WL test cannot see — it is structurally incapable of doing so, regardless of the specific choice of \(\phi^{(k)}\) and \(f^{(k)}\), because it uses exactly the same neighborhood multiset information that the WL test uses, processed through functions that cannot manufacture distinctions from identical inputs.

The break moment — that sum aggregation is strictly more expressive than mean or max aggregation — is now the injectivity lemma: a function on multisets is injective if and only if it can be written as \(f(X) = \sum_{x\in X}\phi(x)\) for some function \(\phi\). The village counting example in the story — Village A with two neighbors at food stock level 3 versus Village B with four neighbors at food stock level 3, indistinguishable under mean aggregation but distinguishable under sum — is the formal counterexample to injectivity of mean aggregation: \(f_{\text{mean}}(\{\!\{3,3\}\!\}) = 3 = f_{\text{mean}}(\{\!\{3,3,3,3\}\!\})\), so \(f_{\text{mean}}\) is not injective on multisets. The formal proof identifies mean aggregation’s failure mode precisely: it maps all multisets with the same element-wise average to the same output, destroying information about neighborhood size. Max aggregation’s failure mode is different but equally precise: \(f_{\text{max}}(\{\!\{1,2\}\!\}) = 2 = f_{\text{max}}(\{\!\{2\}\!\})\), destroying information about all elements below the maximum. Sum aggregation through a universal function approximator \(\phi\) (an MLP of sufficient width, by the universal approximation theorem from COMP 472) is injective on multisets from a countable feature space: distinct multisets produce distinct sums because the MLP embeds elements into a space where linear independence of the embedded values ensures that different multiplicity patterns produce different total sums. This is the precise reason why GIN uses sum aggregation — not as an engineering choice but as the mathematically necessary condition for achieving WL-level expressiveness.

The GIN layer \((1+\epsilon)h_v^{(k-1)} + \sum_{u\in\mathcal{N}(v)}h_u^{(k-1)}\) passed through an MLP — the architecture that achieves exactly WL expressiveness — is the construction that simultaneously satisfies injectivity on the combined (self, neighborhood) pair and universal approximation of any WL-computable function. The scalar \((1+\epsilon)\) with \(\epsilon \neq -1\) ensures the self-feature \(h_v^{(k-1)}\) contributes with a different coefficient than the neighborhood features (which each have coefficient 1 in the sum): without this distinction, a node with one neighbor whose feature equals the node’s own feature could be confused with a node with two neighbors whose features sum to the same value. The separation of self-coefficient \((1+\epsilon)\) from neighbor-coefficient \(1\) makes the combined aggregation injective on the full pair \((h_v^{(k-1)}, \{\!\{h_u^{(k-1)}: u\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\})\), not just on the neighborhood multiset alone. The MLP of sufficient width is the universal function approximator that maps any injectively-encoded input to any target output — its role is not to provide injectivity (which the sum already achieves) but to provide expressiveness: any function of the WL coloring that is continuous and bounded can be approximated arbitrarily well by the MLP applied to the sum-aggregated representation. The lower bound proof’s inductive structure mirrors the upper bound proof exactly, but in the opposite direction: where the upper bound shows that WL-indistinguishable nodes produce identical GNN features, the lower bound shows that WL-distinguishable nodes produce different GIN features, using the injectivity of sum aggregation at each step to propagate distinctions forward through the layers.

The GIN graph-level readout \(h_G^{\text{GIN}} = \text{CONCAT}(\sum_{v\in V}h_v^{(k)}: k=0,1,\ldots,K)\) — concatenating sum readouts from every layer — is the multi-scale WL histogram that captures structural information at every hop depth simultaneously. The sum readout \(\sum_{v\in V}h_v^{(k)}\) at layer \(k\) is the GNN analogue of the WL color histogram at iteration \(k\): it aggregates all node representations at depth \(k\) into a single graph-level vector, encoding the multiset of depth-\(k\) rooted subtree types present in the graph. Concatenating across all \(k = 0,\ldots,K\) preserves the structural information at every resolution — the depth-0 histogram encodes node feature distributions, the depth-1 histogram encodes one-hop neighborhood distributions, and so on. Two graphs that are WL-indistinguishable at every depth will produce identical readouts at every layer and therefore identical concatenated representations. Two graphs that WL distinguishes at some depth \(k^*\) will produce different readouts at layer \(k^*\) and therefore different concatenated representations. The readout is the complete encoding of the WL certificate — the sequence of color histograms that the WL test produces — in the continuous feature space of the GNN.

The census hierarchy beyond individual villages — pairs, triples, \(k\)-tuples — is the \(k\)-WL hierarchy, with the \(k\)-IGN achieving \(k\)-WL expressiveness at \(O(n^k)\) computational cost and \(B_{2k}\) equivariant layer parameters. The 2-WL test colors ordered pairs \((v,u)\) of villages, updating each pair’s color based on the multisets of colors of pairs sharing a village. This allows the test to detect edge-level structure invisible to node-level coloring: a pair \((v,u)\) with an edge between \(v\) and \(u\) receives a different initial color than a pair without an edge, and the pair-level refinement propagates this edge information through the graph. The 3-WL test colors triples, encoding triangle-level structure. At the architectural level, the \(k\)-IGN uses tensor features \(T \in (\mathbb{R}^n)^{\otimes k}\) indexed by \(k\)-tuples of nodes, with equivariant linear layers classified by Schur’s lemma as having \(B_{2k}\) independent parameters — the Bell number counting set partitions of \(\{1,\ldots,2k\}\), each partition corresponding to an independent way of contracting and broadcasting \(k\)-th order tensors consistently with simultaneous \(S_n\)-permutation of all indices. For \(k=1\): \(B_2 = 2\) parameters giving \(L = \alpha I + \beta\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top\), the GNN aggregation. For \(k=2\): \(B_4 = 15\) parameters giving the full space of equivariant operations on pairwise node features. The Bell number grows super-exponentially in \(k\), reflecting the combinatorial explosion of independent equivariant operations at higher tensor orders.

The Cai-Fürer-Immerman theorem — that for every \(k\), there exist non-isomorphic graphs indistinguishable by \(k\)-WL — means the WL hierarchy is infinite and no finite level achieves complete graph isomorphism. The CFI construction produces for every \(k\) a pair of graphs that are locally identical up to depth \(k\) but globally different. This is the graph-theoretic counterpart of the computational complexity results from COMP 314: just as there is no known polynomial-time algorithm for graph isomorphism, there is no finite-level WL test that solves it. The practical consequence for GNN design: no matter how high an order \(k\)-IGN you use, there will always be graphs it cannot distinguish, and the computational cost \(O(n^k)\) grows rapidly with \(k\). The frontier of GNN expressiveness research — subgraph GNNs, random feature GNNs, distance-encoding GNNs — explores ways to break the WL ceiling without incurring the full \(O(n^k)\) cost of higher-order IGNs, injecting global structural information (triangle counts, cycle lengths, random walk return probabilities) as additional node features that the standard 1-WL aggregation can then process.

The invisible graph properties enumerated in the story — triangle counts, cycle lengths, bipartiteness, planarity, graph diameter — are now formally characterized as properties not expressible as functions of rooted subtree multisets, and therefore not computable by any message-passing GNN. Triangle counts require detecting three-cycles: a triangle \(v\)-\(u\)-\(w\)-\(v\) is a cycle of length 3, which in the BFS tree unrolling from \(v\) appears as two depth-1 children \(u\) and \(w\) that are connected to each other in the original graph — but the tree unrolling breaks this back-edge, treating \(w\) as a leaf at depth 2 from \(u\) rather than the same node as \(w\) at depth 1 from \(v\). The triangle’s closing edge is invisible. The random walk return probability \([\mathbf{P}^3]_{vv} = \sum_{u,w\in\mathcal{N}(v)}A_{uw}/(d_v^2 d_u)\) detects triangles precisely because it counts three-step walks returning to \(v\), which requires traversing the closing edge of a triangle — a piece of global information precomputed and injected as a node feature, making it available to the subsequent message passing layers. This is the principled resolution: properties invisible to 1-WL can be precomputed and injected as additional structural features, moving the computational burden from the GNN architecture to the feature engineering step, and allowing the 1-WL-level message passing to then operate on structurally enriched node representations.

The connection to MCSC 201 is fully explicit. The multiset \(\{\!\{c_u^{(t-1)}: u\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\}\) — the fundamental object of WL refinement — is the Discrete Structures concept of a multiset: an unordered collection allowing repeated elements, with equality defined by both element identity and multiplicity. The injectivity lemma’s proof that \(f(X) = \sum_{x\in X}\phi(x)\) is the unique injective multiset function uses the generating function argument from combinatorics: mapping each element \(x\) to \(\phi(x)\) and summing over the multiset produces a vector that encodes both the elements and their multiplicities as a polynomial with coefficient vector, and distinct multisets produce distinct polynomials under linearly independent \(\phi\)-values. The convergence bound \(T^* \leq n\) is the pigeonhole principle: with \(n\) nodes and a color space growing monotonically through refinement, the partition can refine at most \(n-1\) times before every node has a unique color or no further splitting occurs. The connection to COMP 323 is equally direct. Graph isomorphism from Graph Theory is the decision problem that the WL test approximates. The \(K_{3,3}\) and prism graph are classical examples from Graph Theory — \(K_{3,3}\) is the complete bipartite graph on six nodes, one of the two Kuratowski graphs certifying non-planarity, while the prism graph is the Cartesian product \(K_3 \square K_2\). Their WL-indistinguishability despite structural differences (bipartiteness, triangle count, planarity) is a concrete instance of the WL test’s limitations, rooted in the combinatorial properties of 3-regular graphs. The connection to COMP 472 is the universal approximation theorem: the MLP in GIN achieves injectivity on WL-computable functions not by architecture but by width — a sufficiently wide MLP can approximate any continuous function on a compact set, so any injectively encoded input can be mapped to any target output, giving GIN the full function approximation capacity within the WL complexity class.

The Government of Nepal census — its bureau chief, fieldworker, color refinement rounds, four-cycle example, \(K_{3,3}\) versus prism failure, rooted subtree blindness, message passing parallel, and \(k\)-tuple hierarchy — is now completely dissolved into its mathematical form. The isomorphism question is \(G \cong H\). The color refinement is \(c_v^{(t)} = \text{hash}(c_v^{(t-1)}, \{\!\{c_u^{(t-1)}: u\in\mathcal{N}(v)\}\!\})\). WL-equivalence is \(G \stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim} H\). The rooted subtree is \(T_v^{(t)}\). The upper bound is \(G\stackrel{\text{WL}}{\sim}H \Rightarrow h_G^{\mathcal{A}} = h_H^{\mathcal{A}}\). The injectivity lemma is \(f\) injective on multisets iff \(f(X) = \sum_{x\in X}\phi(x)\). GIN achieves the lower bound. The \(k\)-WL hierarchy climbs through \(O(n^k)\) complexity and \(B_{2k}\) parameters. The CFI theorem ensures the hierarchy never terminates.
