    Chapter 3 - Differential Geometry: The Map

    Introduction

    Chapter 1 gave you the language of mass — how to assign sizes to sets, how to integrate functions, how to compare distributions, how to move one distribution through a transformation. Chapter 2 gave you the language of optimization — how to measure size with norms, how to exploit convexity, how to dualize problems, how to certify optimality with KKT conditions. Both chapters operated in flat space — in \(\mathbb{R}^n\), in Hilbert spaces, in the familiar Euclidean geometry where straight lines are straight and distances are computed with the Pythagorean theorem.

    Chapter 3 breaks that flatness assumption completely.

    The world is not flat. Data does not live on a flat grid. The space of all rotation matrices is not Euclidean — it is a curved surface called \(SO(3)\). The space of all probability distributions is not Euclidean — it is a curved infinite-dimensional manifold equipped with the Fisher information metric. The space of protein backbone configurations is not Euclidean — it lives on a product of spheres and tori, constrained by bond lengths and angles. The space of all images is not Euclidean — the perceptually meaningful distances between images are not the pixel-wise \(L_2\) distances you would compute in flat space. In every domain where the roadmap’s generative models operate — molecules, proteins, images, graphs — the data lives on a curved surface, and the flat-space tools of Chapters 1 and 2 are insufficient to describe its geometry correctly.

    Differential Geometry is the mathematical framework that extends the tools of calculus, linear algebra, and optimization from flat Euclidean space to curved surfaces — and to curved spaces of any dimension, including infinite-dimensional ones. The roadmap gives this chapter a name that cuts to its function: it is the Map. Chapter 1 built the engine that moves mass. Chapter 2 built the engine that optimizes. Chapter 3 builds the map of the curved terrain on which both engines operate.

    The chapter’s core concept, stated plainly in the roadmap, is this: the world is not flat, and data lives on curved surfaces. Every concept from Chapters 1 and 2 — distance, gradient, integral, push-forward, optimization — needs to be re-examined and extended to the curved setting. Differential geometry provides the precise tools for doing so, and the four topics of this chapter build those tools in strict logical order.

    The first topic is Manifolds — the objects that replace flat Euclidean space as the domain of the roadmap’s computations. A manifold is a space that looks locally flat — like a small patch of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) — but curves globally. The surface of the Earth is the canonical example: locally, a small patch of Kathmandu looks flat enough to navigate with a grid map, but globally the Earth curves into a sphere, and navigating from Kathmandu to London requires accounting for that curvature. The formal language of manifolds — charts, atlases, and smooth maps — is the precise vocabulary for describing spaces that are locally Euclidean but globally curved. Charts are the local flat maps. An atlas is the complete collection of local maps covering the entire curved space. Smooth maps are the functions between manifolds that respect the smooth structure. This vocabulary lets you do calculus on curved spaces by reducing local computations to the familiar flat-space calculus you know from MATH 201.

    The second topic is Tangent Bundles — the objects that replace vectors when working on curved spaces. On a flat space, a vector at a point is simply a direction and a magnitude in the ambient Euclidean space. On a curved manifold, the notion of a vector must be localized: at each point \(x\) on the manifold, there is a tangent space \(T_xM\) — the flat space that best approximates the manifold near \(x\), like the tangent plane to a sphere at a given point. A tangent vector \(v \in T_xM\) is a direction of motion along the manifold at \(x\) — a velocity vector, encoding how a curve through \(x\) on the manifold is moving at that point. The tangent bundle \(TM\) is the collection of all tangent spaces over all points of the manifold — the full assembly of all possible velocity vectors at all possible positions. The tangent bundle is the curved-space replacement for the flat vector field of classical calculus, and it is the arena in which neural ODEs, flow matching, and diffusion models all operate.

    The third topic is Riemannian Metrics — the objects that give a manifold a notion of distance and angle. A smooth manifold by itself has no notion of distance — it is a purely topological object, telling you which points are near each other but not how far apart they are. A Riemannian metric \(g\) equips each tangent space \(T_xM\) with an inner product \(g_x(\cdot,\cdot)\) — a smoothly varying family of inner products across all points of the manifold. Once a Riemannian metric is in place, you can measure the length of curves on the manifold, define the angle between two tangent vectors, integrate functions over the manifold, and compute gradients of functions on the manifold. The Riemannian metric is the curved-space replacement for the flat Euclidean inner product, and it is what converts the topological manifold into a geometric one — a space with both shape and scale.

    The fourth topic is Geodesics — the curved-space replacement for straight lines. On a flat Euclidean space, the shortest path between two points is a straight line. On a curved manifold, the shortest path is a geodesic — a curve that generalizes the straight line to the curved setting. Geodesics are the solutions to the geodesic equation, a second-order ODE derived from the Riemannian metric, and they are the paths that parallel-transport their own tangent vectors — paths that curve only as much as the manifold forces them to, with no unnecessary bending. On the sphere, geodesics are great circles — the paths that airline routes approximate. On the space of probability distributions equipped with the Wasserstein metric, geodesics are the displacement interpolations of Phase 1 — the paths along which mass flows optimally from one distribution to another. The geodesic is the concept that connects the Riemannian geometry of this chapter to the optimal transport geometry of Phase 1, making the bridge between Chapter 3 and Phase 1 explicit and precise.

    Together, these four topics build the complete language of curved-space mathematics. By the end of Chapter 3, you will have the vocabulary to describe exactly what it means for data to live on a manifold, to define distances and gradients on that manifold, to find the shortest paths connecting points on that manifold, and to understand why the generative models of Phase 3 that operate on manifolds — Riemannian flow matching, SE(3)-equivariant diffusion, protein structure generation — require the full apparatus of differential geometry rather than the flat-space tools of Chapters 1 and 2.
