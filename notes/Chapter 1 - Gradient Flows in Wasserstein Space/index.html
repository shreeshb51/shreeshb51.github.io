Chapter 1 - Gradient Flows in Wasserstein Space

Introduction

Phase 3 opens not with an algorithm or an architecture but with a reinterpretation. Everything you have built so far — the Wasserstein distance of Phase 1, the equivariant networks of Phase 2, the spectral geometry of manifolds — is about to be seen from a new angle. That angle is dynamics. Not the static question of how far apart two distributions are, or what architecture respects a symmetry group, but the dynamic question of how a probability distribution evolves through time, and what geometric structure governs that evolution.

The reinterpretation is this: training a generative model is not optimization in the parameter space of a neural network. It is gradient descent in the space of probability distributions, where the notion of gradient and the notion of distance are both defined by the Wasserstein geometry of Phase 1. The neural network’s parameters are merely the means by which you parameterize a point in distribution space. The training dynamics — the sequence of distributions visited as you adjust the network’s weights — is a curve in Wasserstein space. The loss function — the divergence between the model distribution and the data distribution — is a functional defined on Wasserstein space. Its gradient, in the Wasserstein metric, is a vector field on the data domain. Following that gradient is gradient flow in Wasserstein space, and the partial differential equation governing the resulting distributional evolution is the Fokker-Planck equation.

This geometric picture is not a philosophical reframing of something that could just as well be described algorithmically. It is the mathematical framework that explains why diffusion models work, why score matching is a principled training objective, why certain noise schedules are better than others, and why the straight paths of rectified flows are more efficient than the curved paths of standard diffusion. Without this framework, modern generative modeling is a collection of empirical recipes. With it, it is a coherent geometric theory.

The chapter develops this framework across two topics corresponding to the two bullet points of the roadmap’s Phase 3, Chapter 1 syllabus.

The first topic is The JKO Scheme: Viewing the Heat Equation as Gradient Descent of Entropy. The Jordan-Kinderlehrer-Otto scheme, established in a landmark 1998 paper, is the variational characterization of gradient flow in Wasserstein space. Its central claim is both precise and surprising: the heat equation — the simplest diffusion process, describing how a concentration of heat spreads through a medium over time — is the gradient flow of the entropy functional \(H(\rho) = \int\rho\log\rho\,dx\) in the Wasserstein-2 metric space of probability distributions. This means that the heat equation is not merely a PDE that describes diffusion. It is a steepest descent curve in the geometric space \((\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d), W_2)\), following the negative gradient of entropy exactly as gradient descent on a loss function follows the negative gradient in parameter space. The JKO scheme makes this precise through a time-discretization: each step of the discrete gradient flow solves a proximal optimization problem, minimizing the entropy functional plus a Wasserstein distance penalty from the current distribution. As the time step goes to zero, the discrete scheme converges to the continuous heat equation. This variational principle extends far beyond the heat equation: any functional of a probability distribution — free energy, KL divergence, interaction energy — defines a gradient flow in Wasserstein space, and the corresponding PDE is the Fokker-Planck equation with a drift term determined by the functional’s first variation. The JKO scheme is the Rosetta Stone that translates between PDEs describing distributional evolution and variational principles in Wasserstein space.

The second topic is The Fokker-Planck Equation: Time Evolution of Probability Densities. The Fokker-Planck equation is the PDE that governs how a probability density \(\rho_t(x)\) evolves under a stochastic differential equation \(dX_t = b(X_t,t)dt + \sigma dW_t\), where \(b\) is a deterministic drift, \(\sigma\) is a noise coefficient, and \(W_t\) is a standard Brownian motion. The equation is \(\partial_t\rho_t = -\nabla\cdot(\rho_t b) + \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\Delta\rho_t\) — a linear PDE in \(\rho_t\) combining an advection term \(-\nabla\cdot(\rho_t b)\) (probability mass being carried by the drift \(b\)) and a diffusion term \(\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\Delta\rho_t\) (probability mass spreading due to the Brownian noise). The Fokker-Planck equation is the bridge between the stochastic differential equation perspective — particles following noisy trajectories — and the distributional perspective — probability mass flowing through space. It is also the bridge between Phase 1’s Optimal Transport and Phase 3’s generative modeling: the continuity equation \(\partial_t\rho_t + \nabla\cdot(\rho_t v_t) = 0\), which is the Fokker-Planck equation with \(\sigma = 0\) and drift \(v_t\) replacing \(b\), is precisely the equation that Brenier’s theorem uses to characterize the geodesics of Wasserstein space — the displacement interpolations of Phase 1. The velocity field \(v_t\) that drives the continuity equation is the object that Neural ODEs and flow matching learn to parameterize. Understanding the Fokker-Planck equation is the prerequisite for understanding every generative model in Chapter 2: diffusion models are specific Fokker-Planck equations with Ornstein-Uhlenbeck drift and fixed noise schedule; flow matching models are continuity equations with learned velocity fields; score-based models parameterize the drift term of the Fokker-Planck equation through the score function.

The relationship between these two topics is the relationship between the variational and the differential: the JKO scheme is the variational principle, the Fokker-Planck equation is the differential equation it generates. They are two descriptions of the same mathematical object — the gradient flow of a functional in Wasserstein space — seen from two different perspectives. The JKO scheme asks: what optimization problem does each infinitesimal step of the evolution solve? The Fokker-Planck equation asks: what PDE governs the continuous-time evolution of the density? Both questions have the same answer, expressed in different mathematical languages. Mastering both descriptions — and the precise relationship between them — is what gives you the full geometric picture of how probability distributions flow through Wasserstein space.

The connections to your curriculum are at their deepest here, because gradient flows in Wasserstein space sit at the intersection of every mathematical thread your courses have developed.

Your MATH 217 course on ODEs and PDEs is the primary scaffold. The Fokker-Planck equation is a second-order linear parabolic PDE — the same structural class as the heat equation \(\partial_t u = \Delta u\), which you have encountered in MATH 217 as the canonical example of a parabolic PDE. The method of characteristics, the maximum principle, and the Fourier solution of the heat equation from MATH 217 all apply to the Fokker-Planck equation with appropriate modifications for the drift term. The stochastic differential equation \(dX_t = b(X_t,t)dt + \sigma dW_t\) is the stochastic generalization of the ODE \(\dot x = b(x,t)\) from MATH 217 — the same existence and uniqueness theory applies, with the Itô calculus replacing the classical ODE theory. The geodesic equation from Phase 0’s differential geometry — the ODE that geodesics satisfy on a Riemannian manifold — appears here again as the characteristic equation of the continuity equation in the Wasserstein geodesic problem.

Your MATH 325 course on Analysis II is the secondary scaffold. The functional \(H(\rho) = \int\rho\log\rho\,dx\) is a functional on the space of probability distributions — an element of the dual space to the space of continuous functions, in the sense of functional analysis. Its first variation \(\delta H/\delta\rho = \log\rho + 1\) is the functional derivative, computed exactly as the Gâteaux derivative from Analysis II. The Wasserstein gradient of \(H\) — the object that drives the gradient flow — is the Riemannian gradient of \(H\) viewed as a function on the infinite-dimensional Riemannian manifold \((\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d), W_2)\), connecting the finite-dimensional Riemannian geometry of Phase 0 to the infinite-dimensional functional analysis of Analysis II.

Your MATH 304 course on Computational Operations Research provides the proximal optimization perspective. The JKO scheme’s each-step optimization — minimize \(F(\rho) + \frac{1}{2\tau}W_2^2(\rho, \rho^k)\) over distributions \(\rho\) — is a proximal point algorithm in the Wasserstein metric: exactly the proximal gradient descent algorithm from optimization theory, applied to the infinite-dimensional space of distributions with the Wasserstein metric replacing the Euclidean metric. The convergence theory of proximal algorithms from MATH 304 — the conditions under which proximal gradient descent converges to a minimizer, the role of strong convexity in ensuring exponential convergence rates — applies to JKO scheme convergence in Wasserstein space, with geodesic convexity of the functional replacing Euclidean convexity.

Your MATH 208 course on Statistics and Probability provides the probabilistic foundation. The stochastic differential equation \(dX_t = b(X_t,t)dt + \sigma dW_t\) requires Brownian motion \(W_t\) — the continuous-time stochastic process from MATH 208’s probability theory, with independent increments and Gaussian distributions. The Fokker-Planck equation is the forward equation for the density of \(X_t\), while the backward Kolmogorov equation is the equation for expected values of functions of \(X_t\) — both arising from the same SDE through Itô’s formula, the stochastic chain rule that is the probabilistic counterpart of the chain rule from MATH 201.

By the end of Chapter 1, the heat equation will no longer appear to be a mysterious PDE that describes the spreading of heat. It will be the steepest descent curve of entropy in the Wasserstein metric — the probability distribution that evolves by following the negative Wasserstein gradient of \(\int\rho\log\rho\,dx\), exactly as a ball rolling down a hill follows the negative Euclidean gradient of potential energy. The Fokker-Planck equation will no longer appear to be a technical result from stochastic processes. It will be the master equation of probability flow — the equation that every generative model in Chapter 2 is a special case of, with different choices of drift \(b\) and noise coefficient \(\sigma\) corresponding to different generative model families. The JKO scheme will no longer appear to be an abstract variational principle. It will be the proximal point algorithm in distribution space — the same algorithm from MATH 304’s optimization course, now operating on the infinite-dimensional Riemannian manifold of probability distributions with the Wasserstein metric as its geometry. The car is about to start moving.
